# Spain’s economy and cost of living: the country is growing faster than many neighbours, but ordinary life still feels expensive

### Spain has created jobs, raised wages and expanded its population while outperforming much of Europe. Yet housing, food, energy and essential services consume a growing share of many household budgets. The economy is improving faster than the feeling of prosperity.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

Spain’s economy is not in crisis.

It is growing, creating employment and raising nominal incomes. GDP increased **2.7% year on year in the first quarter of 2026**, while employment was more than half a million higher than one year earlier. The country has performed better than many large European economies since the pandemic. 

But the positive national picture conceals a more difficult household reality:

> **Spain is creating more jobs and income, but too much of the growth is absorbed by housing costs, accumulated inflation, low productivity and an economic structure dominated by relatively low-wage services.**

Five things are true simultaneously:

1. Spain’s economic growth is real.
2. Employment has improved substantially.
3. Wages have recently risen faster than consumer prices for many workers.
4. Prices remain permanently much higher than before the inflation shock.
5. Housing costs are destroying much of the improvement for renters, young adults and new buyers.

The central conflict is therefore not between “a booming economy” and “a collapsing economy.”

It is between **strong aggregate expansion and weak conversion of that expansion into secure, affordable living standards**.

---

# 1. The headline economy is performing well

Spain’s GDP grew by **0.6% during the first quarter of 2026** and by **2.7% compared with the same quarter of 2025**. Domestic demand contributed 3.5 percentage points to annual growth, while external demand subtracted 0.8 points. 

That composition matters.

Growth is currently being supported by:

- employment;
- household consumption;
- population growth;
- public expenditure;
- tourism and other service exports;
- investment associated with European funds;
- recovery in some industrial activity.

This is not the same model as the pre-2008 construction and credit boom. Household indebtedness and bank exposure are lower, mortgage lending is more controlled and Spain’s external position is stronger.

The economy has also become more resilient to shocks. Since 2020 it has absorbed:

- the pandemic;
- the energy crisis;
- inflation;
- rapid interest-rate increases;
- the war in Ukraine;
- weak growth in Germany and other European trading partners.

The fact that Spain continued to expand through these shocks is a significant achievement.

But growth alone does not reveal whether the average resident is better off. Total GDP rises when:

- more people live in the country;
- more people work;
- prices rise;
- the government spends more;
- tourism grows.

Living standards depend more heavily on **real income per person, productivity, public-service quality and essential costs**.

---

# 2. Employment is Spain’s clearest success

Spain had approximately **22.29 million employed people in the first quarter of 2026**, around 528,000 more than one year earlier. Seasonal factors caused employment to fall during the quarter, but seasonally adjusted employment continued to increase. 

The unemployment rate was **10.83%** in the first quarter. That remains high by European standards, but it is dramatically below the levels Spain experienced after the financial crisis. 

The improvement reflects several forces:

- a long economic expansion;
- immigration and population growth;
- recovery of tourism and hospitality;
- stronger public-sector and care employment;
- labour-market reform;
- reduced temporary contracting;
- European investment;
- growth in professional and digital services.

The government is correct to say that Spain has reached historically high employment levels.

But the opposition is also correct that the unemployment rate remains structurally excessive.

Spain’s labour market continues to combine:

- record numbers working;
- one of the highest unemployment rates in the EU;
- strong seasonal variation;
- underemployment;
- involuntary part-time work;
- substantial regional differences;
- unusually high youth unemployment.

This apparent contradiction exists because both employment and the working-age population have increased.

---

# 3. Labour reform improved contract stability, but not every job became secure

The 2021–2022 labour reform sharply reduced the use of conventional temporary contracts.

Many employees who would previously have been hired through repeated short-term contracts now hold permanent or permanent-discontinuous contracts.

That is a meaningful improvement because it can provide:

- greater legal protection;
- easier access to credit and rental housing;
- higher dismissal costs;
- stronger bargaining power;
- less administrative cycling between contracts.

But the headline decline in temporary employment does not mean precariousness disappeared.

Permanent-discontinuous contracts can involve long periods without work. Permanent workers can still receive low pay, work few hours or remain vulnerable to dismissal. Self-employed workers can depend almost entirely on one client. Platform and subcontracted work can shift risk onto workers without classifying them as temporary.

The correct conclusion is:

**Spain reduced contractual temporariness more successfully than it reduced economic insecurity.**

A serious employment dashboard should therefore report:

- annual hours worked;
- months actually employed;
- involuntary part-time work;
- wage progression;
- dismissals;
- periods without activity;
- household income stability.

---

# 4. Wages are rising—but the average conceals the typical worker

The average gross annual salary reached **€29,540 in 2024**, up 5.3% from 2023. The average was approximately €32,058 for men and €26,905 for women. 

A different INE measure found an average monthly gross wage of **€2,385.60** in 2024.

But the median—the wage separating the lower-paid half from the higher-paid half—was only **€2,001.40 gross per month**. 

That difference is important.

The average is pulled upward by high earners. A worker hearing that Spain’s average salary is close to €30,000 may earn significantly less without being unusually poor.

Wages also differ dramatically by sector.

Workers in energy, finance, information technology and senior professional occupations earn far more than workers in:

- hospitality;
- retail;
- agriculture;
- domestic work;
- cleaning;
- care;
- basic administrative services.

In the latest detailed sectoral comparison, accommodation and food-service workers were among the lowest paid, despite tourism’s importance to the Spanish economy. 

Spain’s wage problem is therefore closely connected to its production model.

A country dominated by labour-intensive, price-sensitive services will struggle to produce northern-European wages even when employment grows rapidly.

---

# 5. The minimum wage has risen substantially

The minimum interprofessional wage for 2026 is **€1,221 gross per month in 14 payments**, equivalent to €17,094 per year. It increased by 3.1% from 2025. 

The increase since 2018 has been one of the government’s most consequential economic policies.

It has particularly benefited:

- low-paid workers;
- women;
- young adults;
- agricultural workers;
- cleaners;
- care workers;
- hospitality and retail employees.

The increase has not produced the employment collapse predicted by some opponents.

But its effect should not be exaggerated.

A person receiving €1,221 in fourteen payments may still struggle to rent alone in Madrid, Barcelona, Málaga, Palma or many coastal areas. The minimum wage sets a national floor, while housing costs vary radically by location.

It also creates pressure for small employers in low-margin sectors, particularly where productivity is weak. The sustainable solution cannot be to freeze low wages indefinitely. It must be to help firms produce enough value to pay higher wages.

The minimum wage can distribute more income toward low-paid employees. It cannot by itself transform a low-productivity business model.

---

# 6. Inflation slowed, but prices did not go back down

Annual consumer-price inflation was **3.2% in June 2026**, according to the advance estimate. Underlying inflation was approximately 2.9%. Electricity and gas contributed to the continued increase. 

The distinction between inflation and prices is the source of much public confusion.

When inflation falls from 10% to 3%, prices are still rising—only more slowly.

A product that increased from €100 to €110 during one year of 10% inflation and then increased another 3% costs €113.30. Lower inflation does not return it to €100.

Spain experienced a large cumulative price shock after 2021. Even where wages are now catching up, households remember the previous price level.

This explains why official statements that “inflation has moderated” can sound disconnected from daily experience.

The relevant question is not whether inflation has fallen from its peak.

It is:

> **Have household incomes risen enough to compensate for the entire accumulated increase in essential prices?**

For some workers, especially those benefiting from the minimum wage, negotiated pay rises or job changes, the answer is increasingly yes.

For fixed-income households, unstable workers and renters facing new contracts, the answer is often no.

---

# 7. The cost-of-living crisis is not one uniform crisis

Households experience inflation differently because they buy different things.

A wealthy mortgage-free homeowner may spend a relatively small share of income on:

- housing;
- food;
- electricity;
- transport.

A low-income renter may spend most of their income on those categories.

The official CPI represents an average consumption basket. It does not represent every household.

For low-income households, inflation can feel higher because essential items cannot easily be postponed.

You can delay:

- buying furniture;
- replacing a laptop;
- taking a holiday.

You cannot indefinitely delay:

- rent;
- groceries;
- electricity;
- commuting;
- medicine;
- childcare.

This means identical headline inflation can produce radically unequal hardship.

---

# 8. Housing has become the main cost-of-living problem

Housing, water, electricity, gas and other fuels were the household expenditure category with the largest average increase in 2025: **5.8%**. Average household expenditure rose to **€35,101**, while average expenditure per person reached €14,066. 

House prices increased **12.9% year on year in the first quarter of 2026**, far faster than general inflation and average wages. Existing-home prices rose 13.5%, and new-home prices rose 9.1%. 

This is why macroeconomic improvement feels weak to younger households.

A worker may receive a 4% pay increase but face:

- a 10% rent increase;
- a house price rising 13%;
- a deposit requirement moving further away;
- higher utility costs;
- rising childcare or transport expenses.

For existing owners with fixed-rate mortgages or no mortgage, housing inflation can increase wealth.

For renters and buyers, the same development reduces living standards.

Spain’s economy therefore creates a major division between:

- **asset owners**, who benefit from rising property values;
- **non-owners**, who pay higher rents and need larger deposits.

This is not merely a housing-market problem. It is becoming the main engine of intergenerational inequality.

---

# 9. A national salary buys radically different lives

The minimum wage and most tax rules are national.

Housing and transport costs are local.

A salary of €28,000 may support a reasonable standard of living in a smaller inland city but leave little disposable income in central Madrid, Barcelona, Málaga or Palma.

Regional income differences are partly offset by regional price differences, but not fully.

High-growth cities offer:

- more jobs;
- higher salaries;
- better professional networks;
- stronger universities;
- more international opportunities.

They also have:

- higher rents;
- longer commutes;
- childcare constraints;
- competition for housing;
- tourism pressure;
- expensive social life.

A worker may improve their nominal salary by moving to Madrid while reducing their real disposable income.

Spain lacks a sufficiently developed official system of regional cost-of-living indices. This makes wage, poverty and public-pay comparisons less informative than they appear.

---

# 10. Food remains a powerful psychological measure of economic well-being

Consumers encounter food prices weekly or daily. Housing may dominate the budget, but supermarket prices dominate perception.

The inflation shock affected:

- olive oil;
- dairy products;
- eggs;
- meat;
- cereals;
- fresh produce;
- processed foods.

Some prices later stabilised or declined, but not necessarily to their previous levels.

Food inflation was driven by multiple forces:

- energy and fertiliser costs;
- drought and climate events;
- international commodity prices;
- transport;
- wages;
- packaging;
- retailer and producer margins;
- supply-chain disruption.

Political debate often tries to identify one guilty actor:

- supermarkets;
- farmers;
- intermediaries;
- taxes;
- Ukraine;
- climate policy.

The reality differs by product. Some markets are highly concentrated; others involve fragmented producers with limited pricing power.

Investigating food affordability requires tracing the full chain from farm-gate price to final retail margin rather than assuming that every price increase reflects corporate profiteering or every retailer margin reflects normal cost pass-through.

---

# 11. Energy remains volatile even after the crisis passed

Spain’s electricity market has some structural advantages:

- significant renewable generation;
- nuclear capacity;
- gas import diversification;
- interconnection with North Africa;
- growing solar production.

It also remains exposed to:

- gas prices;
- rainfall and hydroelectric production;
- network charges;
- taxes;
- carbon prices;
- international shocks;
- low interconnection with the rest of Europe.

Households often find electricity bills difficult to understand because the final amount includes:

- wholesale energy;
- regulated charges;
- network costs;
- taxes;
- contract conditions;
- time-of-use pricing.

Government interventions during the energy crisis reduced household harm. But temporary tax cuts and subsidies also created fiscal costs and sometimes benefited households that were not vulnerable.

Long-term affordability requires:

- efficient homes;
- insulation;
- heat pumps where suitable;
- transparent tariffs;
- stronger electricity grids;
- storage;
- targeted social support.

Subsidising energy consumption indefinitely is less sustainable than reducing the energy needed to maintain a decent home.

---

# 12. Spain’s households are spending more—but not necessarily consuming much more

Average household spending increased by 3.1% in 2025, reaching €35,101. Spending per person increased by 3.2%. 

An increase in spending can mean:

- households bought more;
- households bought better products;
- prices increased;
- household composition changed.

It does not automatically mean living standards improved.

The strongest expenditure increase occurred in housing and utilities, which households often cannot avoid. Restaurant and accommodation spending decreased, suggesting at least some shift away from discretionary consumption. 

A household that spends €2,000 more because rent, electricity and food increased is not necessarily better off.

This is why real, inflation-adjusted consumption and disposable income are more useful than nominal expenditure alone.

---

# 13. Average household income improved, but poverty remains high

Average income per person reached **€15,620 in 2024**, an increase of 5.5% from the previous year. 

Yet **25.7% of the population remained at risk of poverty or social exclusion in 2025**. Severe material and social deprivation affected 8.1%. 

These figures are not contradictory.

The poverty measure is relative and multidimensional. It captures people who experience one or more of:

- income below the poverty threshold;
- severe material deprivation;
- very low work intensity.

Average incomes can rise while a large minority remains vulnerable.

Spain’s persistent poverty reflects:

- high unemployment;
- low-paid employment;
- child poverty;
- single-parent household risk;
- housing costs;
- unequal regional development;
- weak access to some benefits;
- labour-market instability.

The welfare state substantially reduces poverty among pensioners but has historically been less effective at protecting children and working-age families.

---

# 14. Having a job no longer guarantees freedom from financial stress

Employment is the main protection against poverty, but not an absolute one.

Working poverty arises when a household combines:

- low hourly pay;
- insufficient hours;
- dependent children;
- one earner;
- expensive rent;
- unstable employment;
- limited benefits.

A full-time employee on a moderate wage may appear secure in national statistics while being unable to:

- rent independently;
- save for a deposit;
- absorb an unexpected expense;
- pay for childcare;
- support children without family help.

Spain’s economic debate focuses heavily on whether jobs exist.

The next question must be whether those jobs support an independent household.

---

# 15. Young adults face a distinct economic system

Older and younger Spaniards experience the same economy differently.

Many older households possess:

- a paid-off or low-cost home;
- inflation-linked pensions;
- financial savings;
- stable local networks;
- lower childcare costs.

Many younger adults face:

- high rent;
- insecure early careers;
- late household formation;
- difficulty saving;
- expensive urban labour markets;
- delayed parenthood;
- little accumulated wealth.

Pensions were revalued by **2.7% in 2026**, in line with the inflation-protection system. 

Protecting pensioners’ purchasing power is socially defensible. But the contrast with younger workers is politically important.

Workers do not receive automatic CPI-linked wage increases. Their rent may rise faster than inflation, and they must finance the taxes and contributions supporting the pension system.

Spain risks developing a welfare state highly effective at protecting established older households while leaving younger adults dependent on family wealth.

---

# 16. The tax burden is not exceptionally low for workers

Spain’s “tax wedge”—the difference between an employer’s total labour cost and the employee’s take-home pay—was **40.6% for a single average worker in 2024**.

That was above the OECD average of 34.9%, although slightly below the EU average of 41.7%. For a married average worker with two children, Spain’s wedge was 39.5%, above both the OECD average and the EU comparison cited by the OECD. 

The tax wedge includes:

- personal income tax;
- employee social-security contributions;
- employer social-security contributions;
- relevant cash benefits.

Workers often do not perceive employer contributions as part of their compensation because those amounts never appear in net salary.

This creates a gap between:

- what employment costs the company;
- the gross wage shown to the worker;
- the money reaching the bank account.

High labour taxation can discourage formal hiring, particularly for middle-income families and lower-productivity jobs.

But describing all social contributions as money “taken away” is also incomplete. They finance:

- pensions;
- unemployment insurance;
- healthcare-related protection;
- disability;
- parental leave;
- other social benefits.

The policy question is not simply whether taxes are high.

It is whether citizens receive sufficiently effective services and security in exchange.

---

# 17. Spain’s tax system relies heavily on labour and consumption

Revenue comes substantially from:

- personal income tax;
- social contributions;
- VAT;
- corporate tax;
- excise duties;
- property-related taxes;
- regional and municipal taxes.

The burden is not evenly visible.

Income tax and social contributions appear directly on payslips. VAT is embedded in prices. Property owners face transaction, ownership and capital-gains taxes at different stages. Companies face statutory rates but may reduce effective burdens through deductions, losses and international structures.

Spain’s political debate simplifies taxation into two narratives.

The left says Spain raises less revenue than some wealthier European welfare states and therefore has room to tax more.

The right says workers and companies already face excessive burdens and inefficient spending.

Both contain truth.

Spain’s total revenue has historically been below some high-spending European systems, while the burden on ordinary formal employment is already substantial.

The most defensible reform would shift some pressure away from productive labour toward:

- environmental externalities;
- under-taxed land-value gains;
- poorly designed tax privileges;
- fraud and evasion;
- selected consumption with social costs.

But every tax shift creates winners and losers. “Tax the rich” cannot finance every programme, while broad tax cuts cannot preserve every service.

---

# 18. Public services are part of the cost of living

Households do not experience living standards only through salary and prices.

They also experience:

- healthcare waiting times;
- public transport;
- childcare availability;
- education quality;
- dependency care;
- administrative delays;
- university costs;
- social housing.

A household with access to reliable public childcare and transport can live better on the same income than one paying privately for both.

Likewise, long healthcare waiting times may force people to purchase private insurance. Insufficient public housing forces vulnerable households into expensive private rentals.

This means public-service deterioration acts like an indirect price increase.

The official CPI does not capture the full cost when a nominally free public service becomes too slow or inaccessible to rely upon.

---

# 19. Productivity is the central long-term weakness

Spain cannot sustainably raise wages faster than the value produced per hour indefinitely.

The Banco de España expects productivity per employed person to remain weak in 2026 before improving in 2027. 

Productivity growth has improved compared with Spain’s worst historical periods, but the level and long-term trajectory remain insufficient to close the gap with the most productive European economies.

Weak productivity has several causes:

- small average company size;
- weak business investment;
- management quality;
- limited technology adoption;
- skill mismatches;
- school dropout;
- insufficient vocational training;
- regulatory fragmentation;
- administrative delays;
- low research commercialisation;
- concentration in low-value services;
- limited competition in some sectors.

Productivity is frequently misunderstood as forcing employees to work harder.

It actually means producing greater value through:

- better tools;
- better organisation;
- more capital;
- improved infrastructure;
- stronger skills;
- innovation;
- larger and better-managed companies.

Without productivity growth, Spain must choose among:

- low wages;
- higher prices;
- lower profits;
- reduced employment;
- greater public subsidy.

---

# 20. Spain’s company structure holds wages down

Most Spanish companies are small or very small.

Small firms can be innovative and productive. But on average they face greater difficulty:

- investing in technology;
- exporting;
- training workers;
- navigating regulation;
- financing growth;
- employing specialised managers;
- providing internal career progression.

Regulations and administrative obligations that change at certain employment or revenue thresholds can unintentionally discourage firms from growing past those boundaries.

The objective should not be to eliminate small firms.

It should be to help productive firms expand without facing disproportionate bureaucratic and financial barriers.

A worker in a larger productive company is more likely to receive:

- higher wages;
- training;
- promotion;
- benefits;
- stable hours;
- technology support.

Spain’s wage problem is partly a company-growth problem.

---

# 21. Tourism is both an economic strength and a productivity trap

Tourism creates:

- exports;
- employment;
- regional income;
- tax revenue;
- investment;
- international visibility.

Spain’s tourism infrastructure and global appeal are genuine competitive advantages.

But tourism also concentrates employment in sectors with:

- seasonal demand;
- low average wages;
- high housing pressure;
- limited productivity growth;
- vulnerability to international shocks.

The question should not be whether Spain should abandon tourism.

It should be whether Spain can capture more value per visitor through:

- higher-quality services;
- better training;
- less seasonal demand;
- improved worker accommodation;
- stronger local taxation of externalities;
- limits in saturated areas;
- investment in non-tourism sectors.

More tourists do not automatically produce better lives if local workers cannot afford to live in the destination.

---

# 22. Immigration is supporting growth, but also exposes capacity limits

Recent population growth has increased:

- labour supply;
- employment;
- consumption;
- tax contributions;
- housing demand;
- public-service needs.

The Banco de España has estimated that foreign residents contributed positively to Spain’s per-capita GDP growth between 2022 and 2024.

Immigration is therefore not merely inflating total GDP through a larger population.

But its benefits depend heavily on what jobs migrants obtain.

If newcomers are concentrated in:

- agriculture;
- hospitality;
- domestic care;
- logistics;
- construction;
- low-paid services,

they increase output and solve labour shortages, but may not transform productivity.

Rapid population growth also increases demand for housing faster than supply can respond.

The economic contradiction is:

> Spain needs workers, but has not built enough homes or public capacity for the population growth supporting its economy.

---

# 23. European funds are an opportunity, not proof of transformation

Spain has received large amounts of financing through the EU Recovery and Resilience Facility.

The funds support:

- renewable energy;
- digitalisation;
- transport;
- housing rehabilitation;
- industrial projects;
- public administration;
- training;
- research.

Their potential is significant.

But political debate often measures success by:

- funds allocated;
- calls published;
- contracts signed;
- money formally committed.

The true test is whether they produce:

- higher productivity;
- additional private investment;
- functioning infrastructure;
- lower energy costs;
- stronger companies;
- better skills;
- durable employment.

Money spent is an input, not an outcome.

Projects should be evaluated after completion and compared against credible alternatives.

---

# 24. Public debt has fallen relative to GDP, but remains high

Spain’s debt ratio has declined from its pandemic peak, helped by:

- economic growth;
- inflation;
- employment;
- stronger tax revenue.

But debt remained above 100% of GDP in the most recent medium-term assessments. AIReF expects the 2026 public deficit to be approximately **2.6% of GDP**. 

A deficit below 3% is not an immediate emergency.

The long-term risk comes from:

- ageing;
- pensions;
- healthcare;
- dependency care;
- interest costs;
- climate adaptation;
- defence spending;
- weak productivity.

AIReF has warned that without further measures, the deficit and debt would deteriorate significantly over the long term; one scenario places debt near 123% of GDP by 2050. 

Spain is therefore enjoying a period in which growth makes the fiscal position look better, but structural pressures remain.

The prudent time to reform public finances is during growth, not after the next crisis.

---

# 25. Pensions are socially strong but fiscally demanding

Spain’s pension system provides significant protection against old-age poverty.

Pensions are linked to inflation, giving retirees greater purchasing-power protection than many workers receive. In 2026, contributory pensions were revalued by 2.7%. 

This stability supports household consumption and protects elderly people who cannot return to work.

But pension expenditure is growing because:

- more people retire;
- retirees live longer;
- new pensions are generally higher than those leaving the system;
- the working-age-to-retiree ratio is declining.

Higher employment and immigration increase contributions, but may not be enough without productivity and wage growth.

The political debate avoids the full set of trade-offs.

A sustainable pension system can be supported through some combination of:

- higher contributions;
- later retirement;
- lower relative benefits;
- more workers;
- higher productivity;
- tax financing;
- additional debt.

There is no painless option.

---

# 26. The welfare state’s strongest protection is not always directed at the poorest

Spain spends heavily on contributory pensions because the system protects entitlements earned through work.

Means-tested benefits such as the Minimum Living Income are more targeted toward poverty but can face:

- complex applications;
- incomplete coverage;
- delayed processing;
- lack of awareness;
- interactions with regional benefits.

In 2026, the guaranteed IMV income for a single adult was €733.60 per month, increasing with household size. 

The central distributional question is not merely how much Spain spends socially.

It is who receives protection automatically and who must prove vulnerability through a difficult administrative process.

---

# 27. The cost of living is also a family-wealth issue

Two people with the same salary can have completely different living standards.

One may receive:

- a family home;
- a deposit gift;
- free childcare from grandparents;
- inherited property;
- help during unemployment.

The other may support relatives, rent privately and have no inheritance.

Income statistics do not fully capture this difference.

Spain’s high homeownership history means wealth transfers increasingly determine:

- who buys a home;
- who can live in a major city;
- who starts a family;
- who can take entrepreneurial risk;
- who survives unemployment without debt.

The cost-of-living crisis therefore reinforces hereditary advantage.

# 28. Testing the main political claims

## “Spain’s economy is booming”

GDP and employment growth are strong relative to Europe.

But housing affordability, productivity and poverty remain serious weaknesses.

**Verdict:** accurate about aggregate momentum; misleading if presented as proof that most households feel prosperous.

## “The economy is collapsing”

Spain is creating employment, expanding output and maintaining access to financing.

**Verdict:** unsupported by the main macroeconomic indicators.

## “People are poorer than ever”

Many households suffered a real-income loss during the inflation shock, especially renters and low-income families. But wages and incomes have more recently recovered, and employment is much stronger than during the post-2008 crisis.

**Verdict:** hardship is real, but the absolute claim is false.

## “Wages have not increased”

Average and minimum wages have risen substantially.

**Verdict:** false in nominal terms; incomplete in real terms because accumulated prices and housing costs differ across households.

## “The minimum wage destroyed employment”

Employment continued to grow after major minimum-wage increases.

**Verdict:** no evidence of a broad national employment collapse, though some sectors and firms may face adjustment costs.

## “Companies are keeping all the inflationary gains”

Some firms and sectors expanded margins; others absorbed cost increases or failed.

**Verdict:** must be tested sector by sector. There is no single corporate-margin story.

## “Taxes in Spain are low”

Total revenue is lower than in some northern European welfare states, but the labour tax wedge for an average worker is already above the OECD average.

**Verdict:** depends on the tax, household and comparison.

## “Tax cuts pay for themselves”

Tax cuts can stimulate activity, but rarely replace their full revenue cost automatically.

**Verdict:** generally unsupported as a universal claim.

## “Public spending always improves the economy”

Spending can raise welfare and productivity, but ineffective projects, subsidies and bureaucracy can consume resources without lasting value.

**Verdict:** outcomes matter more than expenditure totals.

## “Tourism makes Spain rich”

Tourism generates enormous value and employment but also supports low-wage work and intensifies housing pressure.

**Verdict:** major strength, insufficient development strategy.

## “Immigration only inflates GDP”

Immigration has increased labour supply and contributed to per-capita growth, but it also creates housing and service demand.

**Verdict:** economically beneficial overall in recent evidence, but distribution and capacity matter.

# 29. What a serious economic programme would prioritise

## A. Treat housing as economic infrastructure

Housing policy should be integrated with:

- employment;
- transport;
- migration;
- universities;
- industrial development;
- childcare.

A city cannot attract nurses, teachers, engineers or hospitality workers indefinitely if they cannot afford to live there.

## B. Raise productivity rather than relying only on labour growth

Priorities should include:

- company scaling;
- vocational training;
- management quality;
- digital investment;
- research commercialisation;
- faster licensing;
- regulatory simplification;
- competition;
- infrastructure.

## C. Reduce the tax penalty on low and middle employment

Possible approaches include:

- targeted social-contribution reductions;
- stronger child benefits;
- refundable tax credits;
- better coordination of benefits and earnings;
- reducing abrupt benefit withdrawal.

Any reduction must be financed transparently.

## D. Make childcare a labour-market policy

Affordable childcare raises:

- employment;
- household income;
- fertility choice;
- women’s career progression;
- tax revenue.

## E. Link wage growth to productive upgrading

Minimum wages and collective bargaining should be accompanied by:

- investment support;
- training;
- technology adoption;
- enforcement against unfair low-cost competition.

## F. Evaluate every major subsidy

Report:

- beneficiary;
- objective;
- cost;
- additional investment generated;
- employment created;
- productivity result;
- survival after support ends.

## G. Reform public spending before cutting essential services

AIReF and other evaluators should receive stronger follow-up powers. Evaluations should affect future budgets.

## H. Build a regional cost-of-living index

National salary and poverty figures should be adjusted for:

- housing;
- transport;
- childcare;
- energy;
- local prices.

## I. Protect vulnerable households through targeted support

Broad tax cuts on energy or food can benefit high-consuming households.

Targeted transfers can deliver more protection per euro, although administration must be simplified.

## J. Make intergenerational fairness explicit

Every major budget should report its impact on:

- children;
- young adults;
- working-age households;
- retirees;
- future debt payers.

---

# 30. What should be measured instead of political slogans

A serious national dashboard should track:

- real median disposable income;
- median rent-to-income ratio;
- house-price-to-income ratio;
- real median wage;
- hourly productivity;
- involuntary part-time employment;
- household savings by income group;
- poverty after housing costs;
- childcare costs;
- public-service waiting times;
- energy poverty;
- business investment;
- new firms that scale;
- debt interest costs;
- intergenerational transfers.

GDP should remain part of the dashboard.

It should not dominate it.

# Final verdict

Spain’s economic performance is better than the national mood suggests—but the national mood is not irrational.

The country has achieved:

- strong GDP growth;
- record employment;
- lower contractual temporariness;
- substantial minimum-wage increases;
- rising average incomes;
- resilience during major European shocks.

Those are genuine successes.

But too much of the benefit is weakened by:

- housing inflation;
- low productivity;
- low wages in service sectors;
- high unemployment;
- child and working poverty;
- labour taxation;
- regional price differences;
- difficulty forming independent households;
- long-term pension and debt pressure.

Spain is not becoming poorer in aggregate.

It is becoming **more divided between those protected from essential-cost inflation and those exposed to it**.

The main dividing lines are increasingly:

- owner versus renter;
- inherited wealth versus no family support;
- established pensioner versus young worker;
- productive firm versus low-margin service employer;
- high-growth city resident versus affordable-region resident;
- stable dual-income household versus single or precarious earner.

The economy’s central challenge is no longer simply to create jobs.

It is to create an economy in which a normal job permits:

- independent housing;
- family formation;
- savings;
- resilience to emergencies;
- reasonable leisure;
- confidence in the future.

Until wage and productivity growth consistently outrun the cost of housing and essential services, Spain can continue outperforming Europe statistically while many residents continue to feel that the economy is failing them.



# Spain’s political system: democracy works, but trust is collapsing around it

### Spain continues to hold competitive elections, change governments peacefully and prosecute corruption. Yet citizens increasingly experience politics as a permanent conflict between party machines that protect their own, colonise institutions and postpone difficult reforms.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

Spain is not facing the collapse of democracy.

It is facing a crisis of **political credibility, institutional neutrality and governing capacity**.

The country’s democratic machinery still works:

- elections are competitive;
- governments can lose power;
- courts investigate senior officials;
- parliament can block the executive;
- regional governments retain substantial autonomy;
- media and civil society expose misconduct.

But the system increasingly rewards behaviour that weakens those same institutions:

- parties defend conduct they would condemn in an opponent;
- leaders concentrate power inside organisations;
- parliamentary negotiation becomes theatrical confrontation;
- appointments are treated as territorial spoils;
- political responsibility is delayed until a final criminal conviction;
- governing coalitions exchange support through opaque bilateral deals;
- policy announcements receive more attention than implementation;
- scandals displace long-term questions such as housing, productivity, education and ageing.

Spain’s problem is therefore not “too much politics.”

It is **too much partisan competition over institutions and too little competition over measurable governing results**.

---

# 1. The political system in one picture

Spain is governed by Pedro Sánchez’s PSOE-led coalition with Sumar. It does not command an automatic majority and depends on separate agreements with parties including ERC, Junts, EH Bildu, PNV, BNG and Coalición Canaria according to the issue.

The Congress elected in 2023 remains fragmented. As of July 2026, its largest groups were:

| Parliamentary group | Deputies |
|---|---:|
| Partido Popular | 137 |
| PSOE | 121 |
| Vox | 33 |
| Sumar | 27 |
| ERC | 7 |
| Junts | 7 |
| EH Bildu | 6 |
| PNV | 5 |
| Mixed Group | 7 |

The figures can change temporarily when deputies resign or are replaced, but the fundamental arithmetic remains: neither the left nor the right can govern alone. 

This fragmentation is often portrayed as democratic dysfunction. That is only partly correct.

A fragmented parliament can force compromise and represent territorial diversity. The dysfunction appears when parties prefer:

- short-term bilateral concessions over transparent multilateral agreements;
- decree-laws over negotiated legislation;
- blocking the opponent over improving a proposal;
- symbolic votes designed for social media;
- permanent electoral positioning rather than stable policy.

The minority government’s difficulty passing budgets and ordinary legislation is therefore not solely the consequence of fragmentation. It reflects weak trust among parties and a negotiating culture in which almost every agreement is treated as betrayal.

---

# 2. Spain’s citizens do not reject democracy; they reject its political operators

The distinction is essential.

Spanish citizens generally continue to prefer democracy over authoritarian alternatives. What has deteriorated is confidence in:

- parties;
- political leaders;
- parliament;
- judicial appointments;
- the neutrality of institutions;
- the honesty of political communication.

The May 2026 CIS barometer found that political concerns appeared in several overlapping categories:

- government, parties or particular politicians: **12.7%**;
- political problems generally: **12.1%**;
- politicians’ bad behaviour: **9.5%**;
- corruption and fraud: **9.1%**.

These categories should not be added mechanically because respondents could mention three problems, but together they show that political conduct itself is perceived as a significant national problem. 

The striking finding is that distrust is not confined to one camp.

In the same survey:

- Pedro Sánchez inspired “much” or “quite a lot” of confidence in 35.8%, but “little” or “none” in 63%;
- Alberto Núñez Feijóo inspired much or quite a lot of confidence in only 18.7%, while 80.3% expressed little or none.

Their mean ratings were:

| Leader | Mean rating, 1–10 |
|---|---:|
| Pedro Sánchez | 4.59 |
| Yolanda Díaz | 4.25 |
| Alberto Núñez Feijóo | 3.74 |
| Santiago Abascal | 2.93 |

No major leader reached 5 out of 10. 

This matters more than which leader ranked first.

> Spain is not choosing between a broadly trusted government and a broadly trusted opposition. It is choosing among leaders rejected by large portions of society.

---

# 3. Polarisation is asymmetric in each voter’s mind—and reciprocal in the system

Each party describes itself as defending democracy from an exceptional threat.

For the left, the threat is:

- the far right;
- reaction against social rights;
- privatisation;
- attacks on minorities;
- judicial and media conservatism.

For the right, the threat is:

- Sánchez’s alleged institutional capture;
- dependence on separatist parties;
- the amnesty;
- fiscal intervention;
- weakening of national unity.

For nationalist parties, the threat is often:

- Spanish centralism;
- judicialisation of territorial conflict;
- insufficient recognition of plurinationality.

Each concern contains real elements. The problem is what follows from them.

When citizens believe the opponent is not merely mistaken but illegitimate, almost any tactic becomes defensible:

- politicising appointments;
- blocking institutional renewals;
- changing previous principles;
- governing through exceptional procedures;
- attacking judges, prosecutors or journalists;
- tolerating allies’ misconduct;
- using parliamentary inquiries as partisan prosecution;
- presenting electoral defeat as evidence of manipulation.

Polarisation is not simply strong ideological disagreement. It is the belief that ordinary democratic restraint should not apply because the opponent poses an existential danger.

---

# 4. Spain’s parties are leader-centred organisations with weak internal counterweights

Spanish parties formally hold congresses, primaries and internal votes. In practice, national leaders usually dominate:

- candidate lists;
- parliamentary careers;
- internal appointments;
- campaign resources;
- disciplinary procedures;
- communication strategy;
- access to government positions.

The closed-list electoral system reinforces this. Most deputies owe their place less to a personal constituency than to their position on a party list.

This changes parliamentary behaviour.

A deputy who votes against the party leadership risks losing:

- committee roles;
- media visibility;
- future candidacy;
- organisational support;
- access to executive office.

The result is a parliament composed of individually elected representatives who usually behave as disciplined voting blocs.

Party discipline is necessary for coherent government. But excessive discipline makes parliament weaker as a place of scrutiny.

A government deputy may defend a policy they privately doubt. An opposition deputy may reject a measure they privately support because granting the government a victory is strategically costly.

---

# 5. The Sánchez model: political survival as a governing method

Pedro Sánchez has demonstrated exceptional tactical resilience.

He rebuilt his authority after losing control of the PSOE, returned through a party primary, removed Mariano Rajoy through a no-confidence motion, formed Spain’s first national coalition government in the democratic era and retained office after the 2023 election despite PSOE finishing behind the PP.

## Strengths

His governments can credibly claim:

- strong employment growth;
- increases in the minimum wage;
- labour-market reform agreed with unions and employers;
- expansion of social protections;
- a strong European orientation;
- management of major crises without democratic breakdown;
- willingness to negotiate territorial conflicts politically rather than relying only on courts.

Pedro Sánchez remained prime minister in July 2026 and led the PSOE-Sumar executive. 

## Weaknesses

The same adaptability produces a credibility problem.

Policies previously rejected as unacceptable can later become necessary once parliamentary survival depends on them. The amnesty is the clearest example: its transformation from rejected proposal to central investiture agreement allowed opponents to argue that institutional principles were subordinate to remaining in office.

The Sánchez leadership style is also highly presidential:

- communication is centralised;
- ministers frequently depend on the prime minister’s strategic line;
- party and government interests overlap closely;
- criticism from within PSOE is limited;
- major decisions can emerge from negotiations conducted by a narrow leadership group.

The current government is additionally damaged by investigations involving people formerly close to the centre of PSOE power. That does not establish Sánchez’s criminal involvement. It does raise questions about appointment judgment, supervision and the party’s willingness to impose political responsibility before courts complete every stage.

## Evaluation

Sánchez is more effective at obtaining and retaining power than at creating trust beyond his coalition.

His defenders underestimate the damage caused by reversals, institutional confrontation and corruption allegations around former collaborators.

His opponents underestimate his policy achievements, electoral resilience and ability to integrate parliamentary fragmentation without a constitutional crisis.

---

# 6. The Feijóo problem: leading the largest party without controlling the political agenda

Alberto Núñez Feijóo entered national politics presenting himself as a pragmatic regional administrator.

The PP won the most seats in 2023 but could not construct a parliamentary majority. Its dependence on Vox as the only plausible large national partner narrowed its capacity to attract the regional and nationalist parties required for investiture.

## Strengths

The PP can claim:

- extensive governing experience;
- strong regional and municipal organisation;
- credibility among many businesses and higher-income voters;
- a clearer emphasis on taxation, administrative simplification and private investment;
- the ability to present itself as an alternative to Sánchez’s dependence on separatist parties.

## Weaknesses

Feijóo faces a strategic contradiction.

To defeat PSOE, PP must attract:

- moderate former socialist or centrist voters;
- conservative voters tempted by Vox;
- territorial parties that distrust centralist politics.

Moving toward Vox helps with the second group but harms the first and third.

The PP also remains constrained by its corruption history. Its current leadership did not create Gürtel, but the party has never fully escaped the institutional consequences of having benefited from corrupt networks.

Internal competition with Isabel Díaz Ayuso further complicates Feijóo’s authority. Ayuso’s more confrontational style often generates greater mobilisation and media attention than the national leader’s attempt at moderation.

## Evaluation

Feijóo has successfully kept the PP dominant on the right but has not yet solved the coalition arithmetic needed to govern Spain.

His principal weakness is not insufficient opposition. It is the absence of a convincing answer to this question:

> What would a PP government do when Vox’s demands conflict with the moderate and European image Feijóo wants to project?

---

# 7. Vox: successful agenda-setting without broad social trust

Vox has reshaped Spanish political debate despite remaining far from a majority.

Issues it has pushed toward the centre include:

- immigration;
- national identity;
- opposition to gender legislation;
- criticism of autonomous communities;
- crime;
- agricultural and rural discontent;
- rejection of climate and progressive cultural policies.

## Strengths

Vox offers voters:

- ideological clarity;
- message discipline;
- direct language;
- rejection of elite consensus;
- a strong national identity;
- willingness to express positions other parties soften.

It has converted dissatisfaction with PP caution into a durable political space.

## Weaknesses

Vox’s model depends heavily on conflict and threat perception.

Its proposals can involve:

- recentralising powers;
- reversing equality and memory policies;
- weakening climate commitments;
- severe immigration restrictions;
- confrontation with territorial pluralism.

Some proposals would encounter constitutional, European or administrative constraints.

Vox’s leader, Santiago Abascal, had the lowest mean rating among the four major leaders in the May 2026 CIS barometer, although his core electorate is much more enthusiastic than the national average. 

## Evaluation

Vox is effective at identifying fears and forcing other parties to respond. It is less convincing at showing how maximalist positions would operate inside Spain’s decentralised constitution and the European Union.

Its rise is partly caused by problems mainstream parties minimised—especially migration control, insecurity perceptions and cultural alienation. But it often converts legitimate problems into excessively broad group narratives.

---

# 8. Sumar and the fragmented left: policy influence without organisational consolidation

Sumar entered government as a coalition of multiple left-wing organisations. It retained important ministries and policy influence but failed to become a unified political organisation comparable to PSOE or PP.

## Strengths

The space to PSOE’s left has influenced:

- labour rights;
- minimum-wage policy;
- working-time debates;
- tenant protection;
- social benefits;
- feminist and environmental policy.

Yolanda Díaz has generally presented a more negotiating and institutionally moderate image than some previous leaders of the radical left.

## Weaknesses

The project suffers from:

- competition among Sumar, Podemos, Izquierda Unida, Más Madrid, Compromís and regional actors;
- unclear leadership;
- weak territorial organisation;
- disputes over candidate selection;
- tension between governing compromise and activist mobilisation;
- difficulty explaining an identity separate from PSOE.

Podemos’ departure from the wider coalition demonstrated that left unity was more electoral arrangement than stable organisation.

## Evaluation

The left of PSOE has had greater influence on public policy than its current electoral strength suggests. But organisational fragmentation allows PSOE to dominate the coalition while frustrated voters either abstain or seek more confrontational alternatives.

---

# 9. Territorial parties are not anomalies; they are structural actors

ERC, Junts, EH Bildu, PNV, BNG and Coalición Canaria are often described as extracting concessions from weak national governments.

That description is not entirely wrong. Their small number of seats can become decisive, allowing them to negotiate:

- investment;
- debt arrangements;
- institutional powers;
- language policy;
- transport;
- taxation;
- legal reforms.

But the criticism often ignores that Spain’s electoral system intentionally represents territorial communities.

The deeper problem is lack of transparency.

Citizens may discover major territorial agreements only after closed negotiations. The agreement is then defended as necessary arithmetic rather than as a public policy that can be evaluated on national and regional merits.

## Different models

These parties should not be treated as one bloc:

- **PNV** generally combines institutional pragmatism, business orientation and Basque self-government.
- **EH Bildu** combines left economics with Basque sovereignty and has expanded its institutional legitimacy, while opponents continue to focus on its historical relationship to the political world around ETA.
- **ERC** generally pursues negotiated independence and social-democratic policy.
- **Junts** uses a more confrontational and leader-centred strategy, with greater willingness to threaten government stability.
- **BNG** combines Galician nationalism and left-wing economics.
- **Coalición Canaria** focuses more directly on island interests and fiscal arrangements.

## Evaluation

Territorial parties often represent their voters effectively. The democratic weakness lies in a system where nationally important policy can depend on bilateral agreements without a shared public negotiating framework.

# 10. Corruption cases by political party

A fair comparison must distinguish:

1. final convictions;
2. convictions still subject to appeal;
3. ongoing investigations;
4. allegations or archived cases.

It is impossible to produce a meaningful league table simply by counting named cases. One proceeding may concern one mayor; another may contain dozens of branches, contracts and defendants.

The following is a **non-exhaustive map of the most significant cases connected to parties currently represented in Spanish politics or their direct predecessors**.

## Partido Popular

### Gürtel — final convictions

The Gürtel network involved bribery, fraudulent procurement, embezzlement, tax offences, money laundering and influence around PP-controlled administrations.

The Supreme Court confirmed the principal convictions in 2020. It also confirmed the PP’s civil liability as a beneficiary “for profit” of corrupt activity. That status did not mean the party itself was criminally convicted of committing corruption, but it judicially established that it obtained economic benefit from the scheme. 

### Bárcenas and the parallel accounts

The Gürtel litigation included the conviction of former PP treasurer Luis Bárcenas. Separate proceedings concerning renovation of the party headquarters also reinforced evidence of undeclared party accounting, although legal findings differed across defendants and proceedings.

### Púnica — multiple branches

Púnica examined corrupt contracting across municipalities and regional institutions, especially in Madrid. In December 2025, the National Court convicted 29 natural and legal persons in the energy-contract branch, including former mayors from municipalities governed by different parties. The judgment was a first-instance decision and therefore should not be presented as final where appeals remain. 

### Political assessment

No current PP leader should be presumed responsible for crimes committed by previous officials without evidence. But Gürtel was not an isolated private act. It exposed organisational failures across several PP administrations.

The party’s unresolved weakness is that it often treats corruption as historical contamination rather than a reason for permanent reforms to procurement, internal accounting and candidate selection.

---

## PSOE

### ERE of Andalusia — proven fraud, but senior-leadership convictions legally revised

The ERE system distributed large amounts of public money through an unlawful and weakly controlled mechanism. Several people were convicted in different branches for misuse, commissions and inclusion of ineligible beneficiaries.

The legal position of former presidents and senior officials became more complicated after the Constitutional Court partially upheld appeals in 2024, finding violations in how some conduct had been classified and ordering lower courts to reconsider parts of the cases. It is therefore inaccurate either to claim that the whole ERE scandal was invented or that every original senior conviction remains intact without qualification. 

### Koldo/Ábalos-related proceedings — investigation and prosecution, not collective party conviction

The case originated in suspected irregularities around pandemic-era contracts and expanded into allegations involving commissions, influence and former senior PSOE-linked figures. José Luis Ábalos and his former adviser Koldo García became central defendants.

The existence of judicial proceedings and evidence presented by investigators does not prove that PSOE as an organisation or Pedro Sánchez participated in the alleged offences.

It does justify scrutiny of:

- why individuals accumulated influence;
- whether internal warnings existed;
- how emergency suppliers were selected;
- when the party leadership acted;
- whether political responsibility was imposed promptly.

The Audiencia Nacional’s institutional record confirms that the investigation began with the February 2024 arrests, including Koldo García. 

### Political assessment

PSOE’s defence often moves too rapidly from “Sánchez has not been charged” to “the party has no institutional problem.”

Those are different statements.

A leader need not be criminally implicated for the party to have failed in appointments, oversight or political accountability.

---

## Convergència Democràtica de Catalunya and the Junts political tradition

### Palau case — final convictions

The Supreme Court confirmed that CDC obtained €6.6 million through an influence-peddling and commission system connected to public works and the Palau de la Música.

The judgment ordered confiscation of the gains and confirmed convictions of Palau executives and CDC’s former treasurer. 

CDC later disappeared and Junts is a legally different party. That distinction matters legally.

Politically, Junts cannot simply treat CDC’s history as unrelated: there is substantial continuity in personnel, electorate and political tradition. But legal liability cannot be transferred automatically without a court’s determination; litigation over organisational succession has itself been contested. 

---

## PNV

### De Miguel case

The De Miguel case concerned a network involving former PNV officials in Álava and offences including bribery, influence peddling and money laundering. It became the most important corruption case associated with the party.

It does not establish that PNV as an organisation was criminally responsible. It does contradict any claim that the party’s reputation for administrative competence makes it immune to patronage networks.

The PNV’s response was institutionally less destructive than the denial strategies seen in some national cases, but the duration of proceedings again weakened timely accountability.

---

## Vox

There is no final criminal corruption judgment against Vox comparable in scale and status to Gürtel, the ERE branches or the Palau case in the official material reviewed for this investigation.

That does not mean its financing or internal management is beyond scrutiny. Questions and administrative proceedings concerning party financing must be described according to their legal status. Administrative accounting irregularities are not automatically criminal corruption.

The appropriate conclusion is not “Vox has no corruption.” It is:

> Vox is newer, has governed fewer large administrations and has therefore had fewer opportunities to generate the type of long-running procurement networks associated with older governing parties.

Risk generally rises with control of budgets, land, appointments and contracts.

---

## Podemos and the Sumar parties

Investigations involving Podemos—especially the Neurona consultancy controversy—generated extensive political claims, but major accusations were archived or failed to produce a party-level corruption conviction.

That does not validate every financial or organisational decision. It shows why an investigation cannot be listed as proven corruption merely because it dominated headlines.

No comparable final party-level corruption judgment was identified against Sumar as an organisation, which was created much more recently.

---

## ERC, EH Bildu and BNG

The official sources reviewed did not reveal a final party-level corruption judgment of a scale comparable to Gürtel or Palau against the present organisations.

This is not proof that every official has acted properly. Local misconduct, investigations or individual offences may exist.

The comparison must also account for exposure: parties that have controlled more administrations, larger procurement budgets and urban-planning powers have had more opportunities for corruption.

# 11. Why a corruption ranking by number of cases would be misleading

A table saying “Party A has 200 cases and Party B has 20” would look precise but would be journalistically weak.

There is no universally accepted definition of a “case.”

Possible counting units include:

- police operations;
- court proceedings;
- branches of one macro-case;
- defendants;
- convictions;
- municipalities;
- contracts;
- euros affected.

Political lists circulated online often:

- count archived allegations;
- duplicate branches;
- attribute an individual’s acts to an entire party;
- exclude allied parties;
- mix final convictions with newspaper reports;
- compare forty years of one party with ten years of another.

A responsible corruption index should instead measure:

- final convictions;
- public money proven affected;
- seniority of the officials;
- organisational benefit;
- recurrence;
- party response;
- recovery of funds;
- preventive reform.

# 12. Politicians are evaluated more by identity than by performance

Voters increasingly interpret facts through party allegiance.

The same event receives opposite readings:

| Event | Government supporters may say | Opposition supporters may say |
|---|---|---|
| Economic growth | Effective progressive management | Growth driven by debt, migration or Europe |
| Amnesty | Political reconciliation | Exchange of impunity for parliamentary votes |
| Judicial investigation | Presumption of innocence | Evidence of systemic corruption |
| Protest | Democratic mobilisation | Manipulated partisan campaign |
| Institutional appointment | Legitimate democratic mandate | Capture of the institution |
| Regional agreement | Recognition of pluralism | Privilege and blackmail |
| Spending increase | Stronger welfare state | Clientelism and fiscal irresponsibility |

This does not mean objective evaluation is impossible.

It means evaluation needs predeclared criteria that do not change according to the party being judged.

# 13. A better way to evaluate politicians

Every national or regional leader should be scored annually across six categories.

## Integrity

- Proven conflicts of interest.
- Accuracy of declarations.
- Response to corruption allegations.
- Protection or removal of implicated officials.
- Transparency of meetings and assets.

## Institutional conduct

- Respect for judicial decisions.
- Use of decree-laws.
- Compliance with parliamentary oversight.
- Independence of appointments.
- Treatment of critical media.

## Delivery

- Laws implemented, not merely announced.
- Budget execution.
- Waiting times.
- Housing delivered.
- Infrastructure completed.
- Administrative processing improved.

## Economic and social results

- Median income.
- Employment quality.
- Productivity.
- Poverty.
- Housing burden.
- Educational achievement.
- Healthcare access.

## Truthfulness

- False factual claims corrected.
- Manipulated statistics.
- Broken commitments explained.
- Public documents released.
- Campaign promises tracked.

## Cooperation

- Cross-party agreements.
- Territorial coordination.
- Negotiation with social partners.
- Ability to revise failed policy.
- Reduction rather than exploitation of social conflict.

A leader could perform well in one category and badly in another. That is more informative than a single approval rating.

# 15. Political communication is becoming less informative

Modern Spanish political communication increasingly relies on:

- short video clips;
- selective parliamentary confrontations;
- party-aligned television panels;
- anonymous briefings;
- emotionally loaded labels;
- decontextualised statistics;
- claims that opponents are criminals or traitors;
- social-media narratives produced before facts are verified.

This has three effects.

## First: governing becomes campaigning

Every policy is announced as a historic achievement. Every setback is blamed on obstruction.

## Second: correction loses value

A false claim may reach millions before a technical correction appears.

## Third: citizens select incompatible realities

Right- and left-leaning audiences may encounter different facts, not merely different opinions.

Parties benefit from this because loyal media environments reduce the cost of inconsistency.

---

# 16. The media are watchdogs and political actors at the same time

Spanish journalism has uncovered major corruption cases and institutional abuses.

But media organisations also depend on:

- owners;
- advertisers;
- bank financing;
- government advertising;
- political access;
- audience polarisation;
- selective leaks.

A newspaper can publish an important investigation and still apply unequal standards between parties.

A leaked police document may reveal serious evidence. It may also contain an untested hypothesis.

The solution is not neutral journalism without conclusions. It is transparent journalism that labels:

- documented fact;
- allegation;
- inference;
- anonymous-source claim;
- procedural status;
- correction.

---

# 17. Parliament is active but not sufficiently deliberative

Congress formally performs:

- legislation;
- government oversight;
- budget approval;
- appointment functions;
- public debate.

In practice, much public attention focuses on weekly confrontation rather than committee work, amendments or implementation.

The government uses decree-laws partly because it lacks a stable majority and because urgent action is sometimes necessary. But repeated use reduces parliamentary preparation and pushes negotiation toward ratification after the executive has acted.

The opposition uses amendments, inquiries and procedural tools legitimately, but also has incentives to prevent government success rather than improve legislation.

Congress maintains an official record of legislation approved during the current legislature, demonstrating that it is not literally paralysed. The political question is whether legislative output matches Spain’s structural problems and whether laws receive sufficient implementation review. 

---

# 18. Institutional appointments remain a recurring battlefield

Spain’s parties have repeatedly struggled to renew or agree appointments to:

- the General Council of the Judiciary;
- the Constitutional Court;
- the Court of Auditors;
- public broadcasters;
- regulatory bodies;
- senior prosecution or institutional posts.

The issue is not that parliament should have no role. Democratic institutions often require parliamentary appointment.

The risk arises when selection functions as a quota:

> this party chooses these members; that party chooses those.

That model can produce qualified appointees, but it creates an appearance that members represent the party that selected them.

The European Commission’s rule-of-law process continues to assess Spain across judicial independence, anti-corruption, media pluralism and checks and balances. It has also monitored lobbying regulation and institutional reforms. 

---

# 19. The CIS is useful—but should not be the sole electoral truth

The May 2026 CIS estimated:

- PSOE: 36.2%;
- PP: 24.9%;
- Vox: 16.2%;
- Sumar: 5.7%;
- ERC: 2.6%;
- Podemos: 2.5%;
- Se Acabó la Fiesta: 2.4%.

The CIS explicitly states that its estimate measures support during the collection period and is not a prediction. It also publishes microdata so other analysts can apply different models. 

This is important because the CIS has become part of political conflict.

Government supporters cite it as an official survey. Opponents question its leadership, model and divergences from private pollsters.

The correct approach is:

- publish raw intention;
- publish the model;
- compare multiple pollsters;
- calculate polling averages;
- report uncertainty;
- evaluate past error.

No single poll should be used as a substitute for an election.

# 20. Spain’s democratic strengths are often overlooked

A serious investigation should not turn dissatisfaction into a claim that nothing works.

Spain retains major democratic assets:

- peaceful alternation of power;
- high electoral participation;
- strong regional institutions;
- independent investigative journalism;
- courts willing to convict senior politicians;
- active civil society;
- EU legal and institutional constraints;
- a professional permanent administration;
- comparatively low political violence;
- social acceptance of electoral outcomes.

The fact that corruption cases become public and reach court is partly evidence of corruption—but also evidence of detection.

The democratic danger comes when scandals are used to discredit every court, journalist, election or institution rather than to repair the specific failure.

# 21. The principal democratic weaknesses

## Low preventive accountability

Political consequences often wait for criminal judgments.

## Institutional quota-sharing

Appointments are perceived as party-controlled.

## Excessive leadership power

Internal dissent is weak.

## Closed electoral lists

Voters have limited ability to reward or punish individual legislators.

## Weak implementation culture

Announcements dominate follow-up.

## Fragmented transparency

Contracts, meetings, interests and execution data are difficult to connect.

## Media polarisation

Facts are filtered through partisan ecosystems.

## Short-term coalition bargaining

Agreements are often opaque and specific rather than rule-based.

## Unequal territorial incentives

Small pivotal parties can obtain concessions unavailable to non-pivotal regions.

# 22. What political reform should look like

## A. Establish a common integrity standard

Parties should agree that:

- opening of trial triggers suspension from executive or party-finance roles;
- formal investigation triggers disclosure and independent internal review;
- a final conviction for corruption triggers expulsion;
- political responsibility can arise without criminal guilt;
- identical standards apply to allies and opponents.

## B. Reform electoral lists without destroying governability

A workable model could retain proportional representation while allowing voters to alter candidate order within a party list.

This would give deputies some independent legitimacy without converting Spain entirely into single-member constituencies.

## C. Democratise parties

Require:

- transparent membership rolls;
- audited internal elections;
- published leadership debates;
- protection for internal minorities;
- external financial audits;
- clear candidate-selection rules.

## D. Professionalise appointments

For regulators and public companies:

1. publish qualifications;
2. hold public hearings;
3. produce independent suitability reports;
4. disclose conflicts;
5. explain the final choice.

## E. Publish coalition agreements as measurable contracts

Every agreement supporting a government should state:

- measure;
- cost;
- legal instrument;
- deadline;
- responsible body;
- territorial impact;
- completion status.

## F. Create a public promise tracker

Each party manifesto should be converted into a standard database:

- completed;
- partially completed;
- delayed;
- abandoned;
- contradicted;
- impossible to evaluate.

## G. Measure legislative quality

Congress should review major laws after two or three years against:

- objectives;
- costs;
- unintended effects;
- enforcement;
- distributional impact.

## H. Regulate lobbying and political access

Publish meetings, clients, documents submitted and policy targets.

## I. Reduce abuse of institutional advertising

Allocation should follow published audience and public-interest criteria, with outlet-level disclosure.

## J. Strengthen local journalism

Corruption risk is frequently highest where scrutiny is weakest. Public support for journalism should be distributed by neutral rules, never government discretion over friendly outlets.

# 23. The behaviour parties should stop rewarding

Parties currently benefit from behaviours that harm the system:

- promoting the politician who creates the most viral confrontation;
- protecting a useful official until evidence becomes overwhelming;
- describing every concession as victory and every compromise as surrender;
- attacking institutions only when they rule against the party;
- refusing to acknowledge valid evidence from ideological opponents;
- placing loyal communicators above competent administrators;
- using public appointments to settle internal balances;
- promising policies without costs or implementation plans.

The reform cannot be only legal.

Voters, members, donors and media must stop rewarding these incentives.

---

# 24. What citizens should demand from every party

Before accepting a proposal, ask:

1. What is the measurable objective?
2. What does it cost?
3. Who pays?
4. Which law or administration delivers it?
5. How long will it take?
6. What evidence supports it?
7. What trade-off is being concealed?
8. What happens if it fails?
9. Will the party apply the same rule to itself?
10. Can the results be independently verified?

Most political propaganda becomes weaker once forced through these questions.

# 25. The crucial comparison: what each bloc refuses to admit

## The governing left refuses to admit

- immigration can create local costs;
- regulation without supply can fail;
- institutional reversals damage credibility;
- political responsibility does not require a criminal conviction;
- public spending must be evaluated for productivity and execution.

## The right refuses to admit

- immigration contributes substantially to growth and employment;
- tax cuts require spending choices;
- territorial pluralism cannot simply be abolished;
- corruption within PP administrations was systemic in important cases;
- governing with Vox would create real constitutional and European conflicts.

## The nationalist parties refuse to admit

- bilateral leverage can produce unequal national treatment;
- identity does not automatically justify every fiscal or institutional demand;
- self-government also requires accountability for local failures.

## The radical left refuses to admit

- markets cannot be replaced by decree;
- administrative capacity limits policy;
- internal democracy matters as much as ideological purity;
- coalition politics requires durable compromise.

## Vox refuses to admit

- cultural conflict is not an implementation plan;
- foreign nationality does not explain social problems by itself;
- recentralisation would produce enormous legal and political disruption;
- protecting national cohesion also requires protecting plural citizens.

# Final verdict

Spain remains a functioning democracy.

Its crisis is not that elections have ceased to matter. It is that too many citizens believe political actors use democratic institutions primarily to preserve their own side’s power.

The current system produces:

- governments capable of surviving but not always of planning;
- oppositions capable of blocking but not always of presenting viable alternatives;
- parties capable of mobilising identity but weak at admitting trade-offs;
- courts capable of punishing corruption but often years too late;
- journalism capable of exposing scandals but divided into partisan audiences;
- citizens who continue voting while expecting little integrity from those elected.

The evidence does not support the claim that all parties are equally corrupt or equally harmful.

PP carries the heaviest final judicial legacy at national level through Gürtel and related networks. PSOE carries the ERE legacy and present political damage from investigations involving former senior figures. CDC’s Palau financing mechanism was established by final judgment and remains politically relevant to the Junts tradition. The PNV has the De Miguel case. Newer or less frequently governing parties have fewer comparable final cases, but also less historical exposure to large contracting systems.

Corruption is only one part of the wider failure.

The most damaging political behaviour is **selective morality**:

- presumption of innocence for allies, instant guilt for opponents;
- respect for judges when they agree, institutional attack when they do not;
- outrage at the other party’s patronage, silence about one’s own;
- demands for transparency from government, opacity in internal party decisions.

Spain does not need a supposedly neutral technocracy without politics.

It needs better politics:

- parties with internal counterweights;
- leaders evaluated by results and truthfulness;
- transparent coalition agreements;
- independent appointments;
- traceable contracts;
- faster judicial proceedings;
- open legislative negotiation;
- common standards of responsibility.

The fundamental democratic test is simple:

> **Would each party still defend the same institutional rule if its greatest opponent controlled the government?**

In contemporary Spain, the answer is too often no.

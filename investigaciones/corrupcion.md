# Corruption and fraud in Spain: the scandal is visible, but the system behind it is harder to see

### Spain is not a country where citizens normally pay bribes to receive basic services. Its central corruption problem lies elsewhere: political access, public contracts, appointments, subsidies, urban planning and networks that convert institutional power into private advantage.

## The central finding

Spain’s corruption problem is not best described as universal bribery or a permanently criminal political class.

It is a **system of recurring opportunity**.

Political parties control or influence thousands of administrations, public companies, foundations and appointments. Those institutions distribute contracts, grants, permits, land-use decisions and regulatory advantages. Oversight is fragmented, investigations are slow and political responsibility is often postponed until a final judgment arrives—sometimes a decade after the events.

The result is a model with four characteristics:

1. Most public officials are not corrupt, but a relatively small network can control very large amounts of public money.
2. Corruption frequently hides inside formally legal administrative procedures rather than a simple envelope of cash.
3. Detection often depends on insiders, police investigations, tax evidence or rival political networks—not routine preventive controls.
4. The judicial system eventually secures convictions in many prosecuted cases, but often too slowly to recover money, prevent repetition or establish timely political accountability.

Spain’s deeper weakness is therefore not that every institution is captured. It is that **the probability of early detection remains too low and the interval between suspicion, trial and consequence remains too long**.

---

# 1. Spain is not among Europe’s cleanest democracies

Transparency International’s 2025 Corruption Perceptions Index gave Spain **55 points out of 100**, ranking it 49th among 182 countries. Spain’s score has fallen ten points since 2012, one of the largest long-term deteriorations in Western Europe. The index measures expert and business perceptions of public-sector corruption; it does not count proven crimes directly. 

This places Spain in an uncomfortable intermediate position:

- It has professional public administrations, independent courts and functioning investigative institutions.
- It does not generally resemble a state where routine services require personal bribes.
- But it performs clearly worse than the most trusted northern and western European systems.
- Repeated scandals have damaged confidence that political connections, contracts and appointments are governed by equal rules.

The European Commission’s 2025 rule-of-law assessment continued to identify corruption risks around public procurement, conflicts of interest, lobbying, institutional integrity and the duration of investigations. 

The correct description is not “Spain is completely corrupt.”

It is:

> **Spain possesses institutions capable of prosecuting corruption, but not yet a preventive system capable of making corruption unusually difficult.**

---

# 2. Corruption is not the same as fraud

The two overlap, but the distinction matters.

## Political corruption

Political corruption involves the abuse of entrusted public power for private, partisan or network advantage. It may include:

- Bribery.
- Embezzlement.
- Influence peddling.
- Prevarication or deliberately unlawful administrative decisions.
- Manipulated public contracts.
- Illegal party financing.
- Favouritism in grants or appointments.
- Trading regulatory access for future employment.
- Concealing assets or conflicts of interest.

## Fraud

Fraud is broader. It includes deception used to obtain money or avoid obligations, whether or not a politician is involved:

- Tax fraud.
- VAT carousel schemes.
- Social-security fraud.
- Subsidy fraud.
- False invoicing.
- Procurement fraud.
- Insurance fraud.
- Corporate accounting fraud.
- Misuse of EU funds.
- Consumer and investment scams.

A business network can defraud the Treasury without political assistance. A politician can abuse appointments without personally stealing money. A procurement conspiracy may combine both.

Conflating every form of fraud with political corruption inflates partisan accusations. Separating them completely hides the way private fraud networks often depend on public permissions, contracts or protection.

---

# 3. Court statistics capture only the visible end of the process

The General Council of the Judiciary’s corruption repository does not count every allegation or preliminary investigation. It records cases that have reached an advanced procedural stage, such as an order opening oral trial or formal processing, along with final convictions and imprisonment data. 

During 2025, Spanish courts completed the investigative phase in **25 corruption proceedings**, sending **114 natural persons and 13 legal entities** toward trial. Courts issued 71 judgments in corruption-related proceedings during the year. 

Between April and September 2025, 27 of 34 corruption judgments were wholly or partly condemnatory—a conviction outcome in approximately four out of five cases during that six-month period. 

These numbers prove that Spain does investigate and convict corruption. But they cannot tell us the true incidence of corruption because:

- Undetected conduct never enters the statistics.
- Complaints closed at an early stage are excluded.
- A large scheme may produce one proceeding but involve dozens of contracts.
- One minor offence and one network controlling millions may each appear as a single case.
- Cases may take many years to mature.

A low annual case count can mean little corruption, poor detection or extremely slow proceedings. The statistic cannot distinguish among them on its own.

---

# 4. Public procurement is the main structural risk

Government purchasing is where political discretion and private money meet.

Spanish public bodies buy construction, transport, healthcare, software, consulting, cleaning, security, communications, social services and emergency supplies. The contracts range from small municipal awards to infrastructure worth hundreds of millions.

Procurement corruption rarely looks like a politician simply taking cash in exchange for one contract. It can operate through:

- Specifications designed for one preferred bidder.
- Artificial division of contracts into amounts below stronger control thresholds.
- Repeated minor contracts.
- Emergency procedures used beyond genuine urgency.
- Deadlines that only an informed bidder can meet.
- Technical scoring that disguises subjective selection.
- Contract modifications that increase the price after award.
- Subcontractors used to transfer commissions.
- False or inflated invoices.
- Bid-rigging among companies.
- Consultants who prepare a tender and later compete for it.
- Political intermediaries presented as ordinary commercial advisers.

The European Commission’s 2025 assessment reported serious business concern about procurement integrity. Survey evidence cited in the report indicated that 40% of Spanish companies believed corruption had prevented them from winning a public tender during the previous three years, compared with an EU average of 25%. This is perception evidence, not proof that 40% of tenders were corrupt, but the gap is a warning about confidence in market access. 

The crucial vulnerability is not merely that contracts are published. It is that the information is often fragmented across platforms, difficult to compare and weakly connected to data on:

- Company ownership.
- Political donations.
- Administrators and family relationships.
- Previous sanctions.
- Contract modifications.
- Subcontractors.
- Final execution costs.
- Officials who evaluated the bid.

Transparency without usable linkage can produce millions of documents and very little accountability.

---

# 5. The small contract is politically useful precisely because it looks small

A large tender attracts legal teams, competitors, media attention and multiple controls.

A small contract may be approved rapidly, with limited competition and little public scrutiny. One €12,000 consultancy is not a national scandal. Hundreds of similar awards concentrated among politically connected suppliers can become a patronage system.

Artificial fragmentation can transform what should have been one competitive procurement into a series of minor contracts.

Warning signs include:

- The same supplier repeatedly receiving awards just below a threshold.
- Multiple contracts with nearly identical descriptions.
- Awards clustered near the end of a budget year.
- Newly created companies rapidly obtaining public work.
- Suppliers with minimal employees or previous activity.
- Contracts split among related companies.
- Identical text across supposedly independent offers.
- A sequence of emergency contracts after the emergency has passed.

The anti-corruption challenge is therefore not simply to publish contracts. It is to detect patterns across administrations and time.

---

# 6. Emergency procurement reveals an enduring tension

Emergencies require speed. The ordinary tendering process may be too slow during a pandemic, natural disaster, infrastructure failure or security crisis.

But emergency procurement removes or reduces precisely the safeguards designed to prevent favouritism:

- Open competition.
- Longer publication periods.
- Detailed prior justification.
- Multiple bids.
- Normal appeals.
- Extensive preliminary oversight.

The question is not whether emergency contracting should exist. It must.

The question is whether every emergency contract receives stronger retrospective scrutiny:

1. Why was the emergency procedure required?
2. How was the supplier identified?
3. Was its beneficial owner checked?
4. Was the price compared with available market evidence?
5. Was delivery verified?
6. Were intermediaries paid?
7. Did the supplier later receive ordinary contracts?
8. Were modifications or extensions added?

Spain frequently debates emergency corruption only after police or journalists uncover suspicious intermediaries. A serious system would examine every high-value emergency award automatically.

---

# 7. Political parties create both the incentive and the protection network

Political corruption is not limited to personal enrichment.

A politician may direct money toward a party, an election campaign, allied media, friendly associations or a patronage network rather than into a personal bank account. The benefit is still political and can be corrupt even where private consumption is not the objective.

Parties need:

- Campaign resources.
- Staff.
- Offices.
- Advertising.
- Events.
- Consultants.
- Local organisational networks.
- Access to sympathetic media.
- Rewards for loyal intermediaries.

Legal public subsidies and private donations finance much of this activity. But historical corruption cases have repeatedly shown the temptation to create parallel financing through commissions, false invoices or favourable contracting.

The institutional problem is that the party may benefit collectively while criminal responsibility must be proved against identifiable individuals.

This creates a political defence:

> “There is no evidence that the leader personally knew.”

That may be legally relevant. It is not always an adequate explanation of organisational responsibility.

A party that repeatedly benefits from corrupt networks should be judged by:

- Its internal controls.
- Whether warnings were ignored.
- Whether implicated officials were protected.
- Whether accounting systems detected irregularities.
- Whether the party returned improper gains.
- Whether it reformed candidate selection and contracting.
- Whether responsibility was accepted before a final criminal judgment.

---

# 8. Spain confuses criminal responsibility with political responsibility

Criminal law asks whether a specific offence has been proved beyond reasonable doubt against a specific defendant.

Political responsibility asks whether a public official exercised judgment, oversight and integrity compatible with office.

These standards should not be identical.

A minister might not have committed a crime but may have:

- Appointed an unsuitable person.
- Ignored repeated warnings.
- Failed to control a public company.
- Misled parliament.
- Allowed conflicts of interest.
- Benefited politically from a corrupt structure.
- Obstructed disclosure.

Conversely, an accusation or judicial investigation does not prove guilt. Resignation cannot be demanded mechanically for every unsupported complaint, because political opponents could remove officials by filing accusations.

Spain needs a clearer ladder of responsibility:

- **Allegation:** a claim exists.
- **Preliminary inquiry:** authorities are checking whether sufficient basis exists.
- **Formal investigation:** a court or prosecutor is investigating defined conduct.
- **Opening of trial:** the case has passed an important evidentiary threshold.
- **Conviction subject to appeal:** a court has found guilt, but the judgment is not final.
- **Final conviction:** ordinary appeals are exhausted.
- **Political responsibility:** may arise at any stage based on facts that are not themselves criminal.

Public debate frequently collapses these categories. Allies call every case a fabrication until final conviction. Opponents describe every investigative step as proof.

Both practices damage the presumption of innocence and democratic accountability.

---

# 9. Local government is a recurrent risk zone

Municipalities decide matters with enormous private value:

- Where homes can be built.
- Whether rural land becomes developable.
- Construction density.
- Licences.
- Public works.
- Waste collection.
- Water concessions.
- Parking.
- Festivals.
- Sports facilities.
- Social-service contracts.
- Local grants.

A planning decision can multiply the value of land without any money passing through the public budget.

That makes urban planning especially vulnerable to:

- Bribes.
- Preferential information.
- Hidden ownership.
- Family intermediaries.
- Vote trading within municipal councils.
- Agreements exchanging public decisions for private benefits.
- Manipulated valuations.
- Public purchase of land at inflated prices.
- Rezoning that rewards donors or allies.

Smaller municipalities may have fewer professional staff, less internal segregation of duties and less investigative journalism. The mayor, contracting authority, planning network and local party organisation may overlap closely.

Decentralisation improves responsiveness but multiplies the number of corruption opportunities. Spain’s anti-corruption capacity must therefore be distributed as widely as its spending authority.

---

# 10. Patronage can be legal and still corrupt democratic competition

Not every abuse of public power fits neatly into a criminal offence.

Governments possess discretion over:

- Senior appointments.
- Advisers.
- Public-company boards.
- Foundations.
- Regulatory positions.
- Institutional advertising.
- Grants.
- Cultural sponsorship.
- Public media leadership.
- Consulting contracts.

Some political appointment is normal in a democracy. Elected governments need people who share their programme.

The problem begins when loyalty replaces competence throughout the administrative chain or when public resources are used to strengthen a party ecosystem.

This can occur without an envelope, false invoice or explicit quid pro quo.

Examples include:

- Appointing unqualified loyalists to supervise major budgets.
- Directing institutional advertising toward friendly outlets.
- Financing associations that function as partisan infrastructure.
- Creating advisory positions without a real operational need.
- Using public companies to bypass normal staffing constraints.
- Rewarding former officials through boards or consultancy work.
- Withholding access from critical media or civil-society organisations.

The legal system is poorly suited to evaluate this grey zone. Yet it may cause more cumulative institutional damage than some conventional bribery cases.

---

# 11. Revolving doors convert future opportunity into present influence

A public official does not need to receive money while in office to face a conflict of interest.

The expectation of future private employment can influence:

- Regulatory decisions.
- Public contracts.
- Enforcement intensity.
- Concessions.
- Tax settlements.
- Legislative design.
- Access to confidential information.

Cooling-off periods exist, but their effectiveness depends on:

- Which officials are covered.
- How broadly related activities are defined.
- Whether consultancy is included.
- Whether indirect employment through law firms or associations is captured.
- Whether declarations are verified.
- Whether sanctions are meaningful.
- Whether decisions are published promptly.

The opposite movement also matters: executives entering government may retain assets, professional loyalties or relationships with former clients.

A declaration of interests is not prevention unless somebody checks it against company, property and contract records.

---

# 12. Lobbying remains less transparent than its influence

Lobbying is not inherently corrupt.

Businesses, trade unions, environmental groups, professional associations, regions and charities all need to explain how policy affects them. Legislators benefit from expertise.

Corruption risk arises when access is unequal and invisible:

- Some actors meet decision-makers repeatedly while others cannot.
- Draft texts originate with interested parties without disclosure.
- Lobbyists act through former officials or family members.
- Consultancies conceal the clients whose interests they represent.
- Meetings occur outside formal diaries.
- Amendments are introduced without an identifiable author.
- Political donations overlap with regulatory requests.

The European Commission has repeatedly recommended stronger rules on lobbying and conflicts of interest in Spain. 

A credible lobbying register must show more than a name. It should disclose:

- The ultimate client.
- The policy or contract sought.
- The institution contacted.
- Meetings and written submissions.
- Money spent or fee range.
- Officials involved.
- Draft language provided.
- Previous public positions of lobbyists.

---

# 13. Institutional advertising can become invisible party financing

Governments legitimately need to communicate:

- Public-health information.
- Tax deadlines.
- Elections.
- Transport changes.
- Benefits.
- Emergency alerts.
- Public services.

But institutional advertising also transfers money to media organisations whose coverage affects those same governments.

Risk increases when:

- Allocation criteria are not published.
- Audience measurement is selectively interpreted.
- Friendly outlets receive disproportionate spending.
- Campaigns promote political achievements rather than public information.
- Spending rises before elections.
- Contracts are channelled through agencies that conceal the final beneficiaries.
- Digital advertising is microtargeted without adequate disclosure.

This is not only a media problem. It is an anti-corruption issue because public money can purchase a more favourable information environment without appearing as party expenditure.

Publication should include each outlet, campaign, amount, selection criterion, impressions delivered and intermediary commission.

---

# 14. Spain’s enforcement institutions are real, but overloaded

Spain has specialised prosecutors, police units, tax inspectors, auditors, courts, regional anti-fraud bodies and the European Public Prosecutor’s Office.

The Anti-Corruption Prosecutor’s Office reported involvement in **915 judicial proceedings during 2024**, up from 885 in 2023 and 841 in 2022. It processed 54 pre-procedural investigative files during the year and filed 42 indictments, compared with 35 the previous year. 

The office itself has called for additional accounting and financial expertise. Complex economic crime increasingly requires tracing corporate structures, invoices, tax flows, digital evidence and international transactions. 

This creates an asymmetry:

- The suspected network may have lawyers, accountants, consultants and years to design transactions.
- Investigators must reconstruct those transactions after the fact.
- Courts must process millions of documents under procedural rules designed partly for simpler crimes.
- Defendants can legitimately appeal—but delay can become a strategy.

The prosecutor’s 2025 report described procedural delay as damaging both effectiveness and the credibility of justice. It warned that long proceedings allow time to conceal criminal proceeds, increase prescription risks, encourage repeated mitigation for undue delays and expose defendants to years of unresolved public accusation. 

Delay harms both sides: it protects the sophisticated guilty and punishes the innocent through prolonged suspicion.

---

# 15. Macro-cases can make justice less effective

Large corruption investigations are often accumulated into one enormous proceeding because contracts, politicians, companies and intermediaries are connected.

This has advantages:

- The entire network becomes visible.
- Evidence can be understood in context.
- Artificial separation cannot hide the organisation.
- Prosecutors can demonstrate a common scheme.

But macro-cases create severe problems:

- Hundreds of defendants and witnesses.
- Millions of documents.
- Multiple appeals.
- Repeated procedural disputes.
- Illness, death or retirement before trial.
- Prescription problems.
- Public confusion between branches of the case.
- Difficulty separating strong evidence from weak peripheral accusations.

A better strategy may divide cases into manageable trial units while preserving a central investigative map of the network.

Justice that produces one monumental judgment fifteen years later may be less preventive than several focused judgments delivered earlier.

---

# 16. European money creates new opportunities and new oversight

Spain is one of the major recipients of EU expenditure, including agricultural funds, cohesion funds and the Recovery and Resilience Facility.

The European Public Prosecutor’s Office investigates crimes affecting the EU’s financial interests.

At the end of 2025, EPPO reported **97 active cases in Spain** involving estimated damage of **€796.2 million**. These included expenditure fraud, procurement fraud, misappropriation, money laundering, corruption and VAT or customs fraud. Twenty-one active Spanish cases involved recovery and resilience programmes. 

EPPO opened 57 Spanish cases during 2025 from reports and complaints, with estimated damage of €414.15 million. It reported eight final court decisions and eleven convicted persons in Spain during the year. 

These are estimated damages in investigations, not amounts finally proven stolen. The distinction is essential.

The figures nevertheless show that EU-fund fraud is not a theoretical concern. The combination of:

- Large spending volumes.
- Political pressure for rapid execution.
- Complex eligibility rules.
- Multiple administrative levels.
- Private consultants.
- Subcontracting.
- Weak beneficial-ownership visibility.

creates a high-risk environment.

---

# 17. Tax fraud is economically larger than conventional political bribery

Public attention focuses on politicians because public trust is involved. But large-scale tax fraud can remove far more money from public budgets.

Important forms include:

- Undeclared income.
- False deductions.
- Shell companies.
- Hidden offshore ownership.
- VAT carousel fraud.
- Customs fraud.
- Fictitious invoicing.
- False residence.
- Aggressive arrangements crossing into criminal evasion.
- Cash-based undeclared business activity.

At EU level, EPPO reported that VAT and customs fraud accounted for more than two-thirds of the estimated damage in its active investigations at the end of 2025. 

This type of fraud can involve organised crime, money laundering and political protection, but often does not.

The public response is asymmetrical:

- A corrupt mayor taking €100,000 becomes a symbol of national decay.
- A sophisticated VAT network stealing tens of millions may receive less sustained attention because its mechanism is harder to explain.

Both deserve investigation. Political corruption is especially corrosive to legitimacy; tax fraud may be larger fiscally.

---

# 18. Welfare fraud exists, but is often used to divert attention

Fraud involving unemployment benefits, pensions, social assistance or false registration should be investigated and recovered.

However, political rhetoric often treats small recipient fraud as the primary moral threat while giving less attention to:

- Payroll fraud by employers.
- Undeclared overtime.
- False self-employment.
- Corporate subsidy fraud.
- Public procurement overbilling.
- Tax evasion.
- Misuse of insolvency procedures.
- Fraudulent contracting chains.

This is partly because individual benefit fraud is easy to visualise. Complex corporate and political fraud requires forensic accounting.

An anti-fraud strategy should rank enforcement by:

1. Expected financial damage.
2. Organised character.
3. Repetition.
4. Abuse of public power.
5. Effect on vulnerable people.
6. Deterrence value.

It should not allocate attention according to which offender is easiest to stigmatise.

---

# 19. Corruption does not only steal money—it changes what the state builds

The cost of corruption is not equal to the bribe or commission.

Suppose a company pays a €1 million commission to obtain a €50 million project.

The damage may include:

- The commission.
- An inflated contract price.
- Inferior materials.
- Future maintenance.
- A project chosen over a better alternative.
- Reduced competition.
- Honest firms leaving the market.
- Political dependence on the contractor.
- Public distrust.
- Opportunity costs.

A corrupt road may be built where a health centre was needed. A waste contract may lock a city into higher prices for twenty years. A manipulated rezoning decision may shape housing affordability for a generation.

The true social cost is therefore often impossible to recover through confiscating the direct payment.

---

# 20. Why corruption survives despite repeated scandals

## The reward can be immediate

The contract, campaign support or planning gain arrives now.

## Detection is uncertain

Many schemes look administratively normal unless data are connected or an insider speaks.

## Proceedings are slow

Even a strong case may take years to reach final judgment.

## Political polarisation offers protection

Supporters interpret investigations as attacks by the opposing bloc.

## Responsibility is fragmented

The politician blames the official. The official blames the technical report. The contractor blames the intermediary. The party denies institutional knowledge.

## Money becomes difficult to trace

Funds pass through invoices, consultancies, related companies, cash, foreign accounts or property.

## The scandal becomes old before the judgment

By the time guilt is established, the government may have changed, the accused may have retired and public attention may have moved elsewhere.

This produces a rational calculation for corrupt actors: the potential gain is concentrated and immediate; the risk is delayed and uncertain.

---

# 21. The role of political polarisation

Corruption in Spain is processed through party identity.

When a case affects an opposing party:

- Investigative leaks are treated as proof.
- Resignation is demanded immediately.
- Organisational guilt is generalised to the whole party.
- Every procedural development receives attention.

When it affects one’s own party:

- The investigation is described as politically motivated.
- Responsibility is reduced to one individual.
- Final conviction becomes the only acceptable threshold.
- Judicial errors in unrelated cases are used to discredit the process.
- Media coverage is portrayed as a conspiracy.

This does not mean every accusation is valid or every investigation neutral. Institutions can make mistakes, investigations can be leaked selectively and criminal complaints can be weaponised.

But symmetrical scepticism is required.

The same evidentiary and political standards should apply whether the accused belongs to PSOE, PP, Vox, Sumar, Podemos, a nationalist party or a local coalition.

Without that discipline, anti-corruption becomes another instrument of partisan mobilisation rather than institutional reform.

---

# 22. What historical scandals actually demonstrate

Spain’s major corruption cases differ in facts and legal outcomes. They should not be merged into one narrative.

But collectively they reveal recurring patterns:

- Networks connecting politicians, contractors and intermediaries.
- Public contracts exchanged for commissions or political benefit.
- Parallel accounting or hidden party financing.
- Municipal planning decisions creating private fortunes.
- Public grants distributed through weak or manipulated controls.
- Public companies used as lower-visibility spending vehicles.
- Long intervals between events and final judgments.
- Legal disputes over whether organisational leadership knew or benefited.

The lesson is not that one political party invented corruption.

It is that **similar opportunity structures recur under different parties and administrations**.

A reform designed only to punish the current adversary will fail when power changes hands.

---

# 23. What the current scandal cycle cannot yet prove

Spain regularly has politically explosive investigations involving ministers, regional officials, party organisers, relatives, advisers, contractors or public companies.

A serious investigation must keep four categories separate:

### Proven fact

A document, payment, contract, recording or admitted action is authenticated.

### Investigative hypothesis

Police, prosecutors or judges believe facts may constitute an offence and are gathering evidence.

### Prosecution claim

An indictment formally alleges criminal conduct.

### Judicial finding

A court has evaluated the evidence and issued a judgment.

Current allegations should be reported with exact procedural language.

“Investigated,” “charged,” “sent to trial,” “convicted” and “finally convicted” do not mean the same thing.

This is especially important in political corruption because reputational punishment arrives long before a trial.

# 24. The anti-corruption institutions themselves need independence

Enforcement credibility depends not only on formal legal powers but on appointment systems, resources and perceived neutrality.

Relevant bodies include:

- Judges and courts.
- Public prosecutors.
- Guardia Civil and Policía Nacional economic-crime units.
- Tax inspectors.
- The Court of Auditors.
- The Independent Office for Regulation and Supervision of Procurement.
- Regional anti-fraud offices.
- Competition authorities.
- Internal auditors.
- Transparency councils.
- The European Public Prosecutor’s Office.

Potential vulnerabilities include:

- Political appointments.
- Temporary leadership.
- Budget dependence.
- Insufficient specialist staff.
- Selective leaks.
- Weak coordination.
- Limited data access.
- Delayed publication.
- Institutional disputes over jurisdiction.

Independence does not mean absence of accountability. Investigators and prosecutors also need transparent appointment criteria, review mechanisms and published performance data.

---

# 25. The Court of Auditors is necessary but too slow for prevention

External audit often discovers problems after the money has been spent and the political cycle has ended.

Traditional auditing asks whether:

- Procedures were followed.
- Accounts reconcile.
- Expenditure was authorised.
- Documentation exists.

Corruption detection requires additional questions:

- Did connected suppliers repeatedly win?
- Was competition genuine?
- Did prices deviate from comparable contracts?
- Were contracts systematically modified?
- Did a supplier’s ownership change before the award?
- Did public officials have undeclared relationships?
- Did grant beneficiaries share addresses, directors or consultants?
- Did political donors later receive advantages?

Auditing must move from document compliance toward network and pattern analysis.

---

# 26. Whistleblowers are essential because corruption is designed to look legal

Outsiders may see only a valid contract, signed report and approved invoice.

An insider may know:

- The competition was simulated.
- The successful bidder received specifications in advance.
- Work was never delivered.
- A superior ordered the score to be changed.
- An intermediary was included solely to receive a commission.
- Documents were backdated.
- A conflict of interest was concealed.
- Complaints were suppressed.

Spain has incorporated protections associated with the EU whistleblower framework, but formal protection does not eliminate practical risk.

Whistleblowers may face:

- Career stagnation.
- Isolation.
- Defamation suits.
- Disciplinary action.
- Transfer.
- Exposure of identity.
- Years of litigation.
- Political attacks.

A reporting channel is not credible unless retaliation produces rapid consequences and the whistleblower receives legal and financial support.

---

# 27. Journalists are part of the enforcement system

Many corruption cases begin or develop through investigative reporting.

Journalists can connect:

- Contracts.
- Company registries.
- Property.
- Party relationships.
- Family ties.
- Leaked communications.
- Court files.
- Procurement patterns.
- Political donations.

But the media also face conflicts:

- Institutional advertising.
- Ownership concentration.
- Bank or corporate debt.
- Political access.
- Selective leaks.
- Competition for dramatic exclusives.
- Defamation litigation.
- Ideological alignment.

A leaked police report can reveal wrongdoing. It can also present an untested investigative theory as established fact.

The solution is not less reporting. It is stronger evidentiary labelling, document publication, source diversity and corrections when judicial findings change the story.

---

# 28. Technology can detect corruption—but only if the data are designed for it

Spain publishes substantial public information. The problem is interoperability.

An effective system would connect:

- Every public contract.
- Tender participants.
- Evaluation scores.
- Contract modifications.
- Actual payments.
- Subcontractors.
- Beneficial owners.
- Company directors.
- Grants.
- Political donations.
- Public appointments.
- Asset and interest declarations.
- Lobbying meetings.
- Sanctions.
- Court judgments.

Automated risk indicators could flag:

- Single-bid tenders.
- Repeated threshold-adjacent contracts.
- Abnormally short bidding windows.
- Suppliers created shortly before awards.
- High modification rates.
- Concentration among a few companies.
- Shared directors or addresses.
- Repeated emergency awards.
- Officials associated with winning firms.
- Prices diverging from comparable purchases.

An algorithm should not declare corruption. It should prioritise contracts for human audit.

The greatest technical obstacle is not artificial intelligence. It is missing, inconsistent and non-standardised data.

# 29. Testing the popular claims

## “All politicians are corrupt”

Spain has hundreds of thousands of elected officials, political employees and public servants. Court cases involve a small minority.

**Verdict:** factually false and strategically harmful. It discourages distinctions between clean and corrupt conduct.

## “Corruption is only a problem of the other party”

Major cases have affected administrations of different ideologies and territorial levels.

**Verdict:** corruption follows opportunity, weak controls and impunity more reliably than ideology.

## “If no final conviction exists, nothing happened”

Criminal guilt requires a final legal process. Political failures and documented conflicts can exist without criminal conviction.

**Verdict:** correct as a defence against criminal labelling; inadequate as the sole standard of political responsibility.

## “Being investigated proves guilt”

Investigations test suspicions. They can end without prosecution or conviction.

**Verdict:** false and incompatible with the presumption of innocence.

## “Transparency portals solved the problem”

Publishing thousands of PDFs does not reveal hidden relationships or suspicious patterns.

**Verdict:** transparency is necessary, but usable structured data and enforcement are decisive.

## “Corruption costs Spain a precise number of billions each year”

Many frequently repeated figures combine perception surveys, procurement inefficiency and modelled economic effects. They do not measure proven theft.

**Verdict:** the cost is unquestionably larger than direct bribes, but a single exact national figure is usually misleading.

## “More severe prison sentences will stop corruption”

Punishment matters, but actors respond strongly to the probability and speed of detection.

**Verdict:** faster detection, confiscation and disqualification may deter more effectively than symbolic maximum penalties imposed rarely and late.

## “Private companies corrupt innocent politicians”

Corruption often requires both public and private actors. Businesses can initiate schemes, but officials control public decisions.

**Verdict:** responsibility belongs on both sides of the transaction.

# 30. What a serious anti-corruption programme would look like

## A. Make every euro traceable

For every contract and grant, publish:

- Initial budget.
- Bidders.
- Scores.
- Decision-makers.
- Beneficial owners.
- Subcontractors.
- Modifications.
- Invoices.
- Final payment.
- Execution assessment.

All data should use common identifiers and machine-readable formats.

## B. Create automatic procurement risk scoring

Every contracting platform should flag:

- One-bid awards.
- Repeated minor contracts.
- Urgent procedures.
- Short deadlines.
- High price modifications.
- Supplier concentration.
- Politically exposed beneficial owners.
- Shared ownership among bidders.

High-risk does not mean corrupt. It means mandatory review.

## C. Strengthen independent procurement oversight

The procurement supervisory authority needs:

- Sufficient auditors and data specialists.
- Direct database access.
- Power to require explanations.
- Published follow-up.
- Referral protocols with prosecutors, tax authorities and competition bodies.

## D. Verify asset and interest declarations

Declarations should be checked, not merely stored.

Verification should cover:

- Companies.
- Property.
- Securities.
- Spouse or partner interests where legally justified.
- Gifts.
- Outside employment.
- Significant liabilities.
- Changes during and after office.

## E. Regulate lobbying transparently

Require disclosure of:

- Clients.
- Meetings.
- Legislative proposals.
- Documents submitted.
- Spending bands.
- Former public positions.
- Decisions targeted.

## F. Limit patronage appointments

Public-company and regulatory leadership should use:

- Published competence criteria.
- Independent assessment.
- Parliamentary scrutiny for major posts.
- Fixed incompatibility rules.
- Detailed reasons for appointment.

Political confidence may remain relevant, but it should not substitute for minimum qualifications.

## G. Accelerate corruption trials

Create specialised procedural capacity through:

- More judges and clerks.
- Financial experts.
- Digital evidence systems.
- Early division of macro-cases.
- Strict management of avoidable delays.
- Faster interlocutory appeals.
- Dedicated trial calendars.

Speed must not remove defence rights.

## H. Recover money earlier

Use proportionate precautionary measures to prevent dissipation of assets:

- Freezing.
- Bonds.
- Property annotations.
- Tracing of beneficial ownership.
- International cooperation.
- Extended confiscation where legally justified.

Acquitted defendants must receive prompt restoration.

## I. Protect whistleblowers in practice

Provide:

- Anonymous and confidential channels.
- Independent investigation.
- Interim protection against retaliation.
- Legal assistance.
- Compensation.
- Penalties for identifying or punishing reporters.
- Public reporting on outcomes.

## J. Make political parties organisationally responsible

Require:

- Strong internal compliance.
- Supplier due diligence.
- Consolidated accounts for territorial branches.
- Disclosure of campaign service providers.
- Independent audit.
- Repayment of illicit benefits.
- Sanctions that affect the organisation, not only expendable individuals.

---

# 31. What success should be measured by

An anti-corruption plan should not be evaluated by how many laws are passed or how many arrests appear on television.

Useful indicators include:

- Percentage of contracts with more than one bidder.
- Share of minor and emergency contracting.
- Time from complaint to decision.
- Time from opening of trial to judgment.
- Amount frozen and recovered.
- Number of contract modifications.
- Publication completeness.
- Whistleblower retaliation cases resolved.
- Conflicts detected before decisions.
- Lobbying meetings disclosed.
- Final sanctions for procurement collusion.
- Repeat suppliers associated with irregularities.
- Compliance with audit recommendations.

The aim is not necessarily to reduce reported cases immediately. Better detection may initially increase them.

---

# Final verdict

Spain’s political corruption is not primarily a problem of citizens handing cash to minor officials.

It is a problem of **networks operating at the boundary between politics, administration and business**.

The most valuable currencies are often:

- Access.
- Information.
- Contracts.
- Planning decisions.
- Appointments.
- Regulatory favour.
- Institutional advertising.
- Future employment.
- Party advantage.

Spain’s courts and prosecutors demonstrate that corruption can be punished. During 2025, courts continued sending defendants to trial and issuing a high proportion of condemnatory judgments in decided corruption cases. The Anti-Corruption Prosecutor’s Office and EPPO are handling increasingly complex financial investigations. 

But punishment remains too dependent on scandals discovered after the money has moved.

The structural weakness is not the absence of law. It is the distance between:

- The decision and its audit.
- The contract and its real beneficiaries.
- The allegation and the trial.
- The conviction and recovery.
- Criminal guilt and political accountability.

The decisive reform would make corruption **detectable during the transaction**, not merely prosecutable ten years later.

Spain does not need to assume that every politician is a thief.

It needs to build a system in which even a dishonest politician, official or contractor expects that connected data, professional oversight, protected insiders and rapid justice will expose the scheme before it becomes profitable.

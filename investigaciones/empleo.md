# Employment and unemployment in Spain: record job creation, but structural unemployment remains embedded

### Spain has more people working than ever, registered unemployment has fallen to its lowest level since before the financial crisis, and temporary contracting has declined sharply. Yet the country still combines one of Europe’s highest unemployment rates with youth exclusion, regional inequality, weak job matching and large numbers of workers who remain economically insecure despite holding permanent contracts.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

Spain’s labour market has improved more than the national debate often admits.

Employment has expanded strongly, the number of Social Security contributors has reached historic highs, conventional temporary employment has fallen, and registered unemployment dropped below 2.3 million in June 2026 for the first time since January 2008. 

But Spain has not solved unemployment.

The deeper problem is:

> **Spain creates large numbers of jobs during expansions, but still struggles to connect people, skills, employers and regions efficiently—and continues to destroy employment too aggressively during downturns.**

The labour market contains two realities:

1. **The quantity of employment is improving rapidly.**
2. **The quality, productivity and accessibility of that employment remain highly unequal.**

Spain’s challenge is no longer merely to create any job.

It is to create employment that is:

- stable throughout the year;
- productive enough to support decent wages;
- accessible to young people and the long-term unemployed;
- compatible with housing and family life;
- resilient during the next recession.

---

# 1. The headline numbers show genuine progress

In the first quarter of 2026, Spain had **22.293 million employed people**, 527,600 more than one year earlier. The raw quarterly figure declined by 170,300 because the first quarter is normally weak after the Christmas and tourism season, but seasonally adjusted employment still rose by 0.43%. 

Unemployment stood at **2.709 million people**, with an unemployment rate of **10.83%**. That was higher than the 9.93% recorded in the final quarter of 2025, but quarterly deterioration was partly seasonal. 

Monthly administrative data were more positive by June:

- registered unemployment fell by 28,739 during the month;
- the total declined to **2,291,982**;
- registered unemployment was 113,981 lower than one year earlier;
- female registered unemployment fell below 1.4 million;
- registered youth unemployment reached a new series low of about 159,800. 

The apparent contradiction—2.71 million unemployed in the EPA but 2.29 million registered at SEPE—does not mean one figure is false.

They measure different things.

---

# 2. Spain has two unemployment statistics because they answer different questions

## EPA unemployment

The Labour Force Survey counts people as unemployed when they:

- do not have work;
- are available to work;
- have actively sought employment under internationally harmonised criteria.

It includes people who are not registered with the public employment service.

## Registered unemployment

SEPE counts people registered as jobseekers who meet its administrative classification.

It excludes some groups and may include people whose situation does not precisely match the EPA definition.

The EPA is the best measure for international comparison. Registered unemployment is valuable for:

- benefit administration;
- local monitoring;
- monthly change;
- employment-service workload.

Political actors often choose whichever measure produces the more favourable headline.

A serious investigation should show both and explain the difference.

---

# 3. Spain still has the highest unemployment rate among the largest EU economies

Eurostat estimated Spain’s seasonally adjusted unemployment rate at **10.3% in April 2026**, compared with **6.0% in the EU** and **6.3% in the euro area**. Among the largest EU economies, Spain continued to record the highest rate. 



Spain has therefore improved enormously without converging fully toward European labour-market performance.

That is the core structural puzzle.

Why does a country experiencing strong GDP and employment growth still maintain unemployment near double the EU average?

---

# 4. Spain’s unemployment is not explained by one cause

The persistent gap reflects several mechanisms operating together:

- a production model exposed to tourism, construction, agriculture and seasonal services;
- weak matching between qualifications and vacancies;
- large regional differences;
- insufficient vocational and technical skills in some occupations;
- high school dropout and weak transitions from education to work;
- firms that are too small to train and retain workers effectively;
- high housing costs that prevent geographical mobility;
- administrative fragmentation;
- long-term unemployment that erodes employability;
- a history of using dismissal and temporary contracts as the main adjustment mechanism during crises.

No single reform—lower taxes, stronger benefits, easier dismissal or more training—can solve all these problems.

---

# 5. Spain’s greatest historical weakness is cyclical destruction of jobs

Spain repeatedly experiences a pattern:

1. Employment grows rapidly during expansions.
2. A shock arrives.
3. Companies reduce employment more aggressively than in many European countries.
4. Unemployment rises into double digits or beyond.
5. Recovery requires years of job creation.

This occurred after:

- the early-1990s recession;
- the 2008 financial and construction collapse;
- the euro crisis;
- the pandemic, although the ERTE system prevented a much greater collapse.

Historically, employers responded to downturns by ending temporary contracts rather than reducing hours or reorganising work.

The expansion of temporary employment made hiring flexible but transferred economic shocks disproportionately to workers with the least seniority and bargaining power.

The RED and ERTE mechanisms represent an alternative: preserve the employment relationship while hours or activity fall. That reduces loss of firm-specific skills and allows faster recovery.

A resilient labour market should adjust partly through hours and internal reorganisation—not primarily through mass dismissal.

---

# 6. The labour reform reduced temporary employment substantially

The 2021–2022 labour reform restricted temporary contracting and encouraged permanent contracts, including permanent-discontinuous contracts.

The reform produced a clear compositional change. The Banco de España found that firms that had relied heavily on temporary employment reduced their temporary-contract share by about eight percentage points relative to firms that had used it more moderately. 

In May 2026, permanent contracts represented **43.22% of all contracts registered during the month**, a proportion that would have been highly unusual under the previous labour model. 

This is a real improvement.

A permanent contract normally provides:

- greater legal continuity;
- better access to credit;
- higher dismissal protection;
- stronger eligibility for internal training;
- a more stable employment history.

But the reform changed contract labels more decisively than it changed every worker’s lived security.

---

# 7. Permanent-discontinuous work is both an improvement and a statistical complication

A permanent-discontinuous worker has an ongoing employment relationship but works only during periods when the activity is active.

This model is appropriate for genuinely recurring seasonal work such as:

- tourism;
- education-related services;
- agriculture;
- seasonal production;
- some public or subcontracted services.

It is generally more protective than repeated temporary contracts because the worker remains linked to the employer and may accumulate seniority.

But it can conceal instability when:

- inactivity periods are long;
- expected recalls do not materialise;
- annual hours are insufficient;
- workers do not know when the next activity period begins;
- companies use it where ordinary permanent full-year work would be more appropriate.

The political argument often becomes simplistic.

The government emphasises that these workers are permanently contracted.

Opponents claim that every inactive permanent-discontinuous worker should be counted as unemployed.

Neither statement fully captures economic reality.

A better labour dashboard would report:

- number of permanent-discontinuous workers;
- number currently active;
- average annual days worked;
- median income;
- recall frequency;
- movement into full-year employment;
- benefit use during inactivity.

The key question is not the contractual label.

It is whether the worker obtains predictable annual income.

---

# 8. Contract stability does not equal job quality

A worker can have a permanent contract and still experience:

- low hourly pay;
- involuntary part-time work;
- unpredictable schedules;
- unpaid overtime;
- long commuting time;
- poor promotion prospects;
- unsafe conditions;
- subcontracting;
- dismissal after a short period;
- insufficient annual hours.

The reform successfully reduced one dimension of precariousness: formal temporary contracting.

It did not eliminate the broader insecurity generated by low productivity and weak bargaining power.

Spain should therefore evaluate job quality through:

- annual income;
- hourly wage;
- work schedule predictability;
- training received;
- occupational progression;
- health and safety;
- continuity over several years;
- voluntary versus involuntary part-time status.

---

# 9. Youth unemployment remains Spain’s most visible failure

The European Commission reported that Spain’s youth unemployment rate had fallen to about **23% in 2025**, its lowest level since the global financial crisis, but it remained among the highest in the EU. 

The EU youth unemployment rate was approximately **15.1% in April 2026** and 15.2% in May. 

The Spanish youth rate is not the proportion of all young people who are unemployed.

It is the proportion of young people in the labour force—those working or actively seeking work—who are unemployed. Many students are outside that denominator.

Even with this clarification, the rate indicates a severe transition problem.

Young people often face a sequence of:

1. education;
2. unpaid or weakly paid training;
3. temporary or seasonal work;
4. mismatch between qualification and occupation;
5. delayed stable employment.

This affects more than income.

It delays:

- leaving the parental home;
- family formation;
- pension contributions;
- professional development;
- geographic independence;
- savings and homeownership.

---

# 10. The school-to-work transition is badly designed

Spain produces two forms of mismatch simultaneously:

- employers report shortages in technical and vocational occupations;
- many graduates struggle to find work matching their studies.

The problem is not necessarily that Spain has “too many university graduates.”

It is that educational choices, employer demand and career information are poorly connected.

Students may receive limited information about:

- employment rates by programme;
- wages by qualification;
- regional demand;
- dropout risk;
- alternative vocational pathways;
- actual job content.

Employers may complain about skills shortages while offering:

- low wages;
- unstable schedules;
- no training;
- expensive locations;
- limited progression.

A vacancy is not evidence of a skills shortage if the job conditions are substantially below what suitably qualified workers can obtain elsewhere.

A credible labour-shortage test must ask whether employers have tried:

- raising wages;
- improving schedules;
- training entrants;
- offering relocation support;
- recruiting older workers or women;
- adopting technology.

---

# 11. Vocational education has improved, but social prestige still matters

Dual vocational education can connect students directly to employers and reduce the gap between classroom training and occupational needs.

Spain needs more skilled workers in areas such as:

- construction trades;
- industrial maintenance;
- electrical systems;
- energy installation;
- healthcare support;
- care work;
- logistics;
- cybersecurity;
- software;
- advanced manufacturing.

But vocational education has historically been treated by many families as a second-choice route for students who did not enter university.

That cultural hierarchy is economically damaging.

A high-quality technician in a scarce trade may have better employment and earnings prospects than a graduate in an oversupplied field.

The solution is not to push students away from university indiscriminately. It is to publish reliable outcome data and make vocational routes genuinely high quality.

---

# 12. Employers report vacancies while unemployment remains high

This appears contradictory:

- millions are unemployed;
- firms say they cannot find workers.

But unemployment and labour shortages can coexist when there is mismatch.

A vacancy may be:

- in a different region;
- in an expensive city;
- seasonal;
- poorly paid;
- highly specialised;
- physically demanding;
- incompatible with family responsibilities;
- inaccessible without a car;
- dependent on qualifications that take years to obtain.

A construction company in Madrid cannot automatically hire an unemployed service worker in Cádiz.

The worker may need:

- training;
- housing;
- transport;
- childcare;
- certification;
- information;
- financial support during transition.

Spain often treats labour mobility as an individual responsibility while ignoring the infrastructure required to make it possible.

---

# 13. Housing is now a labour-market constraint

A vacancy does not solve unemployment if the worker cannot afford to live near it.

High rents in Madrid, Barcelona, Málaga, Palma, Valencia and other growing areas reduce the attractiveness of jobs that appear reasonable in gross salary terms.

A person may reject a job not because they prefer unemployment, but because:

- rent would absorb most of the salary;
- relocation costs are unaffordable;
- childcare support would disappear;
- the contract is too uncertain to justify moving;
- the commute would be excessive.

This means housing shortages reduce labour-market efficiency.

Spain may simultaneously have:

- unemployed workers in one province;
- unfilled vacancies in another;
- empty homes in areas with weak employment;
- severe shortages where work is concentrated.

Employment policy cannot be separated from housing and transport.

---

# 14. Regional unemployment remains deeply unequal

Spain does not have one labour market.

It has multiple regional labour markets shaped by:

- industry;
- tourism;
- agriculture;
- public employment;
- population age;
- education;
- urbanisation;
- transport;
- historic development.

Northern and central regions often experience lower unemployment than parts of Andalusia, Extremadura, the Canary Islands and some southern provinces.

Even within a region, a successful capital or tourist area may perform very differently from rural municipalities.

National policies therefore produce unequal results.

A hiring subsidy that is unnecessary in Madrid may be insufficient in a province with weak private investment and limited connectivity.

Regional employment policy should distinguish between:

- cyclical unemployment;
- structural industrial decline;
- rural depopulation;
- seasonal tourism dependence;
- metropolitan skill mismatch.

---

# 15. Female unemployment remains higher

In the first quarter of 2026, the unemployment rate was **12.35% for women** and **9.47% for men**. 

The gap reflects multiple mechanisms:

- care responsibilities;
- greater concentration in part-time work;
- occupational segregation;
- career interruption;
- limited childcare;
- lower mobility;
- discrimination;
- concentration in public and service sectors;
- greater willingness or necessity to enter and leave activity according to family circumstances.

Employment policy frequently treats childcare and dependency care as social policy.

They are also labour-market infrastructure.

A parent cannot accept a job whose schedule is incompatible with school, care or transport.

Affordable childcare can increase employment more effectively than some general hiring subsidies.

---

# 16. Immigration is carrying a large share of employment growth

The European Commission found that foreign migration was a major driver of labour-market growth in 2025. 

Foreign workers fill important roles in:

- hospitality;
- agriculture;
- construction;
- logistics;
- domestic work;
- care;
- healthcare;
- technical and professional occupations.

Immigration allows the economy to expand despite ageing and low fertility.

But the distribution of jobs matters.

If migrants are concentrated in low-pay, unstable sectors, Spain increases employment without raising productivity as much as it could.

Foreign qualifications are also frequently underused.

A labour market that employs trained nurses, engineers or teachers below their skill level wastes human capital.

Recognition of qualifications, language training and bridging programmes are therefore employment policies—not merely integration policies.

---

# 17. Long-term unemployment remains the hardest problem

A person unemployed for a few weeks is in a different position from someone outside work for several years.

Long-term unemployment can produce:

- skill loss;
- employer prejudice;
- weaker professional networks;
- declining confidence;
- health deterioration;
- financial exhaustion;
- dependence on benefits or family;
- lower geographic mobility.

The longer unemployment lasts, the less effective generic job-search requirements become.

A long-term unemployed worker may need:

- skills diagnosis;
- mental and physical health support;
- intensive counselling;
- direct employer contact;
- subsidised transition;
- transport;
- childcare;
- gradual return to work.

Spain’s public employment system often has too many registered jobseekers for genuinely personalised assistance.

---

# 18. Active labour-market policy is still weak relative to the problem

Spain spends money on:

- training;
- hiring subsidies;
- employment programmes;
- regional services;
- digital platforms;
- guidance.

The recurring problem is weak evidence about what works.

Training can become:

- detached from vacancies;
- repeated without employment;
- designed around provider incentives;
- too general;
- outdated;
- poorly evaluated.

Hiring subsidies may reward recruitment that would have happened anyway.

The Banco de España and IMF continue to call for more effective active labour-market policy, better incentives and stronger matching. 

Every programme should be evaluated against a comparable control group where possible:

- did participants obtain work?
- how quickly?
- at what wage?
- for how long?
- would they likely have found work without the programme?
- which provider delivered the best result?

---

# 19. Public employment services should function more like professional matching agencies

A modern employment service should know:

- the worker’s verified skills;
- work history;
- location;
- mobility;
- schedule constraints;
- wage needs;
- training gaps;
- available vacancies;
- probability of successful placement.

Instead, jobseekers often experience:

- administrative registration;
- benefit procedures;
- generic courses;
- limited employer contact;
- poorly matched offers.

Employment services perform essential legal and benefit functions, but placement remains fragmented across SEPE, autonomous communities, private platforms, temporary-work agencies and personal networks.

A single interoperable skills profile—controlled by the worker—could improve matching across the country.

---

# 20. Unemployment benefits are necessary, but design affects re-employment

Unemployment insurance protects workers from immediate income collapse.

It also supports the economy during recessions by maintaining consumption.

A humane system must give people time to find suitable work rather than forcing immediate acceptance of any offer.

But benefits can create weaker return-to-work incentives when:

- withdrawal is abrupt;
- a short job risks losing stable support;
- earnings cannot be combined temporarily with benefits;
- job-search obligations are symbolic;
- public services offer little useful assistance.

The challenge is not simply to reduce benefits.

It is to make returning to work financially and administratively safe.

Possible tools include:

- temporary benefit-and-wage combination;
- gradual withdrawal;
- rapid restoration if a short job fails;
- personalised activation;
- stronger verification of genuine job-search activity;
- protection against unsuitable or abusive offers.

---

# 21. The over-52 unemployment subsidy raises a difficult trade-off

The subsidy for unemployed people over 52 provides income and Social Security contributions until retirement eligibility under defined conditions.

It protects workers who face severe age discrimination and may otherwise reach retirement with insufficient contributions.

But the Banco de España has highlighted very low re-employment rates among beneficiaries and questioned incentive design, particularly because of indefinite duration and contribution arrangements. 

The policy dilemma is real.

A 58-year-old industrial worker displaced after decades may face little realistic demand.

Simply removing support could create poverty without increasing employment.

But passive support without retraining, employer incentives or flexible transition can convert temporary unemployment into permanent labour-market exit.

The programme should combine protection with:

- intensive placement;
- part-time return options;
- training linked to actual vacancies;
- employer-age discrimination enforcement;
- gradual benefit reduction;
- occupational-health assessment.

---

# 22. Youth guarantees and hiring subsidies often address symptoms

Governments frequently respond to youth unemployment through:

- subsidised contracts;
- internships;
- training guarantees;
- reduced contributions;
- public programmes.

These can help individual entrants.

But repeated programmes may leave the underlying system unchanged:

- education remains poorly connected to work;
- employers expect public subsidy for junior hiring;
- internships substitute for ordinary jobs;
- career progression remains weak;
- housing prevents mobility.

The objective should not be to create a temporary first line on a CV.

It should be to create a credible first five years of professional development.

---

# 23. Self-employment is frequently presented as a universal solution

Entrepreneurship can create innovation, independence and employment.

But not every unemployed person should become self-employed.

Some self-employment is economically weak:

- dependence on one client;
- low bargaining power;
- irregular income;
- unpaid administration;
- lack of sick-pay protection;
- platform dependency;
- inability to scale.

Policies encouraging self-employment can artificially reduce unemployment statistics if they create fragile microbusinesses that close quickly.

The right question is not how many people register as self-employed.

It is:

- how many survive three or five years;
- what income they generate;
- whether they hire;
- whether they invest;
- whether they were genuinely independent.

---

# 24. Spain’s small-company structure limits career development

Small businesses generate much of Spain’s employment.

But very small firms often have limited capacity to provide:

- formal training;
- promotion pathways;
- specialist human resources;
- internal mobility;
- high wages;
- advanced technology.

Employees may need to change company to progress.

That increases turnover and reduces employer incentives to train.

Helping productive firms scale can improve employment quality more sustainably than subsidising a permanently fragmented structure.

---

# 25. The public sector is both stabiliser and source of temporary employment

Public employment provides:

- healthcare;
- education;
- security;
- justice;
- administration;
- care.

It also stabilises regional labour markets where private employment is weak.

But public administrations have historically relied heavily on interim and temporary staff.

This creates a contradiction:

The state regulates private-sector stability while sometimes maintaining long-term temporary workers in essential public functions.

Stabilisation processes can reduce abuse, but badly designed mass conversion can conflict with principles of equality, merit and open access.

The underlying solution is workforce planning:

- forecast retirements;
- approve vacancies regularly;
- avoid years of temporary coverage;
- accelerate selection processes;
- coordinate training with service demand.

---

# 26. Spain may face worker shortages before solving unemployment

Ageing will cause large numbers of retirements in:

- healthcare;
- education;
- public administration;
- transport;
- skilled trades;
- industry;
- care.

At the same time, unemployment may remain high among people whose skills, location or experience do not match those vacancies.

This means Spain can have both:

- labour scarcity;
- mass unemployment.

The strategic response requires anticipation rather than waiting for vacancies to become emergencies.

Training systems should publish five- and ten-year replacement needs by occupation and province.

---

# 27. Automation and artificial intelligence will change tasks more than eliminate work uniformly

AI is likely to affect:

- administration;
- customer service;
- translation;
- accounting;
- software;
- legal research;
- marketing;
- logistics;
- education;
- healthcare support.

The risk is not simply mass unemployment.

It is polarisation between:

- workers whose productivity rises with technology;
- workers whose routine tasks are automated;
- workers in physical and care roles where automation is slower.

Spain’s weak productivity means AI is also an opportunity.

But benefits will not distribute automatically.

Workers need:

- training during employment;
- portable credentials;
- transition support;
- access to technology in small firms;
- bargaining over monitoring and workload.

Employment policy should focus on task transformation, not speculative predictions of total job disappearance.

---

# 28. Working-time reduction could create benefits, but not automatically jobs

Proposals to reduce the standard working week aim to improve:

- work-life balance;
- health;
- productivity;
- care;
- time equality.

Supporters also argue that shorter hours may distribute employment more broadly.

But the employment effect depends on sector and implementation.

A company may respond by:

- hiring;
- improving productivity;
- increasing work intensity;
- reducing opening hours;
- raising prices;
- reducing profit;
- automating.

A uniform reduction affects a high-margin technology company differently from a restaurant, farm or care provider.

The policy should therefore be evaluated through:

- hourly productivity;
- overtime;
- staffing;
- sickness absence;
- wages;
- small-business costs;
- sectoral exemptions or transition support.

It should not be sold as a guaranteed unemployment solution.

---

# 29. Dismissal reform involves a real trade-off

Strong dismissal protection can:

- protect workers;
- encourage employer commitment;
- reduce arbitrary turnover;
- preserve income stability.

It can also make employers more cautious about permanent hiring where future demand is uncertain.

Weak protection may increase hiring but shift risk entirely onto workers.

Spain’s historical mistake was not simply that dismissal was too easy or too expensive.

It was the sharp division between highly protected permanent workers and disposable temporary workers.

The objective should be:

- broad stable employment;
- predictable dismissal costs;
- effective protection against abuse;
- strong unemployment insurance;
- rapid re-employment support;
- internal flexibility during downturns.

---

# 30. Testing the main political claims

## “Spain has never had more people working”

Correct. Employment is at historically high levels, and more than 22 million people are employed. 

**Verdict:** true, but incomplete because population and labour supply also increased.

## “Unemployment is at a historic low”

Registered unemployment is at its lowest since 2008, but the EPA unemployment rate remains above 10% and much higher than the EU average. 

**Verdict:** true for one administrative series; misleading as a complete description.

## “The labour reform ended precariousness”

It sharply reduced conventional temporary employment.

**Verdict:** significant success, but insecurity survives through low hours, discontinuity, weak wages and unstable annual income.

## “Permanent-discontinuous workers are hidden unemployed”

They have an ongoing employment relationship and should not automatically be classified as unemployed.

**Verdict:** generally false, but the lack of transparent activity and income data creates legitimate questions.

## “Employers cannot find workers because people prefer benefits”

Benefits may affect incentives in some cases. Many vacancies are also poorly paid, seasonal, geographically inaccessible or require scarce skills.

**Verdict:** too simplistic.

## “Spain has too many graduates”

Spain has overqualification in some fields and shortages in others.

**Verdict:** the problem is mismatch and weak guidance, not education itself.

## “Immigration takes jobs from Spaniards”

Employment among both native and foreign-born populations has expanded, while immigration fills many shortages.

**Verdict:** no general fixed number of jobs exists; competition can occur in particular occupations, especially where wages and conditions are weak.

## “Lower dismissal costs will solve unemployment”

They may reduce hiring risk but can also increase instability.

**Verdict:** only one part of a much larger system.

## “Public employment services do not work”

They administer benefits and programmes essential to millions, but placement and personalised activation remain weak.

**Verdict:** too absolute, but the matching function needs fundamental improvement.

# 31. What a serious employment strategy would prioritise

## A. Replace unemployment registration with a verified skills profile

Every jobseeker should have a portable record including:

- skills;
- experience;
- qualifications;
- languages;
- mobility;
- schedule;
- training needs.

## B. Evaluate employment programmes rigorously

Funding should follow demonstrated results:

- sustained employment;
- wage;
- retention;
- progression;
- value added beyond what would have occurred anyway.

## C. Make housing and transport part of labour policy

Provide:

- relocation assistance;
- temporary housing;
- commuter transport;
- regional mobility grants;
- employer-supported accommodation in genuine shortage areas.

## D. Build sector-based training agreements

Employers receiving publicly funded training support should commit to:

- curriculum design;
- placements;
- hiring targets;
- wage transparency;
- independent evaluation.

## E. Publish labour-shortage evidence

Before expanding migration or subsidy routes for a shortage occupation, publish:

- vacancies;
- wages;
- duration;
- employer training investment;
- regional location;
- working conditions.

## F. Improve transitions for young people

Create a right to:

- quality apprenticeship;
- paid practical training;
- transparent graduate outcomes;
- career guidance;
- recognised skills credentials.

## G. Target long-term unemployment intensively

Reduce adviser caseloads and combine employment support with:

- health;
- transport;
- childcare;
- digital skills;
- employer mediation.

## H. Strengthen job quality measurement

Report:

- annual hours;
- annual income;
- involuntary part-time work;
- discontinuous inactivity;
- training;
- schedule predictability;
- occupational mobility.

## I. Preserve jobs during recessions

Make ERTE-style internal adjustment the default where firms are viable, with training during reduced activity.

## J. Help productive firms scale

Simplify growth barriers and support:

- management training;
- digitalisation;
- exports;
- finance;
- worker training;
- innovation.

---

# 32. What success should look like

Spain should not judge employment policy solely through:

- Social Security affiliation;
- registered unemployment;
- contract counts.

A stronger dashboard should include:

- EPA unemployment rate;
- employment rate;
- youth unemployment;
- long-term unemployment;
- time to first stable job;
- median annual labour income;
- involuntary part-time rate;
- annual active days for discontinuous workers;
- vacancy duration;
- job-to-job transitions;
- training outcomes;
- regional mobility;
- productivity per hour;
- poverty among working households.

---

# Final verdict

Spain’s labour market has improved substantially.

It now has:

- record employment;
- fewer conventional temporary contracts;
- registered unemployment below pre-crisis levels;
- stronger mechanisms to preserve employment during shocks;
- sustained job creation supported by economic growth and migration.

These achievements should not be dismissed as statistical manipulation.

But Spain remains structurally abnormal within Europe.

The country still has:

- unemployment above 10%;
- youth unemployment around the low-to-mid twenties;
- large regional gaps;
- long-term exclusion;
- weak public job matching;
- skill shortages alongside unemployed workers;
- insecurity hidden behind some permanent contracts;
- low productivity that limits wage growth.

The central failure is not that Spain cannot create jobs.

It is that the system is poor at converting economic growth into **continuous, productive employment for nearly everyone who wants to work**.

Spain has partially moved beyond its old model of disposable temporary workers.

It has not yet built the replacement:

> **a labour market where workers can acquire relevant skills, move toward available jobs, earn enough to live near them and survive economic shocks without being expelled from employment.**

The next employment reform should be judged not by how many contract categories it changes, but by whether it reduces the unemployment gap with Europe while improving annual income and job continuity.

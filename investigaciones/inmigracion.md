# Immigration in Spain: the country needs workers, but imports inequality

### Spain’s economy is growing partly because hundreds of thousands of people arrive each year. Yet housing, schools, administrative services and integration policies have not expanded at the same speed. The result is a system that produces national economic gains while concentrating many of its costs in particular neighbourhoods, occupations and municipalities.

## The central finding

Spain’s immigration problem is frequently described in the wrong terms.

It is not principally an “invasion by small boats.” Nor can it be reduced to the claim that immigration is either entirely beneficial or inherently damaging.

The deeper problem is this:

> **Spain is relying on immigration to sustain employment, consumption and population growth while integrating many newcomers into an economy characterised by low wages, expensive housing, occupational segregation and weak administrative capacity.**

This produces three simultaneous realities:

1. Immigration is helping Spain grow and mitigating demographic decline.
2. Some communities experience genuine pressure on housing, schools, healthcare and social cohesion.
3. Many immigrants themselves bear the greatest costs through overcrowding, unstable employment, administrative irregularity and downward occupational mobility.

The benefits and costs are not distributed among the same people. Employers, consumers, property owners and the central government may gain, while particular municipalities, renters, low-wage workers and migrants themselves absorb much of the adjustment.

That distributional mismatch—not the crude question “is immigration good or bad?”—is the centre of the investigation.

---

# 1. Spain has become an immigration country extraordinarily quickly

Spain was a country of mass emigration for much of the twentieth century. It became a major immigration destination only around the turn of the century.

By 1 January 2026, Spain had **49.57 million residents**, including more than **10 million people born abroad**. During 2025 alone, its population grew by approximately 442,000. By April 2026 it had reached almost 49.7 million, with annual growth approaching 460,000 people. 

At the beginning of 2025:

- 14.1% of residents had foreign nationality.
- 19.3% had been born outside Spain.
- The largest recent increases involved people from Colombia, Venezuela and Morocco. 

The difference between *foreign nationality* and *foreign-born* matters. A person born abroad may later acquire Spanish nationality and disappear from statistics based solely on citizenship. Conversely, a child born in Spain can retain foreign nationality.

Any investigation that alternates carelessly between “foreigners,” “immigrants,” “asylum seekers” and “irregular migrants” will produce misleading conclusions.

## Four populations that political debate routinely mixes together

**Foreign-born residents** include naturalised Spaniards, workers, retirees, students, refugees and family members.

**Foreign nationals** are residents who do not possess Spanish citizenship, including EU citizens.

**Asylum seekers** are people requesting international protection. Their legal claim may ultimately be accepted or rejected.

**Irregular migrants** are people without valid residence status. Some entered without authorisation, but others arrived legally and later lost or exceeded their permit.

These categories overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

---

# 2. The small-boats image misrepresents the scale and composition of immigration

In 2025, Spain recorded **36,775 unauthorised arrivals by sea or land**, down 42.6% from 64,019 in 2024. Maritime arrivals fell to 32,925, largely because arrivals in the Canary Islands dropped from 46,843 to 17,788. Arrivals by sea to the peninsula and Balearic Islands, however, rose slightly. 

That is a humanitarian, border-management and reception challenge. But it is not the principal numerical route through which Spain’s immigrant population grows.

Compare the magnitudes:



Spain received **141,000 first-time asylum applications in 2025**, the highest number in the EU and 21% of the European total. Spain nevertheless ranked behind Greece and Cyprus relative to population, with 2.9 applicants per 1,000 residents. Venezuelans were the largest applicant nationality at EU level, illustrating that Spain’s asylum system is strongly shaped by Latin American political displacement rather than only African maritime routes. 

The television image of a boat is emotionally powerful because it shows a visible border crossing. The much larger processes—air travel, family reunification, study, employment, asylum applications and permit expiry—are administratively ordinary and visually invisible.

**Verdict:** irregular maritime migration is real, sometimes deadly and locally disruptive. But treating it as synonymous with immigration distorts the entire phenomenon.

---

# 3. Immigration is now one of Spain’s main engines of employment growth

Spain ended 2025 with almost **3.1 million foreign workers affiliated with Social Security**, around 200,000 more than one year earlier. Foreign workers represented roughly 14% of total affiliation. 

The Banco de España estimates that foreign residents contributed between **0.4 and 0.7 percentage points** to Spain’s average annual per-capita GDP growth between 2022 and 2024, when total per-capita growth averaged 2.9%. It attributes the improvement partly to better educational and occupational profiles among recent arrivals and to the gradual assimilation of immigrants who arrived earlier. 

Immigration supports economic activity through several channels:

- It increases the number of workers.
- It raises consumption and demand for housing and services.
- It fills vacancies in sectors with recruitment difficulties.
- It permits more native workers—especially women—to work when migrants provide care and domestic services.
- It increases tax and social-security contributions.
- It slows the decline of the working-age population.

The OECD reported that immigrants’ labour-force participation in Spain reached 69.3% in the third quarter of 2025, compared with 57.6% among native workers. 

That figure undermines the idea that immigrants generally come to Spain to avoid work. It does not, however, establish that the employment they obtain is productive, well paid or stable.

---

# 4. Spain needs migrants—but immigration cannot “solve” ageing by itself

Spain has one of Europe’s most severe ageing trajectories. Low fertility and rising longevity mean that the ratio of retirees to working-age residents will increase sharply over coming decades.

Immigration helps because newcomers are, on average, younger than the existing population and more likely to be of working age. It increases contributors immediately, whereas a higher birth rate would take roughly two decades to produce additional workers.

But immigration does not permanently abolish demographic ageing:

- Immigrants also grow old.
- Some eventually leave Spain.
- Their fertility tends to converge toward the host-country pattern.
- Low wages produce relatively low contributions.
- Population growth also increases demand for healthcare, education, housing and infrastructure.

AIReF’s fiscal analysis treats immigration as relevant to employment and public finances but not as a substitute for productivity growth, pension reform or broader fiscal adjustment. 

The claim that Spain “needs millions of immigrants to pay pensions” is therefore both directionally true and politically misleading.

Spain needs a larger employed population. That can come from immigration, higher employment among existing residents, later retirement, technological change and higher productivity. Merely increasing population without improving productivity and wages does not guarantee fiscal sustainability.

**Verdict:** immigration delays and reduces demographic pressure; it does not make an unreformed pension system permanently sustainable.

---

# 5. Spain is importing labour into precisely the sectors it has failed to modernise

Immigrant workers are disproportionately represented in:

- Agriculture.
- Hospitality.
- Construction.
- Domestic employment and care.
- Logistics.
- Cleaning.
- Other labour-intensive services.

These sectors provide essential work. But they also frequently feature low pay, seasonal demand, long hours, informal employment and weak bargaining power.

This creates an uncomfortable economic mechanism.

An employer facing labour scarcity has several possible responses:

1. Raise wages.
2. Improve conditions.
3. Invest in automation or productivity.
4. Reduce output.
5. Recruit workers with fewer alternatives and less bargaining power.

Migration can enable the fifth response. This does not mean immigrants “lower wages” automatically. It means that a migration system connected to precarious employment can allow low-productivity business models to survive longer than they otherwise would.

The effect is unlikely to be uniform. Higher-skilled immigration may complement native workers and raise productivity. Lower-skilled migration may place some downward pressure on wages or bargaining power in directly competing occupations, while lowering prices and expanding employment elsewhere.

The crucial question is not simply how many people arrive. It is:

> **Does Spain use migration to become more productive, or to avoid fixing low-productivity sectors?**

The OECD identifies both strengths and structural gaps in migrant labour-market integration and recommends better skills recognition, training and alignment between migration channels and labour needs. 

---

# 6. The “immigration creates growth” argument conceals the difference between total GDP and living standards

More residents almost mechanically increase total production and consumption. A country with one million additional workers will usually produce more.

But citizens experience living standards through:

- GDP per person.
- Wages.
- Housing costs.
- Public-service access.
- Working hours.
- Productivity.
- Distribution of income.

Spain’s recent immigration has contributed positively even to per-capita GDP growth, according to the Banco de España. That is important evidence against the claim that migration merely enlarges the population without adding value. 

Nevertheless, national averages can hide distributional losses.

A hotel owner may benefit from more workers and customers. A property owner may benefit from stronger housing demand. The Treasury may collect more contributions. At the same time, a low-paid local worker may face greater competition for a room, a rental contract or an entry-level job.

All these observations can be true simultaneously.

The mistake made by defenders of immigration is to assume that a positive aggregate effect means nobody loses. The mistake made by opponents is to interpret any local cost as proof that the national contribution is negative.

---

# 7. Housing is where migration’s distributional conflict becomes most visible

Immigration is not the original cause of Spain’s housing crisis. The country’s problems of underbuilding, slow planning, geographical concentration and tiny public-housing stock existed independently.

But rapid population growth increases demand in a market whose supply cannot respond quickly.

Spain’s population grew by roughly 442,000 during 2025. The housing system did not create anything close to one new dwelling for each additional household formed. The resulting pressure is strongest in Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Alicante, Málaga, the islands and other areas attracting both employment and migration. 

Migrants are not merely competitors in this market. They are among its principal victims:

- They are more likely to rent.
- They often lack deposits, guarantees and local credit histories.
- Some landlords discriminate by nationality.
- Recent arrivals may share overcrowded accommodation.
- Irregular migrants are vulnerable to exploitative subletting.
- Seasonal workers may be housed in informal settlements or inadequate employer-provided accommodation.

Across the EU, 33% of non-EU citizens lived in overcrowded accommodation in 2024, compared with 13.7% of nationals. Non-EU citizens were also more than twice as likely to face excessive housing-cost burdens. 

The correct causal statement is therefore:

**Immigration increases housing demand, particularly in successful urban areas. Spain’s failure to build and maintain affordable housing converts that additional demand into conflict.**

Blaming migrants alone mistakes the pressure for the broken system transmitting it.

---

# 8. Pressure on schools, healthcare and social services is real—but highly local

An additional resident contributes to aggregate demand and tax revenue. But the public services they require are delivered in specific places.

A municipality may receive hundreds of new residents in one neighbourhood while funding, staffing and infrastructure respond years later. A school may need language support immediately. A health centre may already be overcrowded. Social workers may face complex documentation, housing and family cases.

This creates the central fiscal-geographical mismatch:

- The central state captures much of the additional tax and social-security revenue.
- Autonomous communities provide healthcare and education.
- Municipalities manage registration, basic social services and neighbourhood integration.
- The costs arrive before fiscal allocations fully adjust.

As a result, the claim “immigrants pay more than they receive” may be compatible with a local mayor saying that their services are overwhelmed.

The solution is not to deny local pressure. It is to make funding follow population growth faster and more precisely.

Additional money should be allocated according to:

- Recent population change, not only historical totals.
- School-age children.
- Primary-care demand.
- Housing stress.
- Concentration of asylum or reception facilities.
- Language-support needs.
- Poverty and vulnerability indicators.

Without such adjustment, successful integration depends too heavily on whether a newcomer happens to settle in a wealthy municipality or an already strained one.

# 9. Do immigrants receive more welfare than they contribute?

The popular image of a migrant immediately accessing generous benefits ignores several legal and economic barriers.

Many benefits require:

- Legal residence.
- Registration.
- Prior contributions.
- Minimum residence periods.
- Household-income verification.
- Extensive documentation.

New arrivals are usually of working age and therefore use fewer pensions than Spain’s older native population. But low incomes can make immigrant households more eligible for means-tested assistance, while families with children consume education and healthcare.

The fiscal balance changes over the life cycle and differs enormously by:

- Age at arrival.
- Employment rate.
- Salary.
- Family composition.
- Legal status.
- Duration of residence.
- Education.
- Eventual retirement or departure.

The strongest general conclusion is not that every immigrant is fiscally positive. It is that **employment and earnings determine the balance far more than nationality itself**.

A migrant employed formally at a productive firm contributes through income tax, consumption taxes and Social Security. An undocumented worker in informal agriculture may contribute through consumption but remain outside full payroll taxation while also being vulnerable to exploitation.

Therefore, regularisation and labour enforcement are fiscal policies as well as humanitarian ones.

# 10. Crime: neither denial nor collective accusation survives serious analysis

The 2024 INE statistics show that 71.4% of adults convicted had Spanish nationality. However, the conviction rate was **15.7 per 1,000 foreign adults**, compared with **6.2 per 1,000 Spanish adults**—approximately 2.5 times higher. Among minors, the corresponding rates were 10.9 and 5.8 per 1,000. 

Those figures should not be hidden. But they also do not establish that foreign nationality itself causes criminal behaviour.



At least five complications matter.

First, immigrant populations are younger and more male than the Spanish population. Crime rates are higher among young men in virtually every society. The INE itself reports that the highest overall conviction rate occurs between ages 21 and 30. 

Second, foreign residents are disproportionately exposed to poverty, unstable housing, informal employment and marginal neighbourhoods—all independent risk factors.

Third, statistics based on nationality classify naturalised immigrants as Spanish. They are not a clean comparison between “immigrants” and “natives.”

Fourth, conviction data reflect offending, policing, prosecution, legal representation and sentencing. They do not capture every offence equally.

Fifth, the foreign population is heterogeneous. Combining a British retiree, a Romanian construction worker, a Colombian nurse, a Moroccan teenager and an irregular seasonal worker into one category has little explanatory value.

Spain’s conventional crime rate stood at 40.4 offences per 1,000 residents in 2025, within the low band of its historical series, even after years of rapid immigration. That does not disprove local or offence-specific relationships, but it contradicts the simple narrative of a national crime explosion caused by immigration. 

**Verdict:** foreign nationals are overrepresented in conviction statistics. The difference is real. Treating it as proof of an inherent group tendency is analytically indefensible; pretending the difference does not exist destroys credibility.

# 11. Irregularity is partly produced by the state itself

Irregular migration is often imagined as a permanent identity: a person crosses the border illegally and remains outside the system.

In reality, irregularity is frequently an administrative condition.

A person can become irregular because:

- A visa expires.
- An asylum claim is rejected.
- A job ends and a permit cannot be renewed.
- An employer refuses to formalise employment.
- An appointment or decision takes many months.
- The person entered through an authorised route but overstayed.
- Family or documentation circumstances change.

Spain has repeatedly used regularisation—both formal mass programmes and individual *arraigo* routes—because its economy employs workers whom its legal migration system initially fails to admit efficiently.

That is not simply generous policy. It reveals a design contradiction:

> Spain demands labour first, tolerates informality second, and recognises residence later.

The 2024 reform of the Foreigners Regulation sought to simplify procedures, improve labour-market access and broaden regularisation pathways. The OECD describes these changes as an attempt to modernise the system and align it more closely with integration and labour demand. 

Regularisation has plausible advantages:

- Workers can report abuse.
- Employers must contribute.
- Labour inspection becomes more effective.
- Families can rent and register more normally.
- The state can identify and monitor residents.
- Criminal networks lose leverage.

But regularisation without credible future legal pathways can create expectations of repeated exceptional programmes. The solution is not perpetual irregularity or perpetual amnesty. It is a legal system that admits workers through realistic channels before they become embedded in the underground economy.

# 12. Asylum is being used as a substitute migration channel

Spain received more first-time asylum claims than any other EU country in 2025. 

Some applicants clearly require international protection. Others may primarily be seeking work and safety from severe economic or institutional conditions that do not satisfy the narrower refugee definition.

When ordinary labour-migration channels are too restrictive or slow, asylum becomes one of the few available routes to temporary documentation and presence.

This produces several problems:

- Genuine protection cases wait longer.
- Applicants spend months or years in uncertainty.
- Rejected applicants may later become irregular.
- Reception capacity is consumed by people whose primary route should have been employment.
- Public confidence in asylum declines.

Spain needs to separate the channels more clearly:

- Fast humanitarian protection for strong asylum cases.
- Fast rejection with meaningful appeal for clearly ineligible claims.
- Realistic work visas for occupations with demonstrated demand.
- Search permits allowing selected migrants to find employment under defined conditions.
- Circular routes that protect workers from employer dependence.
- Mobility pathways for students trained in Spain.

A functioning labour-migration system protects asylum by preventing it from becoming the only accessible door.

---

# 13. The labour market wastes immigrant qualifications

The stereotype that immigrants are predominantly unqualified hides a significant problem: many are educated above the level required by their Spanish job.

Foreign qualifications may be difficult to recognise. Administrative homologation can take years. Employers may not understand overseas credentials. Language, professional licensing and lack of contacts create additional barriers.

The result is visible in doctors working as carers, engineers driving delivery vehicles and graduates employed in hospitality.

Eurostat consistently finds that people born outside the EU experience the highest overqualification rates, particularly women. 

This is harmful in three ways:

1. Spain loses productivity and tax revenue.
2. Origin countries lose trained professionals.
3. Migrants experience downward mobility and frustration.

The fastest way to improve immigration’s economic contribution may not be to attract more people. It may be to use the skills of those already present.

A serious reform would create:

- Maximum deadlines for qualification recognition.
- Provisional supervised practice.
- Standard competence examinations.
- Bridging courses.
- Occupational Spanish training.
- Transparent databases showing application status.
- Automatic or accelerated recognition for institutions and professions with proven comparability.

# 14. Integration is not the same as employment

A person can have a job and remain socially separated.

Integration also involves:

- Language.
- Housing stability.
- School outcomes.
- Social networks.
- Political participation.
- Trust in institutions.
- Equality before employers and landlords.
- A plausible route to long-term membership.

Spain has some advantages. Spanish is a global language, and many newcomers arrive from Latin America with immediate linguistic and cultural familiarity. Naturalisation and mixed families can reduce the permanence of ethnic boundaries.

But integration is uneven. Children can enter schools heavily concentrated by class and origin. Migrant women may remain isolated in domestic or care work. Young people born or raised in Spain can still be treated as foreigners. Discrimination in rental and employment markets restricts mobility.

The objective should not be to eliminate private cultural difference. It should be to prevent origin from becoming a durable predictor of unemployment, low education, overcrowding or criminal involvement.

That requires measuring outcomes by generation—not simply counting arrivals.

---

# 15. School concentration may determine Spain’s future more than the border

Immigrant children are not evenly distributed among schools.

Residential segregation, school catchment areas, family resources and the ability of wealthier parents to select alternatives can concentrate disadvantaged pupils in particular public schools.

Once concentration becomes high, several feedback mechanisms can appear:

- Teachers spend more time on language and remedial support.
- Families with greater resources leave.
- Academic expectations decline.
- Staff turnover rises.
- Social networks become more segregated.
- The school acquires a negative reputation.

The issue is not the presence of foreign-origin children. It is the concentration of disadvantage without sufficient resources.

Policy should fund schools according to student need, including:

- Language acquisition.
- Late entry into the academic year.
- Household income.
- Special educational needs.
- Family mediation.
- Previous schooling interruptions.

Spain should also publish school-level segregation indicators while protecting pupils’ privacy.

If children born or educated in Spain remain trapped in low-mobility institutions, immigration’s short-term labour contribution can become long-term inequality.

---

# 16. The most serious security threat may be exploitation, not entry

Irregular and temporary workers can face:

- Wage theft.
- Unsafe housing.
- Employer-controlled transport and accommodation.
- Threats of dismissal or denunciation.
- Passport retention.
- Sexual exploitation.
- Recruitment debt.
- Fraudulent contracts.
- Labour trafficking.

These abuses are difficult to detect because victims fear losing income or legal status. The state may therefore police the border more visibly than the workplaces where illegality becomes profitable.

A migration system that ties residence too tightly to one employer increases that employer’s power.

Effective enforcement would focus on:

- Employers repeatedly hiring undeclared labour.
- Labour intermediaries and subcontracting chains.
- Housing supplied at abusive prices.
- Recruitment fees.
- Agriculture, care, hospitality and construction.
- Safe reporting mechanisms independent of immigration enforcement.

Without this, “controlling immigration” can mean controlling the migrant while leaving the economic beneficiary untouched.

# 17. Testing the most common claims

## “Spain is being invaded”

More than ten million Spanish residents were born abroad, so the demographic transformation is substantial. But the 36,775 recorded irregular arrivals in 2025 represented a very small fraction of the resident foreign-born population and of total annual migration. 

**Verdict:** Spain is undergoing rapid immigration, but the invasion metaphor misrepresents how most people arrive and live.

## “Immigrants do the jobs Spaniards refuse”

Sometimes. But “refuse” often means the wage, instability or conditions are unattractive.

**Verdict:** immigrants fill genuine shortages, but migration can also reduce pressure to improve bad jobs.

## “Immigrants pay our pensions”

They provide important contributions and slow workforce decline. They cannot permanently offset ageing without sufficient wages, productivity and broader reform.

**Verdict:** meaningful support, not a complete solution.

## “Immigration causes crime”

Foreign nationals have a higher conviction rate, and denying this is misleading. But raw nationality rates do not isolate immigration’s causal effect from age, sex, poverty, legal insecurity and neighbourhood conditions. National conventional crime remains historically low despite rapid immigration. 

**Verdict:** a real statistical disparity, routinely turned into an unjustified collective explanation.

## “Immigrants live on benefits”

Foreign workers number more than three million and have high labour-force participation. Access to many benefits requires residence, contributions or extensive eligibility checks. 

**Verdict:** unsupported as a general description; fiscal outcomes depend mainly on employment and earnings.

## “Immigration has nothing to do with the housing crisis”

Additional households increase demand, especially in constrained cities. But the shortage reflects planning, construction and public-housing failures that immigration did not create.

**Verdict:** immigration intensifies the shortage; housing policy determines how destructive that pressure becomes.

## “Regularisation rewards illegality”

It can create incentive concerns. But leaving established workers informal also rewards employers who evade labour law and taxes.

**Verdict:** regularisation is rational when people are already socially and economically embedded, but it should accompany credible legal entry and employer enforcement.

## “Spain can solve depopulation by sending migrants to empty villages”

People settle where work, transport, networks and services exist. A cheap house without employment is not an integration strategy.

**Verdict:** possible in selected local economies, not a national solution imposed from above.

# 18. What a serious immigration policy would look like

## A. Decide the purpose of migration channels

Spain should publish annual assessments covering:

- Labour shortages by occupation and province.
- Expected retirements.
- Housing capacity.
- School and healthcare capacity.
- Unemployment and wages.
- Qualification needs.
- Integration outcomes of previous cohorts.

This should not become a rigid national quota pretending the economy can be forecast exactly. It should establish whether admission policy corresponds to real capacity.

## B. Replace irregularity with legal work routes

Create faster pathways for:

- Care workers.
- Construction trades.
- Agriculture.
- Healthcare.
- Digital and technical professions.
- Students graduating in Spain.
- Verified seasonal and circular employment.

Permits should allow workers to change employer, preventing legal status from becoming a tool of coercion.

## C. Make employers responsible

Businesses recruiting abroad should contribute to:

- Initial accommodation where necessary.
- Language and safety training.
- Transparent contracts.
- Transport in isolated workplaces.
- Enforcement funds in high-risk sectors.

Penalties should focus on systematic exploiters, not only on undocumented workers.

## D. Send resources to receiving municipalities immediately

Funding formulas should update quarterly or annually according to actual population changes.

A municipality receiving a reception facility or sharp inflow should automatically receive:

- Primary-care support.
- School staff.
- Social workers.
- Translation and mediation capacity.
- Housing inspection.
- Policing where evidence supports it.

## E. Prevent housing exploitation

Immigration and housing policy must be coordinated through:

- Inspections of overcrowded rentals.
- Enforcement against illegal subletting.
- Temporary accommodation standards for seasonal labour.
- Expansion of affordable rental stock.
- Penalties for discriminatory advertising and letting.
- Registration that does not depend on landlord cooperation when residence is genuine.

## F. Measure integration across generations

The national dashboard should report:

- Employment and wages.
- Qualification matching.
- School completion.
- Residential overcrowding.
- Poverty.
- Language acquisition.
- Naturalisation.
- Crime and victimisation.
- Intermarriage and social trust.
- Outcomes of children born in Spain.

## G. Speak honestly about crime and social conflict

Authorities should publish age- and sex-standardised data, distinguish offences and avoid nationality headlines without context.

Where particular groups or neighbourhoods show elevated offending, interventions should be targeted rather than diluted into moralistic national arguments.

## H. Protect asylum by accelerating decisions

A credible system must grant protection quickly where justified and decide weak claims within reasonable deadlines.

Applicants should not spend years suspended between temporary permission, work restrictions and possible rejection.

# 19. The policy contradiction no party fully confronts

Much of the political right demands lower immigration while supporting economic sectors dependent on abundant low-cost labour.

Much of the political left defends immigration while underestimating pressure on housing, low-paid workers and struggling public services.

Employers emphasise vacancies but rarely volunteer to raise wages, finance integration or accept responsibility for housing.

National governments celebrate growth and Social Security affiliation, while municipalities face immediate service demands.

Property owners gain from additional housing demand. Renters absorb much of the price pressure.

This explains why the debate remains unresolved. Nearly every powerful actor can support one part of the current system while shifting its costs elsewhere.

---

# Final verdict

Immigration is neither Spain’s catastrophe nor its rescue plan.

It is an increasingly essential part of a country with an ageing population, labour shortages and strong ties to Latin America, Europe and Africa. Recent migration has supported employment, population and even per-capita economic growth. 

But Spain’s current model is fragile.

It frequently recruits people into the lowest-productivity sectors, wastes qualifications, tolerates administrative irregularity, underfunds receiving municipalities and channels newcomers into the same housing shortage already harming young Spaniards.

That model can produce GDP growth without producing integration.

The decisive question is not **how many immigrants Spain can accept in the abstract**.

It is:

> **How many people can Spain incorporate into formal, productive employment, adequate housing, functioning schools and a common civic system—and how quickly can it expand that capacity?**

A serious policy would combine controlled legal entry, rapid asylum decisions, enforcement against exploitative employers, local funding, housing expansion, qualification recognition and transparent measurement of integration and crime.

Without those institutions, high immigration risks becoming a mechanism for expanding low-wage growth and neighbourhood inequality.

With them, it can help Spain remain economically viable while producing new citizens rather than a permanent class of indispensable outsiders.

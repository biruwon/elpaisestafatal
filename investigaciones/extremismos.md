

# Extremism and social tension in Spain: political hostility is growing faster than political violence

### Spain is not experiencing a generalized collapse into extremist violence. But its public conversation increasingly rewards humiliation, collective blame, conspiracy and the idea that political opponents are illegitimate. The greatest danger is not that most citizens are becoming extremists; it is that extremist ways of interpreting reality are entering ordinary politics.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

Spain remains a comparatively peaceful democracy.

Political parties compete through elections. Large demonstrations are normally nonviolent. ETA’s terrorism has ended. Alternation of power remains possible, and ideological disagreement rarely becomes organized street violence.

But underneath that stability, several warning signs are strengthening:

- affective polarization: people increasingly dislike the opposing political group, not merely its policies;
- normalization of dehumanizing language;
- rising recorded hate crimes;
- online communities that convert isolated incidents into collective ethnic or ideological accusations;
- conspiracy narratives that portray institutions as completely captured;
- increasing acceptance of exceptional tactics because “the other side” is considered an existential threat;
- radicalization of some young people through highly personalized digital ecosystems;
- reciprocal escalation between political leaders, partisan media and activist networks.

The core problem is:

> **Spain is not becoming a society of mass extremists. It is becoming a society in which extremist framings are increasingly useful to mainstream political competition.**

This distinction matters.

A country can have few terrorists and still suffer profound democratic damage from hostility, fear and collective suspicion.

---

# 1. Extremism, radicalism and polarization are not the same thing

Public debate often uses these words interchangeably.

They should be separated.

## Radicalism

Radicalism seeks fundamental political or social change.

A radical can reject the current economic model, demand independence, abolish the monarchy, recentralize the state or transform private property without supporting violence or dictatorship.

Radical ideas are compatible with democracy when pursued through lawful, pluralist means.

## Extremism

Extremism generally goes further. It tends to deny legitimate political pluralism and divide society into a morally pure group and an illegitimate enemy.

Common features include:

- rejection of compromise;
- collective guilt;
- political purity tests;
- conspiracy explanations;
- dehumanization;
- willingness to restrict opponents’ rights;
- belief that ordinary democratic rules should not protect the other side.

## Violent extremism

Violent extremism legitimizes, prepares or carries out violence to advance an ideological, religious or identity-based objective.

## Polarization

Polarization means increased distance between political groups.

It can be ideological—larger disagreement over policy—or affective—stronger dislike and distrust of opposing voters and parties.

Polarization is not automatically extremist. Democracies need meaningful differences.

It becomes dangerous when disagreement turns into the belief that opponents are criminal, treasonous, subhuman or undeserving of ordinary democratic rights.

---

# 2. Spain’s main problem is affective polarization

Affective polarization is not simply believing that another party is wrong.

It is emotionally disliking its voters and interpreting them as:

- ignorant;
- immoral;
- dangerous;
- authoritarian;
- anti-Spanish;
- anti-democratic;
- racist;
- communist;
- traitorous.

Research published in the *Revista Española de Investigaciones Sociológicas* identifies affective polarization as a significant feature of Spanish electoral politics. A 2026 longitudinal study found that frequent television-news consumption and ideologically homogeneous media diets can increase individual affective polarization in certain electoral contexts. The study does not support the simplistic idea that every use of media radicalizes people, but it does show that repetitive and one-sided exposure can intensify partisan hostility. 

Another CIS-linked study found that polarized voters are not a single homogeneous social group. Political identity, ideological position and information consumption all matter. 

The decisive shift is from:

> “Your proposal is harmful.”

to:

> “People like you are the problem.”

Once politics becomes a conflict between identities rather than policies, evidence becomes less persuasive. Admitting that an opponent is right can feel like betraying one’s group.

---

# 3. Political tension is intense, but Spain is not on the verge of civil conflict

Spain has experienced highly charged mobilizations over:

- the Catalan independence process;
- the amnesty law;
- feminism and gender legislation;
- immigration;
- housing;
- agricultural policy;
- Israel and Gaza;
- regional financing;
- corruption allegations;
- historical memory.

Demonstrations involving tens of thousands of people are sometimes framed by opponents as proof that democracy is collapsing.

Usually, they demonstrate the opposite: political conflict is being channelled through public assembly rather than organized violence.

Large protests against the Catalan amnesty, for example, brought tens of thousands onto the streets in 2023. The slogans included severe accusations of betrayal, but the national mobilization remained predominantly electoral and demonstrative rather than insurrectionary. 

Similarly, large LGTBI demonstrations in 2025 explicitly demanded stronger action against hate speech and presented themselves as defending rights rather than attacking political opponents. 

The existence of angry demonstrations therefore does not by itself prove extremism.

The relevant questions are:

- Are organizers encouraging violence?
- Are opponents being dehumanized?
- Are false collective accusations spreading?
- Are democratic outcomes being rejected?
- Are participants carrying weapons or seeking confrontation?
- Do political leaders condemn attacks consistently?

---

# 4. Recorded hate crime reached a new high in 2025

The Ministry of the Interior recorded **2,417 hate crimes and hate-related incidents in 2025**, an increase of **23.6%** from 2024. This was the highest figure in the official series. 

That increase is serious, but it requires careful interpretation.

Recorded hate crime can rise because:

- more offences occur;
- victims report more often;
- police classification improves;
- awareness increases;
- digital conduct becomes easier to identify;
- legal definitions or recording practices change.

The official series measures known incidents, not the complete underlying phenomenon.

Underreporting remains important. Victims may avoid reporting because they:

- fear retaliation;
- distrust authorities;
- lack legal status;
- believe nothing will happen;
- do not recognize the discriminatory motive;
- want to avoid public exposure.

The Ministry has maintained annual reporting and approved a third national action plan against hate crime for 2025–2028, indicating that authorities consider the problem structural rather than episodic. 

---

# 5. Hate crime is not a synonym for offensive speech

A democracy must distinguish among:

- insulting or unpleasant opinion;
- political criticism;
- prejudice;
- discriminatory conduct;
- threats;
- incitement;
- assault motivated by hostility toward a protected group.

Not every racist, sexist, anti-religious or anti-LGTBI statement constitutes a crime.

This distinction protects freedom of expression.

But the opposite mistake is also common: assuming that words can never contribute to violence.

Repeated narratives can create a permission structure:

1. A group is presented as dangerous.
2. Individual crimes are generalized to the entire group.
3. Institutions are accused of hiding “the truth.”
4. Ordinary democratic remedies are said to have failed.
5. Harassment or vigilantism becomes framed as self-defence.

Extremist violence rarely begins with an explicit instruction to attack.

It often begins with the normalization of collective suspicion.

---

# 6. Racism and xenophobia form the largest recurring hate-crime category

Racism and xenophobia have consistently represented the largest or one of the largest categories in Spain’s hate-crime statistics. In 2022, for example, Interior recorded 755 known racist or xenophobic offences and incidents, an 18.1% annual increase. 

The 2025 national increase occurred during a period in which immigration had become much more central to political debate. That does not prove that one party or one campaign caused the rise.

But political and media narratives shape which interpretations become socially available.

The most dangerous sequence is:

- a real crime is committed by an immigrant;
- the suspect’s nationality becomes the principal public fact;
- unrelated members of the same nationality or religion are treated as collectively responsible;
- fabricated incidents circulate alongside the genuine case;
- calls for retaliation are framed as community protection.

The unrest around Torre Pacheco in 2025 demonstrated how local incidents, online rumours and transnational extremist networks can interact. Reporting on the aftermath described xenophobic mobilization and anti-racist counterdemonstrations, as well as the use of X and Telegram to spread inflammatory content. 

It is legitimate to discuss crime, integration failure and migration control.

It becomes extremist when individual responsibility is replaced by ethnic guilt.

---

# 7. Anti-LGTBI hostility has become a major cultural battlefield

Spain has some of Europe’s strongest formal protections and broad social acceptance of same-sex relationships.

At the same time, LGTBI people remain targets of:

- harassment;
- assault;
- threats;
- online campaigns;
- school bullying;
- dehumanizing political narratives.

European and Spanish extremism research has noted a broader normalization of anti-LGTBI rhetoric, especially narratives presenting sexual minorities as a threat to children, family or social order. 

The debate is complicated because disagreement over:

- gender identity policy;
- sports;
- medical treatment;
- education;
- legal definitions;

is not automatically hatred.

A democracy must permit disagreement over public policy.

The red line appears when disagreement becomes:

- collective degradation;
- denial of equal citizenship;
- conspiracy claims that an entire minority seeks to corrupt children;
- encouragement of exclusion, harassment or violence.

The political temptation is strong on both sides:

- one side may label every policy disagreement as hatred;
- the other may use legitimate policy uncertainty as a route to stigmatize an entire minority.

Both practices weaken serious debate.

---

# 8. Antisemitism and anti-Muslim hostility have intensified around international conflict

The Hamas attacks of October 2023 and the subsequent war in Gaza had direct consequences for European internal security.

The National Security Department and Europol have warned that jihadist organizations, far-right extremists and other radical actors have exploited the conflict to spread hatred and encourage radicalization online. 

In Spain, legitimate arguments over:

- Israeli military conduct;
- Palestinian statehood;
- Hamas;
- occupation;
- civilian protection;
- Spanish foreign policy;

can slide into collective hostility.

Criticism of the Israeli government is not inherently antisemitic.

Support for Palestinian rights is not support for terrorism.

Concern about Islamist extremism is not inherently Islamophobic.

The lines are crossed when:

- Jews are treated as collectively responsible for Israeli policy;
- Muslims are treated as collectively sympathetic to jihadism;
- attacks on civilians are celebrated;
- religious sites or individuals are threatened;
- conspiracy theories assign hidden group control.

International conflict becomes socially dangerous when local citizens are forced to answer for foreign governments or armed groups.

---

# 9. Jihadist terrorism remains Spain’s principal organized terrorist threat

Spain’s prosecution authorities describe international terrorism—especially jihadist terrorism—as the principal contemporary terrorist threat after the effective neutralization of domestic terrorism linked to ETA. 

Official Interior data show sustained preventive activity.

By 17 December 2025, authorities reported **100 jihadism-related arrests in Spain during 2025**, with another 12 abroad in operations linked to Spanish authorities. In 2024, the corresponding domestic figure was 81. 

By 23 June 2026, the 2026 official map recorded **37 operations and 68 arrests** in Spain linked to jihadist terrorism. Since 2012, the Interior Ministry’s cumulative map recorded 816 arrests. 

Arrest figures are not conviction figures. Some operations may involve:

- propaganda;
- recruitment;
- financing;
- preparation;
- self-radicalization;
- possession of instructional material;
- contact with terrorist networks.

They do not imply that every suspect was preparing an imminent attack.

The main current pattern is increasingly individualized:

- online propaganda;
- self-training;
- encrypted communication;
- rapid radicalization;
- young participants;
- limited direct organizational contact.

A February 2026 Madrid operation, for example, involved a suspect whom police described as being in an advanced state of radicalization and using public Wi-Fi networks to download terrorist material. 

The threat is real and should not be minimized.

But jihadist arrests should not be used to treat Spain’s Muslim population as a security category.

Muslim families, community organizations and religious leaders can be among the most important sources of prevention and early warning.

---

# 10. Far-right violent extremism is smaller than jihadist terrorism in Spain, but cannot be dismissed

Far-right extremism ranges from electoral nationalism to explicitly violent neo-Nazi and accelerationist networks.

These categories must not be merged.

Voting for a right-wing or anti-immigration party is not evidence of violent extremism.

Violent far-right milieus differ in that they may promote:

- racial war;
- white supremacy;
- neo-Nazism;
- antisemitism;
- replacement conspiracy theories;
- armed preparation;
- attacks on minorities;
- acceleration of social collapse.

In December 2025, Europol and the Spanish National Police announced an operation disrupting activity associated with **The Base**, an international far-right terrorist organization. 

Spain’s National Security reporting also recognizes that global conflicts and digital propaganda can be exploited by extremists of both the right and left. 

The far-right threat should not be exaggerated into a claim that Spain faces a mass neo-Nazi insurgency.

Its importance lies elsewhere:

- small networks can cause disproportionate violence;
- lone actors can radicalize quickly;
- mainstream conspiracy language can create recruitment pathways;
- online groups connect Spanish extremists with foreign movements.

---

# 11. Far-left and anarchist extremism exist, but are not symmetrical in every period

Spain has a history of:

- anarchist violence;
- GRAPO;
- violent anti-capitalist groups;
- attacks on property;
- clashes involving radical protest movements.

Contemporary far-left extremism can include:

- legitimization of political violence;
- attacks on party offices;
- assaults on opponents;
- arson;
- sabotage;
- violent anti-police or anti-state rhetoric.

But “far left” is frequently used too broadly.

Trade union action, anti-fascism, squatting, environmental direct action, republicanism or anti-capitalism are not automatically violent extremism.

Similarly, property damage is unlawful but not always terrorism.

The evidence must distinguish:

- disruptive protest;
- public-order offences;
- ideologically motivated violence;
- organized terrorism.

False symmetry is as misleading as partisan denial.

The dominant violent threat can differ across time, place and type of offence.

---

# 12. ETA has ended, but its memory remains politically active

ETA’s armed campaign is over.

Its operational defeat and dissolution represent one of Spain’s greatest democratic achievements.

Yet the political use of ETA remains intense.

The issue appears through:

- treatment of victims;
- prison policy;
- public tributes to released prisoners;
- EH Bildu’s historical political environment;
- accusations that the government legitimizes former ETA-linked politics;
- accusations that the right instrumentalizes victims for electoral purposes.

There are legitimate debates over memory and accountability.

But two distortions recur.

One side may minimize the moral and political continuity between present actors and a movement that justified terrorism.

The other may imply that any democratic cooperation with EH Bildu is equivalent to supporting ETA.

A democratic party can be legal and institutionally relevant while still being required to address its historical relationship to political violence with greater clarity.

---

# 13. Catalan separatism illustrates the difference between radicalism and extremism

Demanding Catalan independence is a radical constitutional proposal.

It is not inherently extremist.

Millions of citizens can support independence peacefully and democratically.

The 2017 crisis involved serious institutional conflict, unilateral action and legal confrontation. But it did not develop into a sustained campaign of organized terrorism.

Extremist tendencies appear when either side claims:

- all Catalans support independence;
- all unionists are fascists;
- Catalonia is an occupied colony equivalent to an authoritarian conquest;
- separatists are collectively traitors;
- legal or electoral disagreement justifies political violence;
- the opposing community lacks legitimate national identity.

The territorial conflict becomes dangerous when identities are treated as mutually exclusive.

Many Catalans possess overlapping Spanish, Catalan and European identities. Polarized political systems pressure them to choose one camp.

---

# 14. “Anti-fascism” and “fascism” are increasingly used as tribal labels

The term fascism refers to a specific authoritarian political tradition involving:

- anti-pluralism;
- ultranationalism;
- leader worship;
- political violence;
- suppression of opposition;
- rejection of liberal democracy.

In contemporary Spanish debate, “fascist” is often applied to:

- conservatives;
- police;
- judges;
- national symbols;
- immigration restriction;
- criticism of identity policies.

The right similarly uses “communist” to describe:

- moderate welfare policy;
- progressive taxation;
- rent regulation;
- unions;
- feminism;
- public intervention.

These labels erase distinctions.

Once an opponent is classified as fascist or communist, compromise becomes morally suspect.

The vocabulary is strategically useful because it permits parties to inherit the moral legitimacy of historical struggles without proving that the present opponent actually fits the category.

---

# 15. Conspiracy theories are a bridge between ordinary distrust and extremism

Not every suspicion of government, corporations or media is conspiratorial.

Institutions do lie, fail and protect powerful interests.

A conspiracy theory becomes epistemically closed when:

- contrary evidence proves the conspiracy is deeper;
- absence of evidence proves successful concealment;
- all institutions are controlled;
- anonymous online sources outrank verifiable documents;
- complex events are attributed to one secret plan;
- believers become a heroic minority who alone see reality.

Common themes include:

- electoral fraud without credible evidence;
- hidden demographic replacement;
- global control through vaccines or climate policy;
- judicial plots;
- secret media coordination;
- foreign control of all political parties;
- fabricated attacks or victims.

Conspiracy narratives offer psychological benefits:

- they simplify complexity;
- give random events intention;
- provide a clear enemy;
- create belonging;
- transform uncertainty into moral certainty.

European research identifies conspiracy thinking as an important contributor to polarization and radicalization because it provides an emotionally satisfying ideological framework for people who feel excluded or powerless. 

---

# 16. Social media does not create polarization alone—but changes its economics

Social networks did not invent political hatred.

Spain experienced ideological violence long before the internet.

Platforms change four conditions.

## Speed

A false or inflammatory claim can reach millions before verification.

## Personalization

Algorithms learn which fears and enemies retain each user.

## Reward

Anger produces replies, shares and watch time.

## Community

Users who once held isolated extreme views can now find reinforcement, status and instruction.

A 2025 European Parliament briefing warned that social-media environments deepen ideological echo chambers and that engagement-driven algorithms can prioritize divisive content. 

The important causal claim is not:

> “The algorithm brainwashes everyone.”

It is:

> “The system rewards political entrepreneurs who produce emotionally divisive material more efficiently than those producing cautious context.”

---

# 17. Homogeneous media diets intensify hostility

Spanish media have long had recognizable ideological identities.

Pluralism is not the problem. Citizens should have access to competing interpretations.

The danger is ideological enclosure.

A person who consumes only one partisan ecosystem repeatedly receives:

- the worst examples from the opposing side;
- the best defence of their own side;
- selective scandals;
- different factual agendas;
- different standards of evidence.

Recent Spanish longitudinal research found that ideologically homogeneous media consumption can increase affective polarization in some electoral contexts. 

This does not mean centrist media are always truthful or that neutrality is possible.

The protective factor is exposure to genuinely different evidence and arguments without immediate social punishment.

---

# 18. Mainstream politicians are central to the problem

Extremist groups have limited reach unless their narratives are repeated by larger political actors.

Mainstream politicians contribute to tension when they:

- describe elected opponents as dictators or criminals without judicial basis;
- call every institutional disagreement a coup;
- imply that entire voter groups are anti-democratic;
- circulate unverified crime stories;
- attack journalists or judges collectively;
- portray compromise as treason;
- encourage followers to view politics as national salvation.

The tactic works because moderate language struggles to compete for attention.

A party spokesperson saying:

> “This policy may create unintended fiscal and constitutional consequences”

receives less engagement than:

> “The government is destroying Spain.”

The political market punishes proportion.

---

# 19. The left and right radicalize different fears

## The radical right emphasizes

- demographic replacement;
- crime by foreigners;
- loss of national sovereignty;
- feminism as hostility toward men;
- LGTBI policy as social corruption;
- climate policy as elite control;
- separatists as internal enemies.

## The radical left emphasizes

- fascist capture of institutions;
- capitalism as inherent violence;
- police as structurally oppressive;
- landlords or business owners as predatory classes;
- conservative voters as morally compromised;
- Western foreign policy as the primary explanation for global violence.

Each side begins with some real evidence:

- migration can create integration and security challenges;
- discrimination exists;
- markets generate unequal power;
- institutions can act unfairly;
- nationalism can exclude;
- corporations can exploit.

Extremism emerges when the partial truth becomes a total explanation.

---

# 20. Gender politics has become a high-intensity identity conflict

Debates over feminism, sexual violence, trans rights, family and masculinity touch intimate experiences.

That makes them unusually vulnerable to polarization.

Some women experience:

- violence;
- harassment;
- discrimination;
- unequal care burdens.

Some men experience:

- educational failure;
- loneliness;
- occupational insecurity;
- family separation;
- the feeling that public discourse treats them as collectively suspect.

Extremist actors convert these different problems into a zero-sum gender war.

One side may deny persistent violence and discrimination.

The other may dismiss male-specific vulnerabilities because acknowledging them appears politically disloyal.

This creates recruitment opportunities for misogynist online communities and for ideological spaces that interpret every disagreement as patriarchal aggression.

The antidote is not false neutrality. It is the ability to protect women’s rights while addressing male vulnerability without treating either sex as an enemy class.

---

# 21. Economic insecurity contributes to tension, but does not mechanically create extremism

Housing stress, precarious work, regional decline and blocked mobility can produce:

- resentment;
- loss of control;
- distrust;
- status anxiety;
- demand for scapegoats.

But poorer people are not automatically more extremist.

Many economically secure people support radical movements.

Material insecurity becomes politically explosive when a narrative identifies:

- who allegedly stole the opportunity;
- why institutions supposedly protect them;
- which group deserves punishment;
- which leader promises restoration.

The emotional issue is often relative loss:

> “People like me used to belong, and now others are being prioritized.”

Extremist politics transforms complex institutional failure into group competition.

---

# 22. Immigration is the most powerful current polarization accelerator

Immigration combines:

- economic interest;
- cultural change;
- visible demographic difference;
- crime;
- housing;
- religion;
- borders;
- national identity.

This allows every local incident to be interpreted as proof of a national theory.

Opponents of immigration may exaggerate crime, welfare use or demographic intention.

Defenders may deny real integration problems, local service pressure or statistical disparities.

Both responses strengthen extremists.

When institutions hide uncomfortable facts, conspiratorial actors gain credibility.

When political actors generalize from individual cases, minorities become targets.

The credible position requires simultaneous honesty:

- immigration contributes economically;
- capacity and integration matter;
- some crime and social conflicts require direct intervention;
- nationality does not create collective guilt;
- borders and asylum rules must be enforceable;
- residents must receive equal protection.

---

# 23. Foreign disinformation exploits divisions more often than it creates them

Foreign actors do not need to invent Spanish conflicts.

They can amplify existing ones:

- Catalonia;
- migration;
- NATO;
- Ukraine;
- Gaza;
- feminism;
- distrust of the EU;
- corruption;
- public-health conspiracies.

Their objective may not be to make one ideology win.

It may be to:

- reduce trust;
- make truth appear unknowable;
- intensify hostility;
- weaken European coordination;
- convince citizens that every institution is fraudulent.

Spanish and European security analysis identifies disinformation and covert influence as threats to democratic resilience. Research also notes links between Russian influence channels and local extremist actors in parts of Europe. 

The strongest defence is not censorship of every falsehood.

It is trustworthy institutions that publish evidence quickly and admit errors.

---

# 24. Political violence is often statistically small but democratically large

An assault on a politician, journalist, migrant, religious site or party office may involve few victims.

Its political impact is much larger.

It communicates:

- some views cannot be safely expressed;
- some citizens are legitimate targets;
- public participation carries physical risk;
- retaliation is acceptable.

Political violence also produces feedback.

Each side uses attacks against it to prove that the opponent is inherently violent, while minimizing attacks committed by allies.

The essential democratic rule is consistency:

> An attack is unacceptable regardless of the victim’s party, nationality, religion, sex or ideology.

Selective condemnation is not condemnation. It is partisan signalling.

---

# 25. Repression alone cannot defeat radicalization

Police and intelligence work are essential when individuals:

- prepare violence;
- finance terrorism;
- recruit;
- threaten;
- possess weapons;
- incite criminal attacks.

But radicalization is not only a policing problem.

People can move toward extremism through:

- social isolation;
- identity crisis;
- humiliation;
- grievance;
- online belonging;
- trauma;
- prison networks;
- perceived injustice;
- desire for status or adventure.

Most people experiencing these conditions do not become violent.

Prevention must identify the combination of vulnerability, ideology and mobilization.

Effective programmes require:

- family support;
- schools;
- mental-health services;
- community organizations;
- credible former extremists;
- prison intervention;
- digital investigation;
- individualized risk assessment.

Treating entire communities as suspect can increase the alienation that recruiters exploit.

---

# 26. Schools teach democratic rules more easily than democratic disagreement

Students learn:

- the Constitution;
- elections;
- institutions;
- rights.

They receive less practical training in:

- testing evidence;
- identifying manipulation;
- arguing without humiliation;
- understanding statistical risk;
- distinguishing a group from an individual;
- changing one’s mind publicly;
- engaging with morally serious opposing views.

Media literacy programmes often focus on detecting obviously false news.

The harder problem is misleading truth:

- a real video without context;
- a correct statistic with the wrong denominator;
- one crime presented as a population trend;
- an exceptional case treated as typical;
- a genuine quote edited to conceal the response.

Democratic resilience requires epistemic skills, not merely institutional knowledge.

---

# 27. “Censorship” and “hate speech” are both overused labels

Some people call any content moderation censorship.

Others call every offensive disagreement hate speech.

Both weaken the concepts.

Private platforms have rules and can remove lawful speech, but their enormous reach gives those rules public importance.

States may restrict certain expression under criminal and civil law, but must avoid vague standards that punish political dissent.

The hardest cases involve speech that is:

- legal;
- dehumanizing;
- misleading;
- repeatedly targeted;
- socially damaging;
- below the criminal threshold.

The response should often be:

- counterspeech;
- context;
- reduced algorithmic amplification;
- transparent moderation;
- user control;
- civil remedies;
- targeted enforcement of threats and harassment.

Criminal law should remain precise.

# 28. Testing the main claims

## “Spain is more divided than ever”

Spain experienced civil war, dictatorship, terrorism and attempted coups during the twentieth century.

**Verdict:** historically false if interpreted literally. Contemporary affective polarization is nevertheless serious.

## “The far right is taking over Spain”

Radical-right ideas have gained greater visibility and influence, especially on migration and identity.

**Verdict:** influence has grown; institutional takeover is not supported by current democratic evidence.

## “Spain is becoming communist”

Spain remains a market economy within the EU and eurozone, with extensive private ownership.

**Verdict:** political propaganda rather than an economic description.

## “All anti-immigration views are racist”

Citizens can legitimately favour lower migration or stronger border enforcement.

**Verdict:** false. Racism appears when individuals are assigned inferior status or collective guilt based on origin.

## “Talking about immigrant crime creates hate”

Suppressing relevant evidence can strengthen misinformation. Collective generalization can fuel hostility.

**Verdict:** data should be published with denominators and context.

## “Hate-crime figures prove hatred suddenly increased by 23.6%”

Recorded incidents rose by that amount in 2025.

**Verdict:** the rise is real in police data, but changes in reporting and detection also matter.

## “Social media causes extremism”

Platforms amplify some mechanisms of radicalization but do not determine user beliefs alone.

**Verdict:** major accelerator, incomplete cause.

## “Only the right spreads hate”

Hostility, dehumanization and justification of coercion can appear across ideologies.

**Verdict:** false, although different movements vary in scale, targets and violence.

## “Both extremes are exactly the same”

Different extremist movements have different histories, capabilities, objectives and current threat levels.

**Verdict:** false symmetry prevents accurate risk assessment.

## “Jihadist terrorism is exaggerated to attack Muslims”

Jihadist terrorism remains the main organized terrorist threat identified by Spanish authorities, with sustained arrests and investigations.

**Verdict:** the threat is real; collective suspicion of Muslims is unjustified.

# 29. What a serious anti-extremism strategy should do

## A. Publish transparent, disaggregated data

Authorities should report:

- offence;
- motive;
- location;
- victim profile;
- suspect profile;
- judicial result;
- reporting method.

Data should distinguish incidents, crimes, arrests, prosecutions and convictions.

## B. Create a rapid-rumour protocol

After events likely to trigger collective retaliation, authorities should quickly publish:

- confirmed facts;
- unknown facts;
- false circulating claims;
- investigation updates.

Silence creates a market for fabricated certainty.

## C. Track political dehumanization

Independent researchers should monitor public statements that:

- assign collective guilt;
- describe groups as contamination or invasion;
- legitimize political violence;
- deny opponents’ democratic rights.

The objective should be accountability, not criminalization of broad opinion.

## D. Make platforms expose amplification risks

Researchers and regulators need access to data showing:

- recommendation pathways;
- coordinated networks;
- bot activity;
- advertising;
- content amplification;
- rapid escalation around violent incidents.

## E. Protect targeted groups locally

Police and municipalities should maintain rapid contact with:

- mosques;
- synagogues;
- LGTBI organizations;
- migrant associations;
- party offices;
- journalists;
- religious communities.

## F. Develop intervention for vulnerable young people

Schools and youth services need pathways for cases involving:

- fascination with violence;
- extremist propaganda;
- isolation;
- threats;
- misogynist or racist obsession;
- terrorist symbolism.

Intervention should not automatically become criminal prosecution.

## G. Separate migration debate from ethnic accusation

Political parties should agree that:

- crime data can be discussed;
- border policy can be contested;
- collective retaliation is never legitimate;
- fabricated nationality claims must be corrected immediately.

## H. Reform political communication

Public broadcasters and parliamentary formats should create more structured debate in which:

- claims are documented;
- opponents receive response time;
- factual corrections are visible;
- interruptions are limited;
- trade-offs are required.

## I. Fund cross-group institutions

The strongest protection against hatred is often repeated cooperation around common interests:

- schools;
- sports;
- neighbourhood associations;
- unions;
- professional groups;
- voluntary services.

One-off “dialogue events” are less effective than sustained shared activity.

## J. Apply one standard to all political violence

Parties should condemn attacks before knowing whether the victim is an ally.

# 30. What should not be done

## Do not classify broad political opposition as extremism

This turns anti-extremism policy into ideological policing.

## Do not hide uncomfortable statistics

Suppression strengthens conspiracy entrepreneurs.

## Do not publish nationality without relevance

This can convert individual offences into collective blame.

## Do not treat every radicalized person as mentally ill

Mental distress may contribute, but ideology and agency remain important.

## Do not depend only on content removal

Removed communities can migrate to less visible platforms.

## Do not create false balance

Threat assessment must follow evidence, not a requirement to accuse each side equally.

## Do not allow political parties to define extremism for their opponents

Criteria must be behavioural and democratic:

- support for violence;
- rejection of pluralism;
- collective dehumanization;
- coercive exclusion;
- destruction of constitutional rights.

---

# 31. What success should be measured by

Spain should track:

- hate crimes and reporting rates;
- prosecution and conviction outcomes;
- threats against public figures and journalists;
- political violence;
- terrorist recruitment and disruption;
- school bullying based on identity;
- trust across political groups;
- willingness to accept an opposing-party government;
- belief in verified conspiracy claims;
- intergroup social contact;
- exposure to ideologically diverse information;
- speed of official correction after viral rumours.

A reduction in offensive posts alone would not prove reduced extremism.

The goal is lower willingness to harm, exclude or deny rights to opponents.

---

# Final verdict

Spain’s social tension is serious, but it should not be exaggerated into a narrative of imminent national collapse.

The country remains:

- electorally democratic;
- socially plural;
- comparatively peaceful;
- capable of mass protest without mass violence;
- institutionally effective against terrorism.

But several protective norms are weakening.

Recorded hate crime rose sharply in 2025. Jihadist radicalization continues to require extensive police intervention. Far-right violent networks remain small but real. Online communities can transform local crime into national ethnic confrontation. Political leaders increasingly describe ordinary opponents in exceptional, anti-democratic language. 

The greatest present risk is not that extremists directly win a majority.

It is that mainstream politics adopts their operating system:

- every problem has an enemy group;
- compromise is betrayal;
- institutions are legitimate only when they agree;
- isolated incidents prove collective guilt;
- opponents are not merely wrong but dangerous;
- fear is more valuable than accuracy.

Spain’s defence against extremism cannot be a demand for everyone to become centrist or polite.

It must protect strong disagreement while rebuilding three limits:

1. **Individuals are responsible for their own acts; communities are not collectively guilty.**
2. **Political opponents retain rights and democratic legitimacy.**
3. **No cause becomes legitimate through intimidation, dehumanization or violence.**

The real test is not whether Spanish society stops arguing.

It is whether citizens can continue arguing without needing to imagine that half the country must be defeated, silenced or expelled before Spain can survive.

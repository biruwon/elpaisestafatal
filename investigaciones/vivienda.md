

# Spain’s housing crisis: the shortage is not where the empty homes are

### Prices are rising again at double-digit rates. Young adults cannot leave home. Renters cannot save enough to buy. Spain nevertheless has millions of vacant or underused dwellings. What explains the contradiction?

## The central finding

Spain does not simply lack houses.

It lacks **affordable, habitable and permanently available homes in the places where population, employment and education are growing**.

The crisis is produced by five mechanisms working together:

1. Household formation and migration are increasing demand rapidly in a limited number of metropolitan and coastal areas.
2. Construction responds slowly because developable land, licences, infrastructure, workers and financing cannot expand quickly.
3. Spain has almost no permanent public or social rental stock capable of buffering the market.
4. Existing homes are increasingly divided among permanent residences, second homes, tourist accommodation, seasonal rentals and properties held by non-residents.
5. Rising prices reward existing owners and make it harder for non-owners to accumulate the deposit required to buy, reproducing inequality across generations.

Tourism, foreign buyers, large landlords and rent regulation all affect the outcome. But none, by itself, explains the national crisis.

---

## The numbers that define the emergency

Spain’s House Price Index rose **12.9% year on year in the first quarter of 2026**. Prices of existing homes increased 13.5%, while new homes rose 9.1%. The quarterly increase alone was 3.5%. 

During 2025, Spain added approximately **240,000 net households**, while only around **92,000 newly constructed homes were completed**. The Banco de España estimates that the cumulative difference between household creation and completed homes reached roughly **750,000 between 2021 and 2025**. 

That number is not a literal count of families without roofs. Some demand is absorbed by vacant homes, subdivisions, conversions and changes in household size. It is nevertheless the clearest indicator of how much faster demand is moving than new construction.

The 2025 imbalance is stark:



At the same time:

- Around **7.6% of people aged 16 or over searched for another home during 2025 but did not move**. Nearly seven in ten identified excessive prices as the reason. 
- An estimated **65.6% of people aged 18–34 lived in the family home in 2023**, about 16 percentage points above the EU average. 
- Around three quarters of renting households would either lack the savings needed to cover purchase costs or would exceed a 35% mortgage-payment-to-income threshold. 

This is no longer primarily a problem of mortgage repossessions, as in the post-2008 crisis. It is an **access crisis**: access to rent, ownership, independence and the locations where economic opportunities exist.

---

# 1. Demand accelerated faster than Spain expected

## More people, but also more households per person

Spain’s population reached approximately **49.7 million in April 2026**, increasing by nearly 100,000 people during the first quarter alone. Recent population growth has depended heavily on international migration. 

The number of households has grown even faster than the political debate often acknowledges. Spain had more than **19.5 million households in early 2025**, almost one million more than in 2021. 

Housing demand is driven by households, not merely population. Demand rises when:

- A young adult leaves their parents.
- A couple separates into two households.
- A migrant household arrives.
- An elderly person remains alone in a large dwelling.
- A student or temporary worker needs accommodation in another city.

Spain is therefore experiencing both population growth and changes in household composition. Smaller households require more homes for the same population.

## Demand is geographically concentrated

The new demand is not distributed evenly across Spain. It concentrates around Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Málaga, Alicante, the islands and other areas with strong employment, universities, tourism or international connectivity.

The Banco de España estimated that approximately half of the 2022–2024 gap between household creation and new construction was concentrated in just five provinces: Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Alicante and Málaga. 

This produces Spain’s central housing paradox:

> A vacant apartment in a shrinking inland municipality does not solve the shortage of a nurse’s apartment in Málaga, a programmer’s home in Madrid or a student’s room in Barcelona.

---

# 2. Construction is not responding fast enough

## The land exists on paper

Spain is often described as having no developable land. The Banco de España reaches a more precise conclusion: urban plans contain potential capacity for roughly **seven million additional homes**, including about 1.1 million in the six largest urban areas. 

The bottleneck is converting theoretical capacity into completed homes.

Land must pass through planning, reparcelling, infrastructure, environmental assessment, licensing, financing and construction. Around **71% of municipalities had not fully revised their general urban plan since 2008**. Sector estimates cited by the Banco de España indicate that transformation processes can take 10–15 years and sometimes more than 20. 

The “build more” slogan therefore conceals a decade-long administrative and industrial pipeline.

## A fragmented, low-productivity industry

Approximately **95% of residential construction companies employ fewer than ten workers**. This fragmentation makes investment in technology, prefabrication, training and large projects more difficult. 

Spanish construction productivity fell by around **20% between 1995 and 2023**, according to the Banco de España’s analysis. Meanwhile, the residential construction-cost index rose by approximately 35% between early 2020 and late 2025. 

By the final quarter of 2025, **63% of construction and property companies cited worker shortages as a constraint**, while only 7.9% reported insufficient demand. Construction employment represented about 7% of total social-security affiliation in early 2026, compared with 11.5% around 1999. 

The sector is being asked to expand dramatically with fewer workers, weak productivity, rising costs and long approval times.

## Developers face a risky calculation

A housing development requires capital years before the first home is sold or rented. During that interval:

- Interest and material costs can change.
- Planning permission can be contested.
- Local infrastructure may be delayed.
- Regulations may change.
- Expected rents or sale prices may no longer cover the project.

This encourages projects aimed at higher-income buyers, where margins can absorb uncertainty. The homes most urgently needed—low-cost rentals—are often the least commercially attractive to construct without public land, guarantees or subsidy.

---

# 3. Spain dismantled its affordable-housing shock absorber

## The most important policy failure

Only about **1.5% of Spain’s principal residences belong to the public or social housing stock**. The corresponding OECD average is approximately 7%. France, the United Kingdom, Austria and the Netherlands maintain much larger buffers. 



This was not inevitable. Between 1991 and 2015, more than **1.3 million protected homes** were constructed with public support. But many were sold to private households under protections that eventually expired. They did not remain a permanent public rental asset. 

Spain repeatedly paid to create affordable housing and then allowed much of that affordability to disappear.

Countries with durable social-housing systems retain the property or maintain permanent price restrictions. The same dwelling continues serving successive generations. In Spain, public support frequently subsidised one purchaser and then became private market wealth.

## Why this changes the whole market

A substantial permanent housing stock allows the state to:

- House lower-income households without competing for private rentals.
- Offer young people and key workers a stable intermediate option.
- Respond to displacement in high-demand areas.
- Maintain affordable rents through downturns and booms.
- Negotiate regeneration without immediately expelling residents.

With only 1.5%, Spain enters each housing shock almost entirely dependent on the private market.

---

# 4. The existing stock cannot simply be “unlocked”

## “Spain has 3.8 million empty homes”

The figure is approximately correct but commonly misused.

The last broad estimate identified around **3.8 million vacant dwellings in 2020**. Yet only approximately 400,000 were located in municipalities with more than 250,000 inhabitants. Some are in shrinking regions, some require major rehabilitation, some are tied up in inheritance disputes, and others are not economically usable. 

In Madrid, the maximum potentially available existing stock was estimated at only around 9.9%; in Barcelona, 11.9%, compared with 27.1% nationally. These figures include categories beyond immediately habitable empty homes and should be read as an upper boundary, not ready-to-rent inventory. 

Vacancy policies can help, particularly where a usable dwelling is being deliberately withheld in a high-demand area. They cannot transport obsolete or poorly located homes to labour markets hundreds of kilometres away.

## Conversions have already absorbed part of the pressure

Between 2021 and 2025, residential use increased by approximately 1.2 million dwellings. The Banco de España estimates that at least 60% came from existing properties previously used as second homes, vacant dwellings or other non-primary residences rather than newly built homes. 

Spain has therefore already been “mobilising” existing stock. The remaining reserve in the most pressured markets is becoming increasingly limited.

---

# 5. Tourism is an amplifier, not the complete explanation

Tourist accommodation matters greatly in specific neighbourhoods and cities. It does not explain the entire national shortage.

The number of tourist dwellings approached 400,000 during 2021–2024 and averaged approximately **355,000 in 2025**. That represents only around 1.5% of Spain’s total housing stock, but close to **10% of the conventional rental market**—a much more relevant denominator. 

In tourist centres, its share can exceed 40% of the local rental stock. A national average therefore understates the impact in central Málaga, Barcelona, Sevilla, Palma, the Canary Islands and particular coastal municipalities. 

The causal effect is strongest where:

1. The neighbourhood is attractive to visitors.
2. Conventional rental supply is already scarce.
3. Tourist revenue significantly exceeds residential rent.
4. Enforcement of licences and community rules is weak.

Removing every tourist dwelling would not close Spain’s estimated national housing flow gap. But restricting them in saturated neighbourhoods can materially increase residential availability and reduce displacement.

The correct verdict is:

**Airbnb did not create Spain’s national supply problem, but short-term rentals intensify it in precisely some of the places where the social damage is greatest.**

---

# 6. Foreign buyers matter locally, especially on the coast

Purchases by non-residents have remained around 60,000 a year since 2022 and represented approximately **8.4% of house purchases in 2024**. More than half a million homes are estimated to be owned by foreign-born non-residents, around 2% of the national stock. 

The national percentage is limited, but Alicante, Málaga, the Balearic Islands and certain coastal markets have much higher concentrations.

Combining tourist accommodation and homes owned by foreign non-residents produces an estimated 900,000 dwellings in 2025, about 3.3% of Spain’s housing stock. In Alicante and Málaga, their combined presence was approximately 14%. 

This demand can support employment and local tax revenue. It can also disconnect local house prices from local salaries.

A national ban would be badly targeted. Measures based on local scarcity, non-primary-residence use, taxation and planning would address the actual geography more accurately.

---

# 7. Large investors are visible, but small landlords dominate

Investment funds are politically salient because their transactions are large, professionally managed and sometimes concentrated in vulnerable neighbourhoods. Some acquired distressed portfolios and public properties after the financial crisis.

But the national rental market remains highly fragmented.

Banco de España estimates indicate that approximately **92% of principal rental homes are owned by individuals**, while legal entities own around 8%. Individuals with more than ten rental properties represented, at most, approximately 7% of the market covered by the analysis. 

This does not make concentration harmless. A company can strongly influence a particular development or district without owning much of Spain’s total stock.

But blaming “the funds” for the entire crisis confuses a visible actor with the underlying market structure. Even after excluding large investors, the basic shortage, slow construction and tiny public stock remain.

---

# 8. Credit is supporting demand—but this is not 2007 again

Easier financing conditions, income growth and employment have supported purchases. Housing transactions, credit and residential investment all increased during 2025. 

However, the Banco de España does not currently identify the type of broad macrofinancial imbalance that preceded the 2008 crash. Mortgage standards remain materially more prudent, household indebtedness is lower, and the housing boom has not been driven by the same uncontrolled credit expansion. 

That distinction is important:

- **2007:** excessive construction plus excessive leverage.
- **2026:** insufficient well-located supply plus strong household demand.

Prices can still correct. Individual buyers can still overpay. But waiting for a 2008-style collapse is not a housing strategy because the underlying metropolitan scarcity may persist even through a recession.

---

# 9. The crisis is creating a hereditary housing system

For households that do not already own a home, the Banco de España estimates that a purchase would require an average of **6.8 years of total net household income**, before normal consumption expenditure. The figure reaches approximately ten years in Málaga, 9.7 in Madrid and nine in Barcelona. 



For young people still living with their parents, the estimated national burden reaches 8.3 years. It rises to 13.8 years in Madrid and 12.3 in Barcelona. 

This creates two different housing systems.

**Households with property or parental assistance** can convert accumulated wealth into a deposit and benefit from future appreciation.

**Households without family wealth** pay high rents, accumulate savings more slowly and face house prices rising faster than their deposit.

Employment income alone becomes less decisive. Access increasingly depends on inheritance, gifts, parental guarantees or whether a family happens to own a home in the right city.

---

# 10. Rent control: useful brake, incomplete treatment

The argument is often reduced to two absolute claims:

- Rent controls always work.
- Rent controls always destroy supply.

The evidence from Catalonia is more complicated.

Research on the 2020 Catalan rent cap found an average reduction in regulated rents of approximately 5%. Another study found no statistically significant immediate reduction in rental supply under the initial policy. 

More recent Banco de España evidence shows a decline in the rate at which properties entered or rotated through the conventional rental market—from around 13.3% in 2020 to 11.3% in 2024—alongside higher prices for newly entering contracts. This is compatible with a tightening of available residential supply, although regulation is not the only possible cause: owners can switch to seasonal rentals, sell, retain tenants for longer or respond to broader market conditions. 

The most defensible conclusion is:

- Caps can protect tenants and moderate regulated rents.
- Poorly designed rules can encourage conversion to unregulated categories.
- Controls are more sustainable when paired with enforcement, long-term contracts, tax alignment and rapid expansion of public or affordable supply.
- Rent regulation distributes scarce housing differently; it does not create additional homes.

---

# 11. Testing the popular explanations

## “It is all immigration”

Migration increases housing demand and helps explain why household creation has accelerated. But migrants did not determine Spain’s slow planning system, sell off its protected housing stock or create its construction productivity problem.

Migrants are also disproportionately renters and therefore among the groups most exposed to the shortage. The estimated purchase burden for foreign-born non-owner households is approximately 7.6 years of net income, above the national figure. 

**Verdict:** a demand contributor, not a sufficient explanation—and often used to divert attention from the failure to expand supply.

## “It is all Airbnb”

Tourist rentals can dominate specific central neighbourhoods but remain a small share of the total national stock.

**Verdict:** powerful local amplifier; incomplete national explanation.

## “It is all investment funds”

Large investors can influence individual areas and have sometimes benefited from public asset sales. But individuals own the overwhelming majority of rental dwellings.

**Verdict:** important for concentration, conduct and accountability; not the root national imbalance.

## “There are millions of empty homes, so no construction is needed”

Most vacant stock is not located where the strongest demand exists, and part is unsuitable or unavailable.

**Verdict:** mobilisation and rehabilitation should be used, but the headline number is geographically misleading.

## “Just build more”

Spain must build more—but quantity alone does not guarantee affordability. New construction aimed at luxury purchasers, second homes or tourist demand can raise total stock without adequately serving local workers.

**Verdict:** necessary but insufficient. Tenure, location, price and permanent protection matter.

## “Price controls alone will solve it”

They can slow increases and protect current tenants, but loopholes and shrinking conventional supply can undermine them.

**Verdict:** a defensive policy, not a complete production system.

# 12. What an effective housing strategy would look like

## Immediate: stop the emergency worsening

Over the next two years, policy should concentrate on keeping homes in the long-term residential market:

- Enforce tourist and seasonal-rental registration in high-pressure areas.
- Close the regulatory gap that allows ordinary residential leases to be disguised as temporary contracts.
- Accelerate rehabilitation of demonstrably habitable vacant homes in high-demand municipalities.
- Expand eviction prevention, mediation and targeted rental support for vulnerable households.
- Require publication of comparable municipal data on licences, completions, rent contracts and changes of use.

Broad buyer subsidies or guarantees should be treated carefully. Where supply is rigid, increasing purchasing power can be capitalised into higher prices.

## Medium term: make supply capable of reacting

The most productive reforms would not simply “liberalise land.” They would shorten the whole delivery chain:

- Create binding deadlines and digital tracking for planning and licences.
- Fund water, transport, schools and utilities alongside new developments.
- Establish pre-approved building systems and standardised technical requirements.
- Expand vocational training and legal migration routes for construction skills.
- Support industrialised and modular production while measuring actual cost reductions.
- Convert suitable offices, hotels and obsolete commercial properties.
- Use public land through long leases rather than irreversible sales.

Municipalities should receive part of the fiscal benefit from development so that approving homes does not merely impose infrastructure costs on local budgets.

## Long term: build a permanent public alternative

The objective should be measured as **net additions to permanently affordable rental homes**, not subsidies announced or temporary protected units initiated.

A credible programme would:

1. Retain public ownership of public land.
2. Make affordability restrictions permanent.
3. Use independent waiting lists and allocation audits.
4. Combine construction, acquisition and rehabilitation.
5. Place key-worker and lower-income housing near employment and transport.
6. Publish project-level costs, delivery dates and occupancy.

Reaching even the OECD average of around 7% would require well over one million additional public or social homes. This cannot be delivered during one parliamentary term. It requires a 15–20 year institutional commitment.

# 13. Does the current government plan match the diagnosis?

The State Housing Plan for 2026–2030 has a stated budget of **€7 billion**. It includes funding for construction and acquisition, stronger permanent-protection provisions and maximum aid that can reach €85,000 per dwelling in relevant programmes. 

These are meaningful improvements, especially permanent protection. The previous model lost too many publicly supported homes to the private market.

But the plan should be judged against annual delivery:

- Protected homes completed.
- Net homes added to permanent public rental stock.
- Average time from funding to occupation.
- Cost per completed dwelling.
- Location relative to demand and transport.
- Number of properties leaving tourist or temporary use.
- Share of lower-income applicants housed.

In 2025, only around 11,000 protected homes were completed and 15,500 were started. That is an improvement on the preceding decade but far below the scale suggested by the estimated flow gap. 

The danger is that large euro figures create an impression of immediate action while planning and construction delays postpone the actual homes for years.

---

# 14. What the evidence does—and does not—prove

Several data limitations should be visible on the eventual webpage.

**The 750,000 figure is an imbalance indicator.** It compares household formation with completed new homes. It does not perfectly account for conversions, demolitions, vacancies, second homes, household sharing or location.

**Rental data are weaker than purchase data.** Advertised rents measure new offers rather than what all tenants pay. Administrative contract data arrive slowly and definitions differ between autonomous communities.

**Vacancy data are old and incomplete.** A dwelling classified as empty may be uninhabitable, undergoing inheritance proceedings or temporarily unused.

**National averages conceal local crises.** Tourism and non-resident purchases may be modest nationally and dominant within individual neighbourhoods.

**Causality is difficult.** A landlord may leave conventional renting because of regulation, tourist demand, expectations of appreciation, tax treatment, tenant risk or a decision to sell. Observing a reduction does not identify one cause automatically.

A serious investigation must publish these caveats rather than presenting every estimate as an exact count.

---

# Final verdict

Spain’s housing crisis is fundamentally a **coordination and capacity failure**.

The country allowed its population and economy to concentrate in successful urban areas without creating a housing system capable of following them. It relied on private construction while making development slow and risky. It built protected homes without retaining them permanently. It treated rental policy, urban planning, transport, tourism and taxation as separate subjects even though they compete for the same square metres.

The crisis is then intensified locally by tourist accommodation, foreign non-resident demand, seasonal rentals and investor concentration. But removing any one of these would not repair the underlying system.

The decisive test for future policy is not whether Spain announces more money, grants more buyer assistance or approves more theoretical land.

It is whether the country produces, year after year, **more permanently affordable homes in the municipalities where ordinary people actually need to live than the number of new households those places are creating**.

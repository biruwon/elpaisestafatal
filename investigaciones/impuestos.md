# Taxes in Spain: Spaniards do not pay the most in Europe, but employment is taxed heavily and the system is unusually complex

### Spain raises less tax than the highest-tax European welfare states, but more than the OECD average. The principal problem is not simply the amount collected: it is that formal employment bears a large burden, consumption taxes contain extensive exceptions, regional rules differ sharply, tax benefits are poorly evaluated and citizens struggle to connect what they pay with the services they receive.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

The slogans surrounding Spanish taxation are mostly incomplete.

Spain is neither a low-tax country nor one of Europe’s highest-tax states.

Its tax-to-GDP ratio was **37.3% in 2023**, above the OECD average of 33.9% but below the tax burden of countries such as France, Denmark and Austria. The latest European data place the EU average close to 39.4% of GDP in 2024. 

But the aggregate percentage hides the central structural problem:

> **Spain taxes formal labour relatively heavily, collects comparatively little through some consumption and environmental taxes, maintains a vast system of exemptions and deductions, and distributes tax authority across governments whose rules can differ substantially.**

Spain’s tax debate therefore combines four different disputes:

1. How much revenue should the state raise?
2. Who should provide that revenue?
3. Which activities should be encouraged or discouraged?
4. Does the public sector deliver enough value in return?

A tax can be economically efficient but socially regressive. Another can be progressive but easy to avoid. A deduction can help one household while rewarding behaviour that would have happened anyway.

The relevant question is not simply whether taxes should rise or fall.

It is:

> **Which taxes impose the least damage, distribute the burden fairly and finance services citizens actually value?**

---

# 1. How much tax does Spain collect?

The Spanish Tax Agency collected **€325.36 billion in 2025**, 10.4% more than in 2024.

The increase reflected:

- approximately 7% growth in the main tax bases;
- rising employment and wages;
- stronger business and capital income;
- inflation’s effect on nominal values;
- regulatory and administrative measures adding an estimated €7.82 billion.

Personal income-tax revenue grew by 10.1%, while direct taxes overall increased by 11.4%. 

These figures do not represent every public revenue source.

They mainly cover taxes administered by the Tax Agency. The full tax burden also includes:

- Social Security contributions;
- regional taxes;
- municipal taxes;
- taxes collected under the Basque and Navarrese systems;
- various fees and compulsory charges.

Tax revenue often rises faster than real economic growth because inflation and nominal wage increases enlarge taxable income and spending.

A government can therefore achieve record collection without announcing a broad increase in statutory rates.

---

# 2. Spain is not the highest-tax country in Europe

Countries with larger welfare states often collect more.

In 2024, the highest EU tax-to-GDP ratios included:

- Denmark: 45.2%;
- France: 43.5%;
- Austria: 43.4%.

The EU average was 39.4%. 

Spain’s ratio is lower than these countries but higher than the OECD average.

This allows opposing political camps to select different comparisons:

- The left compares Spain with France or Scandinavia and concludes that revenue remains insufficient.
- The right compares Spain with the OECD average or lower-tax European economies and concludes that the burden is excessive.

Both can cite accurate numbers.

The disagreement is really about the model of state Spain wants.

Higher-tax countries generally finance more extensive combinations of:

- childcare;
- income support;
- pensions;
- healthcare;
- education;
- dependency care;
- active employment services.

A tax comparison without a spending comparison is incomplete.

---

# 3. The tax wedge shows why employees feel heavily taxed

The **tax wedge** is the difference between the total cost of employing a worker and the amount that worker receives after income tax and employee contributions.

It includes:

- personal income tax;
- employee Social Security contributions;
- employer Social Security contributions;
- relevant family cash benefits.

For a single worker earning the average wage, Spain’s tax wedge was **41.44% in 2025**. 

This means that when employment costs an employer €100, substantially less than €60 reaches the worker as disposable salary under the OECD’s standardized assumptions.

Not all of the difference appears on the payslip.

Employer contributions are paid above the employee’s gross wage. Economically, however, they remain part of the cost of employing that person and can influence:

- hiring;
- salary negotiations;
- formalization;
- automation;
- relocation of activity.

Calling employer contributions “free money paid by the company” is misleading.

Calling them pure confiscation is also misleading because they finance pensions, unemployment protection and other benefits.

---

# 4. Low- and middle-income families receive weak tax support compared with some peers

Spain’s labour taxation is especially problematic when combined with limited family relief.

The OECD has highlighted that Spain’s tax wedge remains high for lower-income households with children compared with many countries that provide larger:

- child allowances;
- refundable tax credits;
- contribution reductions;
- in-work benefits. 

This creates a difficult zone where a household can be:

- too well paid for some means-tested support;
- not wealthy enough to absorb housing and childcare costs;
- subject to substantial employee and employer contributions.

Spain’s system is effective at collecting from formal wages.

It is less effective at ensuring that taking additional work always produces a strong improvement in household disposable income.

---

# 5. Employer contributions are one of Spain’s least understood taxes

An employee usually sees:

- gross salary;
- employee deductions;
- net salary.

They may not see the employer’s additional Social Security cost as part of their compensation.

But employers base hiring decisions on total cost.

Suppose a worker receives a gross annual salary of €30,000.

The employer may face several thousand euros more in contributions. The worker then pays employee contributions and income tax from the €30,000.

This produces three different values:

1. total labour cost;
2. gross salary;
3. net income.

Political comparisons frequently use whichever value supports the argument.

A salary can look high in employer-cost terms and modest in net household terms.

---

# 6. Personal income tax is progressive—but inflation can raise it without a real increase in purchasing power

IRPF uses progressive brackets.

A common misunderstanding is that moving into a higher bracket causes the entire salary to be taxed at that higher rate.

It does not.

Only the portion of income above each threshold is taxed at the next marginal rate.

But inflation creates another problem: **fiscal drag**.

If a salary rises 4% while prices also rise 4%, the worker has not gained real purchasing power. Yet the higher nominal salary may:

- move part of the income into a higher bracket;
- reduce eligibility for deductions or benefits;
- increase the average effective tax rate.

Unless brackets, allowances and benefit thresholds are adjusted, the state collects more even without a statutory rate increase.

This partly explains Spain’s rapid growth in income-tax receipts during the inflationary period.

---

# 7. Deflating income-tax brackets is not automatically progressive

The right often proposes adjusting all IRPF brackets for inflation.

This prevents hidden tax increases.

But a uniform percentage adjustment can produce larger euro savings for higher earners because they have more taxable income.

The left sometimes opposes broad deflation for that reason.

A targeted solution could adjust:

- personal allowances;
- lower and middle brackets;
- family minima;
- benefit thresholds;

while limiting relief at the top.

The debate should therefore not be framed as:

- deflation equals fairness;
- no deflation equals progressivity.

The distribution depends on the design.

---

# 8. Regional governments create large differences in income taxation

Autonomous communities control part of IRPF and can set:

- regional brackets;
- rates;
- family deductions;
- rental deductions;
- birth deductions;
- rural-residence incentives;
- education or health-related relief.

The Ministry of Finance’s 2026 survey documents a wide range of autonomous deductions and rates. It also shows that regional tax systems increasingly use targeted deductions for rent, depopulation, family circumstances, donations and other policy objectives. 

As a result, taxpayers with identical gross income and family status can face different liabilities depending on residence.

This is partly the purpose of fiscal autonomy: regions can choose different priorities.

But it creates three concerns:

- complexity;
- tax competition;
- unequal treatment.

Madrid can use lower taxation as part of its economic strategy. Other regions argue that Madrid benefits from concentration of headquarters, institutions and wealth while reducing taxes on mobile taxpayers.

There is no politically neutral solution. Fiscal autonomy necessarily permits divergence.

---

# 9. The Basque Country and Navarre operate under distinct fiscal systems

The Basque Country and Navarre collect most taxes through their own administrations and transfer an agreed contribution to the central state for common services.

This system is constitutionally and historically distinct from ordinary regional financing.

Supporters argue that it provides:

- accountability;
- fiscal responsibility;
- institutional continuity;
- alignment between spending and collection.

Critics argue that the calculation of contributions and territorial resources can produce unequal financing.

The problem is often debated through identity rather than transparent fiscal evidence.

A credible evaluation requires publication of:

- taxable capacity;
- revenue raised;
- common state services;
- contribution methodology;
- expenditure needs;
- comparable per-capita financing.

---

# 10. VAT is Spain’s largest everyday invisible tax

VAT is included in the price of most consumption.

Spain uses:

- a standard rate;
- reduced rates;
- super-reduced rates;
- exemptions.

The social logic is to tax essential goods and services less heavily.

But VAT reductions create several problems:

- wealthy households also benefit;
- classification boundaries become complex;
- businesses lobby for favourable rates;
- lost revenue can be difficult to measure;
- lowering a rate may not be passed fully to consumers.

Spain has the **largest VAT policy gap in the EU**, estimated at 59.1%.

This does not mean that 59.1% of VAT is stolen.

The policy gap estimates revenue deliberately not collected because of reduced rates and exemptions compared with applying the standard rate broadly. 

That distinction is vital.

The VAT **compliance gap** concerns fraud, evasion, insolvency and collection failure.

The **policy gap** mainly reflects political choices.

---

# 11. Reduced VAT rates are a costly way to help low-income households

A reduced rate on food benefits poor households because they spend a large share of income on food.

But higher-income households usually spend more euros in total and may therefore receive a larger absolute benefit.

A targeted cash transfer can often deliver more assistance per euro of revenue lost.

Reduced VAT remains attractive politically because:

- it is automatic;
- no application is required;
- consumers see it at purchase;
- it avoids administrative exclusion.

The best approach may combine:

- reduced rates for a narrow group of essentials;
- targeted income support;
- regular evaluation of whether businesses pass reductions through.

Temporary VAT reductions should have explicit end dates and price-monitoring plans.

---

# 12. Spain taxes consumption less than many European peers—but that does not mean VAT feels low

The European Commission and OECD both identify relatively weak Spanish revenue from consumption taxation compared with peer countries. 

This can coexist with households feeling heavily taxed at the supermarket because:

- VAT is visible in nearly every purchase;
- lower-income households consume most of their income;
- excise taxes increase fuel, electricity, tobacco and alcohol prices;
- the standard VAT rate remains substantial even if exemptions reduce total revenue;
- housing costs leave little disposable income.

Consumption-tax burden depends not only on the statutory rate but on the share of household income that must be spent.

---

# 13. Corporate tax debates confuse statutory rates with actual payments

Spain’s general corporate-income-tax rate is higher than those of some European jurisdictions.

But statutory rates do not tell us what companies actually pay.

Effective taxation depends on:

- deductions;
- previous losses;
- depreciation;
- financing;
- foreign income;
- tax credits;
- group consolidation;
- sector-specific rules;
- minimum taxation;
- profit location.

A profitable domestic small company and a multinational group may face entirely different effective burdens.

Corporate-tax revenue can also be volatile because profits vary more sharply than wages or consumption.

The correct question is not merely:

> “What is the corporate tax rate?”

It is:

> “What proportion of genuine economic profit is ultimately taxed, where is it taxed and what investment behaviour does the system create?”

---

# 14. Taxing companies ultimately affects people

A corporation is the legal taxpayer, but the economic cost can fall partly on:

- shareholders through lower returns;
- workers through lower wages;
- consumers through higher prices;
- suppliers;
- future investment.

The distribution depends on:

- market competition;
- international mobility;
- labour bargaining;
- ownership;
- time horizon.

Claims that corporate taxes are paid only by wealthy shareholders are too simple.

Claims that every corporate-tax increase is paid entirely by workers are equally simplistic.

Tax incidence must be investigated empirically by sector.

---

# 15. Special taxes on banks and energy companies have political appeal—but design matters

Spain has introduced special or temporary levies affecting sectors including banks and energy companies.

The argument for such taxes is that extraordinary profits can arise from:

- interest-rate changes;
- energy-price shocks;
- regulated market structures;
- public support;
- limited competition.

The counterargument is that sector taxes can:

- reduce credit;
- discourage investment;
- be passed to customers;
- create legal uncertainty;
- tax revenue rather than actual excess profit.

Spain’s levy on certain financial institutions’ interest and commission margins remains part of the tax architecture in 2026. 

A well-designed windfall tax should identify profit generated by an exceptional external event.

A poorly designed one simply taxes politically unpopular companies.

---

# 16. Self-employed workers experience taxation through both income tax and contributions

The self-employed commonly complain that they pay tax even before achieving secure income.

Their burden can include:

- Social Security contributions;
- IRPF payments on account;
- VAT collection and reporting;
- accounting;
- advisers;
- cash-flow timing;
- local charges;
- sector regulations.

VAT collected from customers is not the worker’s income, but it creates cash-management and administrative obligations.

The income-based contribution system attempts to align contributions more closely with net earnings.

But “net earnings” for contribution purposes do not always correspond intuitively to actual disposable income.

The self-employed are extremely heterogeneous:

- high-income professionals;
- shopkeepers;
- dependent freelancers;
- tradespeople;
- platform workers;
- farmers;
- owners with employees.

One contribution regime cannot perfectly represent all of them.

---

# 17. The *módulos* system simplifies tax—but creates fairness concerns

Some self-employed activities can calculate taxable income through objective indicators rather than complete accounts.

This reduces administrative burden in sectors where detailed bookkeeping may be difficult.

But it creates risks:

- estimated income may diverge from real income;
- firms can remain artificially small;
- cash activity becomes harder to verify;
- similar businesses may pay different effective rates;
- the system can protect some taxpayers from taxation of actual profits.

The 2026 tax code continues to include the objective-estimation framework for eligible self-employed workers. 

Simplification is legitimate.

But simplification should not become systematic undertaxation.

---

# 18. Property is taxed repeatedly—but in different economic moments

Property taxation can include:

- VAT or transfer tax at purchase;
- stamp duty;
- municipal property tax;
- capital-gains tax;
- municipal land-value tax;
- income tax on rent;
- wealth tax;
- inheritance or donation tax.

Citizens often describe this as paying repeatedly for the same asset.

Legally, the taxes apply to different events:

- transaction;
- ownership;
- rental income;
- capital gain;
- transfer at death.

But the cumulative burden can be high and the structure confusing.

Spain also relies heavily on transaction taxes, which can discourage:

- moving for work;
- downsizing;
- buying and selling;
- efficient use of housing.

Economists frequently prefer recurrent taxation of land or property value over high taxes on transactions because recurrent taxes distort mobility less.

Politically, however, annual property taxes are much more visible and unpopular.

---

# 19. Property taxation often fails to distinguish land value from productive investment

A property’s value can rise because:

- the owner renovated it;
- local infrastructure improved;
- planning rules changed;
- population grew;
- public services expanded;
- scarcity intensified.

Taxing the return on a productive renovation may discourage investment.

Taxing land-value gains created by public decisions is economically more defensible.

Spain’s cadastral and municipal systems do not always capture this distinction effectively.

A better system would focus more on:

- underlying land value;
- vacant or underused property where justified;
- planning-created gains;
- regularly updated assessments.

It should reduce penalties on transactions that improve mobility or housing use.

---

# 20. Wealth tax is highly progressive—but geographically fragmented

Spain retains a recurrent wealth tax, with autonomous communities exercising substantial control through exemptions and bonuses.

Some regions historically reduced the effective charge dramatically, while others collected it more fully.

The state responded with the Solidarity Tax on Large Fortunes, which remained operational in 2026 and required electronic filing through model 718. 

Supporters argue that wealth taxation:

- reduces concentration;
- captures ability to pay beyond annual income;
- taxes assets benefiting from public stability;
- prevents regional elimination of taxation on the richest.

Critics argue that it:

- taxes assets from previously taxed income;
- can affect illiquid owners;
- encourages relocation;
- produces limited revenue;
- adds complexity;
- can penalize domestic investment.

The design question is whether the tax reaches genuine economic wealth without penalizing productive business assets or forcing taxpayers to sell illiquid property.

---

# 21. Inheritance tax is the clearest conflict between family freedom and equality of opportunity

Supporters of inheritance taxation argue:

- inherited wealth was not earned by the recipient;
- inheritance increasingly determines housing and life chances;
- taxing transfers is less damaging than taxing work;
- large untaxed inheritances reproduce inequality.

Opponents argue:

- the assets were already taxed;
- families save to support children;
- property-rich but cash-poor heirs may struggle;
- family businesses can be damaged;
- regional differences are unfair.

Spain’s autonomous communities apply very different deductions and bonuses, particularly for close relatives. The Ministry of Finance documents substantial regional variation in the 2026 system. 

A defensible reform would:

- strongly protect ordinary family homes and small inheritances;
- permit deferred payment for illiquid assets;
- protect genuine operating businesses subject to continuity conditions;
- tax very large transfers more consistently nationally.

---

# 22. Environmental taxation is low relative to Spain’s climate and energy needs

Spain collects comparatively little from environmental taxes relative to some peers, while maintaining significant dependence on labour taxation. 

Environmental taxes can make harmful behaviour more expensive:

- emissions;
- pollution;
- landfill;
- congestion;
- inefficient energy use.

But they can also be regressive.

A fuel tax affects a rural low-income worker more severely than a wealthy city resident with public transport.

The correct approach is not to reject environmental taxation.

It is to recycle part of the revenue through:

- household rebates;
- rural transport;
- home renovation;
- clean-vehicle alternatives;
- lower labour taxes.

A climate tax without an affordable alternative feels punitive because it often is.

---

# 23. Tax benefits are public spending hidden inside the tax system

A tax deduction and a direct subsidy can produce the same fiscal effect.

But deductions often receive less scrutiny.

Spain’s tax expenditures create an estimated annual revenue loss of around **4 percentage points of GDP**, according to the European Commission’s reference to AIReF estimates. 

They include:

- exemptions;
- reduced rates;
- deductions;
- allowances;
- special regimes;
- deferrals.

Some serve strong public objectives.

Others persist because beneficiaries are organized and the cost is dispersed.

AIReF has found that some tax incentives, including parts of the R&D system, show a substantial gap between potential and actual effectiveness. 

Every tax benefit should be treated as a budget programme:

- clear objective;
- annual cost;
- beneficiary distribution;
- evidence of additional behaviour;
- expiration date;
- independent evaluation.

---

# 24. A deduction often rewards people who would have acted anyway

Suppose a company receives a deduction for research.

The relevant question is not whether it conducted research.

It is whether the deduction caused **additional** research.

Likewise, a housing deduction can benefit people who would have purchased regardless.

A pension deduction can reward saving that would have occurred anyway.

Without counterfactual evaluation, governments confuse subsidized activity with policy impact.

This is one reason tax benefits survive: their cost is real but their failure is difficult to observe.

---

# 25. Tax fraud is real, but no precise “shadow economy bill” should be treated as proven

Fraud can involve:

- undeclared cash income;
- false invoices;
- hidden employment;
- offshore assets;
- VAT carousels;
- false residence;
- shell companies;
- abusive deductions;
- criminal tax evasion.

Estimating the total tax lost is difficult because the behaviour is hidden by definition.

Widely circulated figures about the “cost of fraud” often combine:

- estimates of the shadow economy;
- theoretical tax rates;
- assumptions about recoverability;
- different forms of noncompliance.

Not every euro of undeclared economic activity would generate the full theoretical tax amount if formalized.

This does not mean the problem is small.

It means precision should not be fabricated.

---

# 26. VAT fraud and VAT policy are frequently confused

The EU’s VAT reports distinguish:

- the compliance gap;
- the policy gap.

The compliance gap can reflect fraud, evasion, insolvency, errors and collection failure.

The policy gap reflects legal exemptions and reduced rates.

Spain’s exceptionally large policy gap is mainly a product of design choices—not evidence that Spanish businesses are uniquely fraudulent. 

This distinction matters because the solutions differ.

Fraud requires:

- audits;
- information exchange;
- electronic invoicing;
- enforcement.

The policy gap requires political decisions about rates and exemptions.

---

# 27. Digitalization has made tax collection more effective

Spain’s Tax Agency has developed extensive systems involving:

- third-party income reporting;
- pre-filled income-tax returns;
- electronic VAT information;
- international data exchange;
- digital filing;
- bank reporting;
- electronic invoicing.

The OECD recognizes progress in compliance and supports the planned expansion of electronic invoicing, including a national system expected by 2027. 

This reduces some forms of evasion.

It also creates privacy, complexity and cybersecurity concerns.

Digital enforcement should follow the principle that:

- taxpayers provide the same information once;
- the administration explains discrepancies clearly;
- small errors are distinguished from intentional fraud;
- appeal channels remain accessible.

---

# 28. Employees and small businesses feel monitored more closely than complex wealth

Salary income is relatively easy to tax because:

- employers report it;
- tax is withheld;
- payments enter banks;
- employment is registered.

Complex business and investment structures can involve:

- valuation disputes;
- multiple companies;
- international income;
- family ownership;
- deductions;
- timing strategies.

This creates a perception that ordinary employees pay automatically while wealthier taxpayers negotiate.

Sometimes that perception reflects actual differences in enforceability.

But large businesses and high-wealth individuals also face specialist audits and extensive reporting.

The underlying issue is capacity asymmetry.

Sophisticated taxpayers can hire advisers to use every lawful option and contest assessments for years. Ordinary workers cannot redesign salary income.

---

# 29. Legal avoidance and illegal evasion are not the same

Tax evasion violates the law.

Tax avoidance arranges affairs within legal rules to reduce taxation, although some aggressive arrangements may later be challenged as abusive.

Political debate often calls every low tax payment fraud.

That is inaccurate.

A company using a deduction enacted by parliament may be acting exactly as the law permits.

The correct response to an undesirable legal outcome is to change the law, not accuse the taxpayer of a crime without evidence.

But lawmakers should also acknowledge responsibility when they create exploitable rules.

---

# 30. Do wealthy people leave because of taxes?

Some individuals and companies change residence partly for tax reasons.

But migration decisions also depend on:

- employment;
- family;
- climate;
- language;
- markets;
- regulation;
- infrastructure;
- public services;
- lifestyle.

Claims that every tax increase causes a mass exodus are overstated.

Claims that high-income taxpayers never respond to tax differences are equally implausible.

Mobility is greatest among:

- internationally connected professionals;
- investors;
- business owners;
- people whose income can be relocated legally.

Tax design should therefore balance progressivity with:

- stable rules;
- broad bases;
- international cooperation;
- credible residence enforcement;
- limited opportunities for artificial relocation.

---

# 31. Tax competition among regions can discipline or destabilize the system

Supporters of regional tax competition argue that it:

- limits government excess;
- allows policy experimentation;
- attracts investment;
- gives voters alternatives.

Critics argue that it:

- favours wealthy regions;
- pressures governments to reduce progressive taxes;
- disconnects revenue from public-service needs;
- creates a race to the bottom.

Both effects can exist.

Competition may improve efficiency where residents and businesses can compare governments.

It becomes problematic when one region’s structural advantages allow it to attract tax bases generated across the entire country.

A minimum national floor can preserve autonomy while limiting elimination of selected taxes.

The Solidarity Tax on Large Fortunes partly reflects that logic.

---

# 32. Taxes finance transfers between generations

A large share of public revenue finances:

- pensions;
- healthcare;
- unemployment protection;
- education;
- debt interest;
- public employment;
- dependency care.

Spain’s ageing population means a growing portion of labour taxes and general revenue supports older citizens.

This is not inherently unfair.

Current retirees previously paid taxes and contributions, and social insurance deliberately redistributes across time.

But intergenerational tension rises when younger workers face:

- high contribution rates;
- expensive housing;
- uncertain pensions;
- weak family support;
- delayed independence.

Tax analysis must therefore examine not only income groups but generations and wealth.

A low-income pensioner and a wealthy property-owning retiree should not be treated as economically identical.

---

# 33. Social contributions are not personal savings accounts

Workers often assume that their contributions are saved for their own pensions.

Spain primarily operates a pay-as-you-go system.

Current contributions finance current benefits. Future workers will finance future pensions, supplemented by taxes and other revenues.

The relationship between contribution history and pension entitlement is real, but the money is not stored in an individual account.

This matters when evaluating the tax burden.

Social contributions are simultaneously:

- compulsory labour charges;
- financing for current social expenditure;
- the basis for future entitlement.

Their economic effect resembles taxation even where they are legally treated as contributions.

---

# 34. Citizens judge taxes through the quality of public services

The same effective burden can feel acceptable or excessive depending on:

- healthcare waiting times;
- education;
- childcare;
- pensions;
- public transport;
- policing;
- administrative speed;
- social protection.

A taxpayer paying substantial contributions while also buying:

- private health insurance;
- private childcare;
- private education;
- private pension saving;

may feel that they pay twice.

This does not prove that public provision has no value. Universal systems still provide risk protection and social stability.

But deteriorating access weakens tax legitimacy.

The political contract is not merely:

> “Pay because the law requires it.”

It is:

> “Pay because shared institutions provide reliable services and protection.”

---

# 35. Tax legitimacy also depends on visible fairness

People tolerate taxation less when they believe:

- political elites waste money;
- neighbours evade tax;
- large companies exploit loopholes;
- subsidies reward insiders;
- different regions receive unfair treatment;
- public procurement is corrupt.

Even a technically efficient tax can fail politically where trust is low.

Anti-corruption, spending evaluation and tax compliance are therefore part of the same legitimacy system.

Citizens compare what they pay not only with what they receive, but with what they believe others avoid.

---

# 36. Spain’s tax system is too complex

Complexity arises from:

- national rules;
- regional rules;
- municipal taxes;
- special regimes;
- deductions;
- temporary measures;
- sector-specific taxes;
- frequent legal changes;
- European requirements.

Complexity creates costs:

- advisers;
- filing time;
- errors;
- uncertainty;
- litigation;
- barriers to entrepreneurship;
- advantage for sophisticated taxpayers.

It can also conceal political choices.

A government can claim to maintain a tax rate while changing:

- deductions;
- bases;
- thresholds;
- valuation;
- withholding;
- exemptions.

The European Commission recommends simplifying and rebalancing Spain’s system. 

---

# 37. Simplicity and fairness can conflict

A single flat tax is simple.

But it may shift burden toward lower and middle incomes unless combined with a large allowance and transfers.

A highly progressive system can target ability to pay more precisely but requires more brackets, definitions and anti-avoidance rules.

The objective is not maximum simplicity.

It is the minimum complexity necessary to produce the desired distribution and prevent avoidance.

Every exception should have to justify its existence.

---

# 38. Testing the main political claims

## “Spain is a tax hell”

Spain’s tax burden is above the OECD average but below that of several large European welfare states.

**Verdict:** political exaggeration. Certain workers, businesses or transactions can nevertheless face a high effective burden.

## “Spain has low taxes”

Spain collects less than France or Denmark, but formal labour is taxed heavily.

**Verdict:** misleading as a general statement.

## “The employer pays Social Security, not the worker”

The employer transfers the payment, but the contribution forms part of labour cost and can influence wages and hiring.

**Verdict:** legally true, economically incomplete.

## “Entering a higher income-tax bracket makes you earn less”

Only the income above the threshold faces the higher marginal rate.

**Verdict:** normally false.

## “The government did not raise taxes; collection rose only because the economy grew”

Economic and tax-base growth explains much of the increase, but regulatory and management measures also contributed billions in 2025. 

**Verdict:** incomplete.

## “VAT reductions help the poor”

They help, but higher-income households also benefit and may receive more euros.

**Verdict:** true but inefficiently targeted.

## “Spain loses most VAT through fraud”

Spain’s very large VAT policy gap mainly reflects reduced rates and exemptions.

**Verdict:** false conflation.

## “Companies pay 25% of all profits”

Statutory and effective rates differ because of deductions, losses and international activity.

**Verdict:** false as a universal description.

## “The rich pay no tax”

High earners and asset owners contribute substantial revenue, but can access more planning opportunities and derive income taxed differently from wages.

**Verdict:** false literally; valid concern about effective-rate differences.

## “Inheritance tax makes families sell ordinary homes”

This can occur in particular liquidity situations, but regional deductions often protect close-family transfers significantly.

**Verdict:** possible, not representative of every inheritance.

## “Inheritance has already been taxed”

The asset may derive from previously taxed income, but the current taxable event is receipt by a new owner.

**Verdict:** rhetorically powerful but not a decisive economic argument.

## “Tax cuts always increase revenue”

Some cuts can expand activity, but rarely recover their entire fiscal cost automatically.

**Verdict:** generally false.

## “Higher taxes always improve public services”

Revenue enables services; management determines results.

**Verdict:** false without spending quality.

# 39. What a serious tax reform should prioritize

## A. Reduce the tax penalty on low and middle employment

Possible tools include:

- refundable employment credits;
- targeted contribution reductions;
- larger child benefits;
- gradual benefit withdrawal;
- stronger personal allowances.

The cost must be financed transparently.

## B. Review every reduced VAT rate and exemption

Each should be evaluated for:

- distribution;
- price pass-through;
- behavioural effect;
- administrative complexity;
- fiscal cost.

Essential goods can remain protected while ineffective privileges are removed.

## C. Shift some burden from transactions toward recurrent land value

Reduce taxes that discourage:

- moving;
- buying;
- selling;
- business transfer.

Strengthen fair recurrent taxation of land and planning-created gains.

## D. Harmonize minimum standards for wealth and inheritance taxation

Protect:

- ordinary homes;
- small inheritances;
- genuine family businesses;
- illiquid taxpayers through deferral.

Prevent complete regional elimination for the largest fortunes and transfers.

## E. Simplify corporate taxation while broadening the base

Reduce overlapping incentives and improve certainty.

Preserve support only where it generates demonstrable additional investment, innovation or employment.

## F. Evaluate tax benefits like public spending

Every benefit should have:

- budget cost;
- objective;
- beneficiaries;
- evaluation;
- expiration date.

## G. Complete electronic invoicing carefully

Use it to reduce fraud and administrative duplication—not to impose disproportionate costs on microbusinesses.

## H. Recycle environmental-tax revenue

Return part of it through:

- lower labour taxes;
- targeted household payments;
- public transport;
- energy renovation.

## I. Publish effective tax rates by income source

Citizens should be able to compare effective burdens on:

- employment;
- self-employment;
- dividends;
- capital gains;
- rental income;
- corporate profit;
- inheritances;
- wealth.

## J. Connect tax increases to measurable outcomes

Any major increase should specify:

- revenue expected;
- service or deficit objective;
- distributional impact;
- evaluation date.

# 40. What should be measured

A serious Spanish tax dashboard should include:

- total tax-to-GDP ratio;
- tax wedge by household type;
- effective IRPF rate by income decile;
- marginal effective rates after benefit withdrawal;
- VAT compliance and policy gaps;
- corporate effective rates;
- cost and distribution of each tax benefit;
- environmental-tax revenue;
- property and land taxation;
- inheritance by size;
- regional effective burdens;
- compliance costs;
- audit yield;
- dispute duration;
- public-service outcomes;
- citizen confidence in fairness.

Statutory rates are not enough.

The relevant measure is the burden people and businesses actually face after deductions, contributions and benefits.

# Final verdict

Spain’s tax system is not defined by one extreme.

It is not a Scandinavian high-tax model, but neither is it a low-tax economy.

It collects more relative to GDP than the OECD average while placing a particularly heavy burden on formal labour. At the same time, it collects comparatively less from consumption and environmental taxation because of extensive exemptions and reduced rates. Its regional structure permits meaningful autonomy but also generates complexity and unequal effective treatment. 

The system has genuine strengths:

- progressive personal taxation;
- strong digital administration;
- broad revenue capacity;
- financing for universal services;
- extensive third-party reporting;
- improving anti-fraud tools.

Its main weaknesses are:

- high labour taxation;
- weak family support through the tax-benefit system;
- too many tax expenditures;
- transaction-heavy property taxation;
- regional fragmentation;
- frequent legal changes;
- poor evaluation;
- low public trust in spending quality.

The question “Do Spaniards pay too much tax?” has no single answer.

A low-income worker facing a 41% employment tax wedge at average-wage assumptions may reasonably feel heavily taxed.

A large inheritance benefiting from extensive regional bonuses may be lightly taxed relative to labour income.

A consumer pays VAT every day, while an internationally mobile owner may have more scope to structure income and residence.

The most defensible conclusion is:

> **Spain does not simply need higher or lower taxes. It needs to collect more intelligently—less from ordinary formal employment, more consistently from poorly taxed rents, externalities and large transfers, and with far fewer exceptions whose social value has never been demonstrated.**

Tax reform will not be credible unless it is paired with spending reform.

Citizens are unlikely to accept a larger fiscal contribution while healthcare, housing, administration and procurement appear inefficient.

The true fiscal contract is therefore two-sided:

1. taxpayers must contribute according to real ability to pay;
2. governments must prove what each additional euro achieves.

# Youth and opportunity in Spain: a generation with more education, but less access to adulthood

### Young people in Spain are better educated, more digitally capable and entering a stronger labour market than those who came of age after the 2008 crisis. Yet they leave the family home later, accumulate less wealth, postpone children and depend more heavily on parental support. The problem is no longer simply youth unemployment. It is the growing distance between obtaining a job and constructing an independent life.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

Spain’s young people are not a “lost generation” in the sense that they lack education, ambition or economic value.

Nor are their difficulties merely a temporary rite of passage that disappears with age.

The deeper problem is:

> **Spain has improved young people’s access to education and employment faster than it has improved their access to housing, stable income, wealth and independent adulthood.**

The result is a prolonged transition:

1. Young people study for longer.
2. They enter employment later and through weaker positions.
3. Their early wages are insufficient for the housing markets where opportunities concentrate.
4. They remain in the parental home or depend on family transfers.
5. Partnership, parenthood, saving and homeownership are postponed.
6. Those without family wealth fall further behind those who receive housing, guarantees or inheritances.

Spain is therefore not merely producing delayed emancipation.

It is creating an increasingly **family-dependent opportunity system**, in which parental resources determine how safely a young person can study, move, take professional risks and become independent.

---

# 1. “Youth” is not one homogeneous group

Official statistics use different definitions depending on the subject:

- youth unemployment usually refers to people under 25;
- the NEET indicator often covers ages 15–29;
- emancipation reports frequently define youth as 16–29;
- education and graduate-employment statistics may extend to age 34;
- Injuve studies examine broader transitions from adolescence into adulthood.

This matters because a 17-year-old student, a 24-year-old apprentice and a 32-year-old renter have very different lives.

They can nevertheless share the same structural problem: adult milestones have moved later.

The 2024 *Informe Juventud en España*, published in June 2025, explicitly frames contemporary youth through delayed transitions involving employment, emancipation, housing, family formation and mental health. 

The question is therefore not simply whether young people eventually find work.

It is **how long they remain unable to make consequential adult choices without family permission or support**.

---

# 2. The great contradiction: employment improved while emancipation collapsed

Spain’s labour market has strengthened substantially. Youth unemployment has fallen from the extreme levels reached after the financial crisis, and registered unemployment among people under 25 reached a new low in June 2026. 

Yet the youth emancipation rate fell to **14.5% in 2025**, the lowest level in the comparable series. Only around one in seven people aged 16–29 lived outside the parental home. Approximately 1.07 million young people were emancipated—almost 50,000 fewer than a year earlier. 



The paradox reveals a major analytical mistake.

Employment is not equivalent to independence.

A young person can be working and still be unable to leave home because:

- the salary is too low;
- the contract or annual hours are unpredictable;
- the deposit and guarantees are unaffordable;
- rent consumes nearly all earnings;
- available jobs are in expensive metropolitan areas;
- sharing a room still exceeds a sustainable housing burden.

The latest CJE report found that only around one in four employed young people was emancipated. 

---

# 3. Housing has become the gatekeeper of adulthood

At the end of 2025, the average advertised rent used in the CJE analysis was approximately **€1,176 per month**, equal to **98.7% of the average young person’s monthly salary of €1,191**. Renting a room averaged around €400 and consumed approximately one third of that income. 

These figures should not be interpreted as meaning that every young Spaniard faces exactly the same rent or salary. They compare national averages drawn from different markets and are designed as an affordability indicator.

Their message is nevertheless unmistakable:

> Independent housing based on one ordinary young salary has become economically irrational across much of Spain.

The estimated average age of emancipation reached **30.2 years**, compared with an EU average of roughly 26.2–26.3 years. 

This is not simply a cultural preference for living with parents.

Culture affects household formation, but the continuing decline in emancipation while employment rises points toward a material barrier.

If remaining at home were mainly a preferred lifestyle, improvements in employment would not coexist so consistently with worsening residential independence.

---

# 4. Living with parents protects young people—and conceals the severity of the problem

The family home functions as Spain’s unofficial youth welfare state.

Parents provide:

- accommodation;
- food;
- utilities;
- transport;
- childcare for grandchildren;
- financial transfers;
- rental guarantees;
- mortgage deposits;
- support during unemployment;
- a place to return after a failed relationship or job.

This protection prevents greater homelessness and deprivation.

It also makes the system appear more successful than it is.

A 27-year-old employed adult living with parents may not be classified as experiencing severe housing deprivation. But they may lack:

- privacy;
- geographic mobility;
- capacity to form a household;
- space for a partner or child;
- freedom to change family dynamics;
- the ability to accumulate independent experience.

Family protection is therefore both a social strength and an institutional failure.

Spain is relying on private households to absorb risks that housing, employment and welfare policy have not resolved.

---

# 5. Family wealth increasingly determines opportunity

Two young people with the same qualifications and salary can face completely different futures.

One may receive:

- a free room in a family-owned city apartment;
- a car;
- help with university costs;
- a €40,000 deposit;
- a parent acting as rental guarantor;
- unpaid professional connections;
- time to prepare competitive examinations.

The other may:

- contribute to household expenses;
- commute long distances;
- support unemployed relatives;
- finance education through work;
- lack a guarantor;
- accept the first available job;
- be unable to move to an opportunity-rich city.

The formal labour market sees two equally qualified individuals.

The real opportunity system does not.

As housing becomes more expensive relative to early-career income, inheritance and parental transfers become more important than salary in determining:

- who buys;
- who rents independently;
- who studies longer;
- who moves abroad;
- who launches a business;
- who can tolerate an unpaid or low-paid entry period.

Spain’s crisis of youth opportunity is therefore becoming a crisis of **inherited life chances**.

---

# 6. Young people are more educated than previous generations

Spain has dramatically expanded university and post-compulsory education.

Across the EU, 45% of people aged 25–34 held tertiary qualifications in 2025. Spain has typically ranked above the EU average in tertiary attainment, while also retaining a large group with low or intermediate qualifications. 

This produces an unusual education structure:

- many university graduates;
- insufficient numbers with some mid-level technical qualifications;
- a persistent group leaving education early;
- employer complaints about practical and vocational skills;
- graduates working below their qualification level.

The problem is not simply “too many graduates.”

Higher education has genuine individual and social value.

The weakness lies in how education connects to employment:

- course choices are not always informed by labour outcomes;
- qualification recognition can be slow;
- practical experience varies considerably;
- employers underinvest in training;
- career guidance is weak;
- the economy does not create enough high-productivity graduate roles.

Spain has expanded educational aspiration faster than it has transformed its production model.

---

# 7. Early school leaving remains a major failure—especially among young men

Spain’s early education and training leaving rate fell enormously over the previous decade, from around 20% in 2015 to **12.8% in 2025**.

That improvement was among the largest in the EU.

But Spain still had the third-highest rate in the Union in 2025, behind Romania and Germany, and remained well above the EU average of 9.1%. 

The gender gap is particularly serious. Eurostat reported a **6.4-point difference** between young men and women in Spain—the largest male disadvantage in the EU comparison. 

This has long-term consequences.

Young people leaving education with only lower-secondary attainment face greater risk of:

- unemployment;
- low wages;
- temporary and seasonal employment;
- physical jobs vulnerable to injury;
- weak access to retraining;
- poverty;
- disengagement from institutions.

The male education problem is also politically sensitive.

Discussing it is sometimes portrayed as competing with women’s equality.

That is a mistake.

Spain can address the disadvantages women face in wages, care and leadership while also recognizing that a substantial group of boys and young men is disengaging from education.

---

# 8. University access expanded, but class still shapes educational success

Public university fees and scholarships make Spanish higher education more accessible than systems based on very high tuition.

Yet formal access does not eliminate class differences.

Students from higher-income families can more easily:

- relocate;
- study full-time;
- undertake unpaid placements;
- learn languages abroad;
- extend studies;
- prepare postgraduate qualifications;
- use family professional networks;
- reject unsuitable job offers.

Lower-income students may need paid work that reduces study time.

They may select a nearby course rather than the best programme, or leave education when household income becomes unstable.

The key inequality is not only whether a young person enters university.

It is **how much risk and delay their family can finance while they convert education into employment**.

---

# 9. Vocational education is improving, but is still treated as second class

Spain needs technicians and skilled workers in:

- industrial maintenance;
- construction;
- energy;
- healthcare support;
- transport;
- logistics;
- cybersecurity;
- advanced manufacturing;
- care;
- installation trades.

Vocational education and dual training can offer faster transitions into employment than some general academic paths.

But three obstacles remain:

1. Social prestige still favours university.
2. Training quality and employer participation vary.
3. Some supposedly dual programmes offer low-value placements rather than structured learning.

A good vocational system does not push disadvantaged students away from university.

It provides an equally respected route with:

- real workplace training;
- portable qualifications;
- employer commitment;
- progression into higher qualifications;
- transparent employment and salary outcomes.

---

# 10. Youth unemployment fell, but remains structurally high

The under-25 unemployment rate remains much higher than Spain’s already elevated overall unemployment rate.

The 2025 CJE emancipation report used a broader youth measure and reported unemployment of approximately **17.2%** among young people in its target population. Under-25 unemployment statistics are higher because the denominator covers a younger, less established labour force. 

Youth unemployment rates require careful interpretation.

They do not mean that one quarter of all young people have no job. Many are studying and are not counted in the labour force.

But a high rate still indicates that young people actively seeking employment encounter unusually severe barriers.

The problem is not only time without work.

Early unemployment produces “scarring”:

- weaker initial wages;
- delayed skill acquisition;
- lower confidence;
- poorer professional networks;
- increased likelihood of accepting mismatched jobs;
- slower accumulation of contributions and wealth.

---

# 11. Entry into work is easier than entry into a career

A young person may obtain employment without finding a credible progression path.

Common early-career experiences include:

- temporary projects;
- discontinuous work;
- internships;
- subcontracting;
- jobs unrelated to qualifications;
- limited training;
- low responsibility;
- repeated employer changes;
- public competitive examinations lasting years.

This creates a long “waiting room” between education and stable professional identity.

The labour reform reduced conventional temporary contracting, which is significant.

But job quality must be assessed through:

- annual income;
- hours worked;
- progression;
- training;
- schedule predictability;
- occupation–qualification matching;
- continuity over several years.

A permanent contract that produces insufficient hours or no development is more secure than a short temporary contract, but it may still not create opportunity.

---

# 12. Spain has both overqualification and skill shortages

Employers report difficulty hiring in technical fields, construction, care, healthcare and parts of the digital economy.

At the same time, many graduates work in jobs below their education level.

These facts can coexist because:

- qualifications do not match vacancies;
- jobs are in different regions;
- employers offer insufficient pay;
- housing makes relocation impossible;
- training is too theoretical;
- graduates lack professional experience;
- small firms cannot provide structured entry pathways.

The solution is not to blame young people for choosing “useless degrees” or employers for every vacancy.

It is to publish better information about:

- completion rates;
- employment by programme;
- median earnings;
- geographic demand;
- skill shortages;
- actual career trajectories.

Young people are currently asked to make major education decisions with incomplete data and bear most of the risk when the choice does not work.

---

# 13. Spain’s opportunity geography is highly unequal

Opportunity concentrates in:

- Madrid;
- Barcelona;
- Valencia;
- Málaga;
- Bilbao;
- university centres;
- tourist areas;
- selected industrial and technology clusters.

Many rural and smaller urban areas offer lower housing costs but fewer high-skill jobs.

This creates a difficult choice:

- stay near family in a weak labour market;
- move toward work and face unaffordable housing;
- commute for long periods;
- leave Spain.

Digital work can reduce the conflict for some occupations, but remote employment is not available to nurses, construction workers, hospitality staff, teachers, carers or laboratory workers.

Regional development policy often promises to “bring young people back” without creating the professional ecosystems required to retain them.

A cheap house is not an opportunity if:

- broadband is weak;
- transport is poor;
- employment is seasonal;
- childcare is unavailable;
- there is no peer community;
- promotion requires eventual relocation.

---

# 14. Migration abroad is both opportunity and loss

Thousands of young Spaniards study and work abroad.

This can provide:

- languages;
- higher salaries;
- international experience;
- professional networks;
- independence;
- access to sectors underdeveloped in Spain.

Describing all outward migration as “brain drain” is too negative.

Mobility can improve individual careers and later benefit Spain through return, investment and international connections.

The problem appears when leaving is not a choice but the only realistic route to:

- professional progression;
- scientific careers;
- affordable independence relative to salary;
- stable research funding;
- merit-based advancement.

Spain does not need every young professional to remain.

It needs a system attractive enough that departure is reversible.

---

# 15. Immigration is also transforming Spanish youth

A growing share of Spain’s young population was born abroad or has immigrant parents.

These young people are highly diverse:

- recent arrivals;
- naturalized citizens;
- children raised entirely in Spain;
- EU nationals;
- refugees;
- Latin American migrants;
- North African and other communities.

They may face additional barriers:

- qualification recognition;
- language;
- discrimination;
- residential segregation;
- legal insecurity;
- weaker family wealth;
- overrepresentation in low-paid sectors.

At the same time, they are essential to Spain’s demographic and economic future.

The test of integration is not whether young people of immigrant origin work in low-wage shortage occupations.

It is whether their children can move into the same educational, professional and residential opportunities as other Spaniards.

---

# 16. The NEET label is useful—but can stigmatize

NEET refers to young people who are neither employed nor in education or training.

Across the EU, **11% of people aged 15–29** were in this situation in 2025. Spain has historically remained above the EU average, although its rate has improved substantially. 

The category includes very different realities:

- unemployed graduates;
- young parents providing care;
- disabled people;
- discouraged jobseekers;
- people preparing informally for examinations;
- individuals experiencing illness;
- socially disconnected youth.

Treating them all as inactive or unmotivated obscures the solution.

A graduate needs matching and experience.

A young carer needs services.

A person with depression needs healthcare.

A disconnected school leaver may need intensive outreach rather than an online training portal.

The category is a warning signal, not a diagnosis.

---

# 17. Mental health is not separable from material opportunity

The 2024 Injuve report made mental health a central area of analysis rather than a secondary lifestyle issue. 

Young people report anxiety, emotional distress and uncertainty for many reasons:

- family conflict;
- loneliness;
- educational pressure;
- social comparison;
- discrimination;
- insecure work;
- housing dependence;
- digital harassment;
- climate anxiety;
- relationship problems;
- uncertainty about the future.

There is a temptation to interpret the rise in reported distress in only one way.

One explanation says young people are less resilient.

Another says society is objectively harsher.

A more credible interpretation includes both measurement and reality:

- young people are more willing to identify and report mental-health difficulties;
- diagnostic awareness has improved;
- some social and economic pressures have intensified;
- online environments produce new forms of comparison and exposure;
- access to treatment remains uneven.

Therapy is important.

But a mental-health strategy cannot treat unaffordable housing, unemployment and social isolation as merely individual emotional-management problems.

---

# 18. Social media expands opportunity and insecurity simultaneously

Digital platforms allow young people to:

- learn;
- create;
- organize;
- find work;
- build communities;
- maintain relationships;
- access identities and ideas unavailable locally.

They also expose users to:

- constant comparison;
- beauty and wealth standards;
- harassment;
- scams;
- extremist recruitment;
- misogyny;
- misinformation;
- addictive engagement loops;
- pressure to convert personality into content.

Eurostat reported in June 2026 that **54% of young people in the EU had encountered hostile or degrading messages online**, indicating that hostile digital exposure is not marginal. 

The problem cannot be solved by simply telling young people to use phones less.

Platforms are integrated into education, employment, relationships and identity.

The policy objective should be greater user control, safer design, algorithmic transparency and serious digital education.

---

# 19. Young women and men face different opportunity structures

## Young women

Young women tend to achieve stronger educational results and have lower early school-leaving rates.

But they also face:

- greater care expectations;
- occupational segregation;
- lower pay in some sectors;
- sexual harassment;
- greater part-time employment;
- career penalties associated with motherhood;
- higher reported mental-health distress in some surveys.

## Young men

Young men are more likely to experience:

- early school leaving;
- educational disengagement;
- occupational injury;
- weaker help-seeking;
- social isolation;
- recruitment into misogynist or extremist online spaces;
- difficulty constructing identity where traditional male roles have weakened.

A serious youth policy should not turn these disadvantages into a competition.

The objective is not to decide which sex is the true victim.

It is to design interventions matching each problem while defending equal rights.

---

# 20. Parenthood is increasingly postponed beyond preference

Spain’s very low fertility is sometimes explained as a cultural rejection of children.

Attitudes matter, but material timing matters too.

A person who:

- finishes education in their mid-twenties;
- spends years in unstable work;
- leaves home around 30;
- then searches for suitable housing and partnership stability;

has a much narrower period in which to have the number of children they may desire.

Delayed emancipation therefore influences:

- fertility;
- family size;
- maternal and paternal age;
- demand for assisted reproduction;
- grandparent availability;
- population ageing.

Cash birth payments alone are unlikely to reverse this.

Family formation depends on predictable conditions:

- housing;
- childcare;
- working time;
- employment continuity;
- confidence that temporary difficulties will not produce collapse.

---

# 21. Political participation has changed rather than disappeared

Young people are often described as apathetic.

But participation increasingly occurs through:

- demonstrations;
- digital campaigns;
- consumer choices;
- issue-based movements;
- climate activism;
- feminism;
- housing movements;
- anti-racist or nationalist politics;
- creator and influencer ecosystems.

Membership in traditional parties, unions and associations may be weaker than in earlier periods, but political expression is highly visible.

The weakness is durability.

A viral campaign can mobilize attention rapidly but may lack:

- organization;
- institutional knowledge;
- funding;
- leadership succession;
- long-term negotiation capacity.

Young people can influence the agenda without gaining equivalent power over budgets, housing or employment institutions.

---

# 22. Spain’s politics is structurally biased toward older voters

Older adults:

- vote at higher rates;
- constitute a growing share of the population;
- are more likely to belong to established organizations;
- often own housing;
- receive automatic pension protection.

Young adults are fewer, less institutionally organized and more mobile.

This creates a political asymmetry.

Policies protecting pensions are immediate and electorally visible.

Policies producing affordable housing, childcare, productivity or educational reform may take years and impose costs on current owners or protected groups.

The result is not necessarily a deliberate conspiracy against youth.

It is an incentive system in which the most reliable voters receive the most reliable protection.

---

# 23. Spain protects retirement income more automatically than early adulthood

Contributory pensions are indexed through national rules designed to protect purchasing power.

Young workers do not receive equivalent automatic protection for:

- salary;
- rent;
- deposits;
- childcare;
- postgraduate training;
- first-home access.

This does not mean pensions should be cut indiscriminately.

Many pensioners have low incomes, and pensions support entire extended families.

The problem is that intergenerational debate often compares averages rather than circumstances.

A low-income elderly tenant can be more vulnerable than a wealthy young professional.

A retired owner with several properties can be much wealthier than a working family.

Policy should therefore be based more on income and wealth than age alone.

But the overall distributional imbalance must still be acknowledged.

---

# 24. Public youth policy is fragmented across departments

Youth opportunity depends on:

- education;
- employment;
- housing;
- transport;
- healthcare;
- taxation;
- social security;
- equality;
- migration;
- digital regulation;
- municipal services.

No youth ministry or strategy controls all these systems.

The 2030 Youth Strategy and its action plans attempt to coordinate government activity, and an interim evaluation covering 2022–2024 was published in December 2025. 

The recurring weakness of youth strategies is that they can become catalogues of programmes rather than mechanisms for changing the main structural indicators.

A policy should not be judged by:

- number of actions;
- money announced;
- workshops delivered;
- young people contacted.

It should be judged by:

- emancipation;
- real income;
- employment continuity;
- educational completion;
- poverty after housing;
- access to mental healthcare;
- wealth accumulation;
- age of desired family formation.

---

# 25. Rent subsidies can help recipients—and raise rents

Youth rent assistance provides immediate relief to some households.

But where housing supply is rigid, a broad subsidy can increase the amount renters are able to bid without increasing the number of homes.

Landlords may capture part of the benefit through higher prices.

This does not mean all assistance is useless.

Targeted support can prevent hardship and enable independence.

But subsidy works best when combined with:

- additional supply;
- public and social housing;
- enforcement against fraudulent rent increases;
- longer contracts;
- transparent eligibility;
- limits linked to real local prices;
- evaluation of landlord capture.

Giving young people more money to compete for the same scarce homes is not a complete housing policy.

---

# 26. First-home guarantees may increase access—and debt

Public mortgage guarantees reduce the deposit barrier for young buyers.

They can help households with adequate income but insufficient family wealth.

This addresses a real inequity: two households able to afford the monthly mortgage may differ only because one receives a parental deposit.

But guarantees create risks:

- increasing demand in a constrained market;
- raising prices;
- encouraging highly leveraged purchases;
- exposing young households to unemployment or rate risk;
- transferring credit risk to the state.

They should therefore be targeted toward households with sustainable repayment capacity and paired with housing supply.

A guarantee solves the deposit problem.

It does not solve overpricing.

---

# 27. Free or discounted transport has unusually broad value

Transport support can improve opportunity by connecting young people to:

- education;
- work;
- housing;
- culture;
- social networks.

Unlike some demand subsidies, improved public transport can expand the effective labour and housing market.

A young worker may accept employment farther away if the commute is reliable and affordable.

A student can attend a better programme without relocating.

Investment should prioritize service frequency and connectivity, not only reduced ticket prices.

A free bus that arrives twice a day does not create mobility.

---

# 28. Youth entrepreneurship is over-romanticized

Young people are often encouraged to create their own opportunities through entrepreneurship.

Successful entrepreneurship can produce independence and innovation.

But many young adults lack:

- savings;
- collateral;
- experience;
- networks;
- stable housing;
- the ability to survive business failure.

Entrepreneurial policy sometimes shifts responsibility from institutions to individuals:

> There are no suitable jobs, so create one.

A serious programme should measure:

- three- and five-year survival;
- income;
- additional employment created;
- productivity;
- access by family background;
- debt after failure.

The objective should be viable firms, not a temporary increase in self-employment registrations.

---

# 29. Testing the popular claims

## “Young people today live worse than their parents”

They are more educated, live longer and have access to technologies and rights unavailable to previous generations.

But they face later emancipation, weaker early wealth accumulation and far higher housing barriers.

**Verdict:** too broad as an absolute claim, but substantially true regarding access to housing and independent adulthood.

## “Young people do not leave home because they are comfortable”

Family culture matters, but emancipation has fallen to 14.5% despite improving employment.

**Verdict:** economic conditions are the dominant explanation.

## “There are plenty of jobs; young people are too demanding”

Employment has improved, but many jobs do not support independent housing in opportunity-rich areas.

**Verdict:** job availability and viable adulthood are different standards.

## “University no longer has value”

Graduates generally retain stronger employment and income prospects over their lifetime.

**Verdict:** false generally, but course quality, mismatch and overqualification are real.

## “Spain has too many university students”

Spain has both many graduates and persistent early school leaving.

**Verdict:** the deeper problem is polarization between high and low qualification, plus insufficient technical pathways.

## “Young people are mentally weaker”

Greater awareness may raise reporting, but material uncertainty and digital pressures are also real.

**Verdict:** stigmatizing and unsupported as a complete explanation.

## “Rent subsidies solve emancipation”

They help selected recipients but can be absorbed into prices where supply is fixed.

**Verdict:** useful relief, weak structural reform alone.

## “Mortgage guarantees create equal opportunity”

They reduce dependence on parental deposits but can increase debt and house prices.

**Verdict:** potentially fair when targeted, dangerous when used as broad demand stimulus.

## “Young people are politically apathetic”

Traditional organizational participation may be weaker, but issue-based and digital mobilization is extensive.

**Verdict:** participation has changed form.

## “The solution is for young people to move somewhere cheaper”

Cheaper areas often provide fewer jobs and weaker transport.

**Verdict:** relocation can help individuals, but does not repair the geographic mismatch between housing and opportunity.

# 30. What a serious youth-opportunity programme would prioritize

## A. Make emancipation a national outcome

Every annual budget should report its expected effect on:

- youth employment;
- real income;
- rent burden;
- public housing access;
- average age of leaving home;
- household formation.

## B. Build permanently affordable housing near opportunity

Prioritize:

- transport-connected public land;
- social rental;
- university and vocational residences;
- key-worker housing;
- conversion of suitable existing property;
- permanent affordability conditions.

## C. Replace isolated youth programmes with transition guarantees

Every person leaving compulsory education should have access to one of:

- continued education;
- quality vocational training;
- paid apprenticeship;
- supported employment;
- intensive reintegration.

## D. Publish course-level outcome data

For every degree and vocational programme:

- completion rate;
- employment;
- occupation match;
- median income;
- geographic destination;
- further-study rate.

## E. Create a fair entry-career standard

Regulate internships and junior placements through:

- payment;
- genuine training;
- maximum duration;
- named supervision;
- written learning objectives;
- conversion and employment reporting.

## F. Make relocation possible

Provide portable support for:

- deposits;
- temporary accommodation;
- transport;
- childcare;
- recognition of qualifications.

## G. Expand youth mental-health access

Guarantee timely care through:

- primary care;
- schools;
- universities;
- digital services;
- specialist referral.

But pair treatment with action on housing, work and loneliness.

## H. Make childcare a youth policy

Affordable childcare helps young adults:

- remain employed;
- have children closer to their preferred timing;
- reduce gender inequality;
- stabilize income.

## I. Protect young people without family wealth

Scholarships and housing access should account for:

- actual parental support;
- family assets;
- geographic relocation;
- independent living costs.

## J. Measure family-wealth dependence

Track how often deposits, guarantees, housing and educational costs are financed by parents.

Without this data, Spain cannot see how opportunity is inherited.

---

# 31. A youth compact should contain explicit trade-offs

A credible intergenerational settlement would require older and established groups to accept some costs.

Possible elements include:

- using public land for permanent rental rather than sale;
- reducing tax privileges that reward passive property accumulation;
- concentrating some benefits more strongly by income and wealth;
- increasing housing density in high-demand areas;
- prioritizing childcare, education and housing in future spending growth;
- protecting pensions while avoiding automatic expansion regardless of fiscal capacity;
- financing dependency and healthcare without loading the entire burden onto younger labour income.

The purpose is not generational punishment.

It is to prevent the life chances of one generation being financed through the permanent dependence of the next.

---

# 32. What should be measured

Spain needs a youth-opportunity dashboard including:

- emancipation rate;
- average age of leaving home;
- median rent-to-income ratio;
- youth poverty after housing costs;
- median annual labour income;
- under-25 and 16–29 unemployment;
- NEET rate;
- early education leaving;
- qualification mismatch;
- apprenticeship quality;
- mental-health waiting times;
- involuntary part-time work;
- parental financial transfers;
- homeownership by age;
- wealth by generation;
- desired versus actual family formation;
- return rate after working abroad.

The objective should not be to keep people statistically “in education” or “employed.”

It should be to expand their practical capacity to make adult choices.

---

# Final verdict

Spain’s young people have more formal opportunity than many earlier generations:

- broader education;
- greater social freedom;
- digital access;
- European mobility;
- a much stronger labour market than after 2008;
- lower conventional temporary employment.

But formal opportunity is being cancelled by material dependence.

A young person can:

- complete a degree;
- obtain a permanent contract;
- work full-time;
- behave responsibly;

and still be unable to rent an ordinary home.

That is the central failure.

The emancipation rate fell to 14.5% in 2025, while the average rent used by the CJE consumed 98.7% of average youth salary. Spain’s early school-leaving rate remained among the EU’s highest, and family wealth increasingly determines who can convert education into independence. 

The system does not primarily lack talented young people.

It lacks a reliable bridge between:

- education and career;
- employment and adequate income;
- income and housing;
- housing and family formation;
- individual effort and independent adulthood.

Spain’s youth crisis should therefore not be measured only by unemployment.

It should be measured by a harder question:

> **Can an ordinary young person without inherited wealth build an autonomous, secure life through education and work?**

For too many, the current answer is no.

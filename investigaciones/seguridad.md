

# Public safety in Spain: street crime remains comparatively contained, while fraud and localized violence reshape insecurity

### Spain is not experiencing a generalized crime explosion. Conventional crime remains near the lower end of its historical range, and thefts and burglaries declined in 2025. Yet cyberfraud, sexual violence, violent incidents in particular neighbourhoods and the visibility of repeat offending sustain a strong perception that public safety is deteriorating.

*Situation assessed as of 11 July 2026.*

## The central finding

Spain is safer than much of its political debate suggests—but less safe in some ways than reassuring national averages imply.

In 2025, police recorded **2.47 million criminal offences**. Conventional, offline crime fell slightly, while cybercrime continued to rise. The conventional-crime rate stood at **40.4 offences per 1,000 residents**, which the Interior Ministry places within the lowest band of the historical series. During the first quarter of 2026, the rate remained almost unchanged at 40.5. 

But national stability conceals several distinct problems:

1. Violent crime remains relatively uncommon but has increased in some categories.
2. Theft and robbery are concentrated in cities, transport systems and tourism zones.
3. Sexual offences remain seriously underreported even as recorded complaints rise.
4. Online fraud has become one of the most common forms of victimization.
5. A small group of repeat offenders can create disproportionate local harm.
6. Public perception is driven by visible incidents, viral videos and neighbourhood experience—not only national rates.

The real question is therefore not simply:

> “Is crime going up or down?”

It is:

> **Which crimes are changing, where are they concentrated, who suffers them, and how effectively does the justice system prevent repetition?**

---

# 1. Total crime rose slightly because cybercrime rose

Spain registered **2,474,156 criminal offences in 2025**, 0.8% more than in 2024.

The total divides into two increasingly different realities:

- **1,984,908 conventional offences**, down 0.2%;
- **489,248 cyber offences**, up 5.3%.

Cybercrime represented almost one fifth of all recorded criminality. 

This distinction changes the meaning of public insecurity.

The traditional image of crime involves:

- assault;
- burglary;
- street robbery;
- vehicle theft;
- drug dealing.

But a growing proportion now involves:

- fraudulent transfers;
- phishing;
- false online purchases;
- investment scams;
- identity theft;
- impersonation of banks or relatives;
- account takeover.

A person may live in a neighbourhood with little street violence and still face repeated criminal attempts through their phone, email and bank account.

---

# 2. Conventional crime is not experiencing a national explosion

In 2025, conventional crime fell by 0.2%. Several large property-crime categories declined:

- robberies involving violence or intimidation fell 1.5%;
- burglaries and other forced-entry robberies fell 9.1%;
- residential burglaries fell 8.3%;
- thefts fell 2.3%;
- vehicle theft rose only 0.2%.

Together, theft, robbery and vehicle-related offences still represented 42% of conventional criminality, so their decline materially affected the national trend. 

During the first quarter of 2026, thefts fell another 3.2%, residential burglaries declined 3%, and robberies involving violence were practically unchanged. 

This evidence contradicts the broad claim that Spain has entered a general wave of street crime.

It does not prove that every place is safer.

A national reduction can coexist with deterioration in:

- one municipality;
- a transport hub;
- a nightlife area;
- a shopping district;
- a particular housing estate.

Crime is geographically concentrated, while national averages spread it across almost 50 million residents.

---

# 3. Serious violence remains uncommon, but cannot be dismissed

Spain recorded **376 completed intentional homicides or murders in 2025**, compared with 349 in 2024—an increase of 7.7%.

Attempted homicide and murder increased by 5%, and serious or less-serious assault and mass-fight offences increased by 7.2%. 

In the first quarter of 2026, completed homicides rose from 85 to 94 compared with the same period in 2025. Serious assault and mass-fight offences increased by 11.1%, while attempted homicides declined. Quarterly numbers are relatively small and can vary substantially, so they should not be treated as proof of a stable national trend without several years of evidence. 

The balanced conclusion is:

- Spain does not have a mass-homicide problem;
- increases in serious violence deserve close monitoring;
- low national frequency is no consolation to neighbourhoods where violence clusters.

A country can remain statistically safe while particular families and communities experience severe insecurity.

---

# 4. “Crime” is dominated by property offences, not extreme violence

Political communication often focuses on the most frightening cases:

- murder;
- stranger assault;
- sexual violence;
- violent robbery.

Those crimes deserve attention because their human cost is high.

But the volume of recorded crime is dominated by other conduct.

Spain recorded **633,888 thefts in 2025**, compared with 62,061 robberies involving violence and 376 completed intentional homicides. 

This difference matters for policy.

The measures that reduce pickpocketing in a tourism district are not the same as those needed to prevent:

- intimate-partner homicide;
- gang conflict;
- sexual assault;
- online fraud;
- organized drug violence.

A single national crime total can conceal more than it explains.

---

# 5. Theft remains the crime most associated with urban insecurity

Theft includes taking property without the force or intimidation required for robbery.

It is especially associated with:

- crowded transport;
- nightlife;
- shopping areas;
- beaches;
- airports;
- major events;
- tourism zones.

Individual losses may be smaller than in serious violent crime, but repeated theft has major social effects:

- residents avoid public spaces;
- businesses spend more on security;
- tourists distrust transport;
- police resources are repeatedly consumed;
- victims lose phones containing personal and financial data.

A neighbourhood’s sense of order can deteriorate even when offences are nonviolent.

Visible repetition matters as much as the national trend.

---

# 6. Repeat offending is one of the largest gaps between statistics and lived experience

A relatively small number of prolific offenders can be associated with repeated:

- theft;
- robbery;
- shoplifting;
- vehicle crime;
- fraud;
- drug distribution.

Each offence enters the national statistics separately, but citizens experience the same person returning repeatedly to the same transport line, shopping area or commercial district.

This creates a strong belief that:

- arrest has no consequence;
- police work is wasted;
- the judicial system cannot react quickly;
- offenders understand how to exploit procedural delays.

The political demand for “tougher penalties” often originates here.

But repeat offending is not solved only by increasing maximum sentences.

The operational problems may include:

- slow identification across jurisdictions;
- delayed trials;
- lack of consolidated case information;
- low-value offences processed separately;
- addiction;
- homelessness;
- mental illness;
- organized exploitation of vulnerable offenders;
- ineffective probation or post-release supervision.

A credible strategy should identify high-frequency offenders early and concentrate judicial, policing and social intervention on them.

---

# 7. Cyberfraud is the real mass-crime growth story

Spain registered **430,493 computer-fraud offences in 2025**.

They represented:

- 88% of cybercrime;
- 17.4% of all recorded crime.

Computer fraud had increased by **513.4% since 2016**, when 70,178 such offences were recorded. 

The transformation is profound.

Many offenders no longer need to be physically near the victim. They can operate:

- abroad;
- through networks of money mules;
- with spoofed phone numbers;
- through fraudulent advertisements;
- with stolen personal data;
- using automated messages and artificial intelligence.

The Interior Ministry identifies online fraud, impersonation and increasingly sophisticated automated attacks as major contemporary threats. 

This crime is difficult to deter because:

- one operation can target thousands of people;
- stolen money moves rapidly between accounts;
- offenders cross borders;
- victims may feel embarrassed and report late;
- small losses may not receive intensive investigation;
- banks, telecommunications companies and platforms hold different pieces of evidence.

Public safety policy remains too focused on patrols and not enough on the digital infrastructure through which mass victimization now occurs.

---

# 8. Banks and platforms are part of crime prevention

Cyberfraud is usually described as a problem of careless users.

That framing is incomplete.

Victims should use caution, but institutions control many of the relevant systems:

- banks detect unusual transfers;
- telecom operators detect number spoofing;
- marketplaces identify fraudulent sellers;
- platforms distribute scam advertisements;
- domain providers host fake pages;
- payment services move proceeds.

Crime prevention should therefore include:

- real-time confirmation of unusual transfers;
- stronger beneficiary-name checking;
- delayed execution for high-risk transactions;
- rapid freezing of destination accounts;
- visible verification of legitimate institutional messages;
- liability where platforms repeatedly distribute fraudulent advertising;
- faster sharing of fraud indicators.

Teaching citizens not to click suspicious links is useful.

It cannot be the principal defence against industrialized fraud.

---

# 9. Sexual violence is increasing in police and court records

Police recorded **21,659 offences against sexual freedom in 2025**, 2.3% more than in 2024. Offences involving penetration rose 2.8%. In the first quarter of 2026, sexual offences increased another 1.1%, with penetration offences up 3.8%. 

Interior notes that part of the long-term rise may reflect:

- greater awareness;
- lower tolerance;
- increased willingness to report;
- reduced underreporting.

That explanation should not be misused to dismiss the increase. 

More reporting is institutionally positive because hidden crimes enter the justice system.

But recorded growth can still contain real growth.

Both may occur simultaneously.

The correct conclusion is neither:

- “sexual violence is exploding exactly as complaint figures suggest,” nor
- “nothing changed; women are merely reporting more.”

---

# 10. Conviction data cannot be compared directly with annual police reports

In 2024, the register recorded **3,936 adults with final convictions for sexual offences**, 37.3% more than in the previous year, and 550 convicted minors, up 29.7%. Almost all were male. 

But these figures do not mean that all offences occurred in 2024.

Final judgments registered in one year can concern crimes committed years earlier. Legal reforms also changed classifications, making short-term comparisons difficult. The INE explicitly warns that these conviction increases must be interpreted cautiously. 

Four separate stages should always be distinguished:

1. reported or police-recorded incident;
2. arrest or investigation;
3. prosecution;
4. final conviction.

Mixing them produces false conclusions about either impunity or crime growth.

---

# 11. Women and men experience insecurity differently

Men are more exposed to many forms of:

- public violence;
- fights;
- homicide;
- criminal offending;
- occupational and nightlife risk.

Women experience greater fear and risk in relation to:

- sexual violence;
- intimate-partner violence;
- stalking;
- harassment;
- travel alone at night.

A city can appear safe according to homicide and robbery figures while remaining restrictive for women who alter:

- routes;
- clothing;
- transport;
- schedules;
- social activity.

Public safety should therefore measure not only victimization but constrained behaviour:

- Who avoids public transport?
- Who refuses night work?
- Who pays for taxis due to fear?
- Who avoids parks or stations?
- Who changes housing because of harassment?

Freedom from crime includes freedom to use public space.

---

# 12. Youth crime should not be inferred from viral videos

In 2024, **13,491 minors aged 14–17 received final convictions**, 3.6% more than the previous year.

The most frequent offences were:

- injuries: 31.6%;
- robberies: 15.8%;
- threats: 9.8%.

Four out of five convicted minors were male. 

These figures demonstrate a real youth-offending problem, especially involving violence and robbery.

They do not establish that a generation is becoming criminal.

A video of a group assault can be replayed millions of times and create a perception of constant prevalence. Social media measures attention, not incidence.

Youth violence prevention needs attention to:

- school disengagement;
- family instability;
- neighbourhood conflict;
- drugs;
- status competition;
- online humiliation;
- weapons;
- gang identity;
- previous victimization.

Punishment is necessary for serious offending, but prevention must begin before the first major violent offence.

---

# 13. Youth gangs are not one uniform phenomenon

The label “gang” can describe very different groups:

- organized criminal structures;
- identity-based youth groups;
- drug-distribution networks;
- neighbourhood peer groups;
- loosely connected online groups;
- temporary groups formed for fights or robbery.

Overbroad labelling creates two errors.

First, ordinary adolescents can be treated as organized criminals because of clothing, ethnicity or friendship networks.

Second, genuinely organized groups can be underestimated as merely troublesome teenagers.

Police and social services need behavioural indicators:

- planned violence;
- weapons;
- hierarchy;
- recruitment;
- territorial control;
- extortion;
- drug markets;
- repeated coordinated offending.

Origin or aesthetic style is not a sufficient definition.

---

# 14. Organized crime is less visible than street crime but more institutionally dangerous

Street theft produces immediate fear.

Organized crime can cause deeper structural damage through:

- drug trafficking;
- money laundering;
- human trafficking;
- labour exploitation;
- corruption;
- fraudulent companies;
- violence between criminal networks;
- infiltration of logistics and property markets.

In 2025, Policía Nacional and Guardia Civil conducted 413 operations against human trafficking and exploitation, detained 1,056 people and dismantled 112 criminal organizations or groups. Authorities reported freeing 1,869 victims, including 28 minors. 

These crimes often remain invisible because victims:

- fear traffickers;
- lack legal status;
- depend on employers;
- do not identify themselves as victims;
- work in isolated or illicit environments.

A country can appear peaceful on the street while criminal networks profit from hidden coercion.

---

# 15. Drug markets create highly localized insecurity

Drug trafficking offences increased **5.5% in 2025**, to 22,731 recorded incidents. 

The social impact is highly uneven.

Many communities rarely encounter visible drug-market violence.

Others experience:

- open dealing;
- intimidation;
- occupied or abandoned properties used for sales;
- weapons;
- territorial disputes;
- money laundering;
- exploitation of minors;
- unsafe public spaces.

National statistics can understate the severity for areas positioned along trafficking, storage or distribution routes.

The most effective response combines:

- investigation of financial networks;
- port and logistics security;
- local policing;
- protection of witnesses;
- action against weapons;
- treatment for addiction;
- recovery of neighbourhood space.

Arresting low-level sellers alone leaves the economic structure intact.

---

# 16. Immigration and crime: the data require honesty and restraint

In 2024, 71.4% of adults with final convictions had Spanish nationality.

But the conviction rate was **15.7 per 1,000 foreign adults**, compared with **6.2 per 1,000 Spanish adults**. Among minors, the corresponding rates were 10.9 and 5.8. 

The disparity is real.

It should not be denied or disguised.

But raw nationality rates do not identify the causal effect of immigration because the populations differ in:

- age;
- sex;
- income;
- employment;
- neighbourhood;
- family situation;
- legal insecurity;
- policing exposure.

Foreign populations contain a larger proportion of young men, the demographic group with the highest offending rates across societies. The INE reports that the highest adult conviction rate occurs between ages 21 and 30 and that men’s conviction rate is more than four times women’s. 

Nationality is therefore relevant descriptive information, but a poor final explanation.

---

# 17. Two dishonest positions dominate the migration–crime debate

## Dishonest denial

This position claims that immigration has no relationship to any crime pattern and that publishing disparities is inherently racist.

That destroys trust when official figures show differences.

## Dishonest collective accusation

This position uses an individual crime or unadjusted rate to treat millions of foreign residents as a dangerous category.

That ignores the overwhelming majority who are not convicted of crime and substitutes group suspicion for individual responsibility.

The credible position is:

- publish the data;
- adjust for age, sex and socioeconomic factors;
- identify specific offence and locality patterns;
- intervene against offenders;
- avoid collective guilt;
- address integration and marginalization where they contribute to risk.

Suppressing facts strengthens extremist narratives.

Generalizing them strengthens discrimination.

---

# 18. “Okupación” combines several legally different problems

Public debate commonly uses *okupación* to refer to:

- unlawful entry into an occupied home;
- occupation of an empty property;
- tenants who stop paying;
- fraudulent subletting;
- vulnerable families in abandoned buildings;
- criminal groups controlling properties.

These are not legally or practically identical.

Entry into someone’s residence raises a different legal issue from unauthorized occupation of an empty investment property. A tenant remaining after non-payment is also different from forced entry.

Political debate frequently selects exceptional cases and presents them as the standard process.

The practical problems nevertheless deserve attention:

- slow civil proceedings;
- owner uncertainty;
- fraudulent utility use;
- neighbourhood deterioration;
- criminal intermediaries;
- exploitation of vulnerable occupiers.

The response should distinguish cases rapidly rather than treating every dispute as either a harmless social need or a violent home invasion.

---

# 19. Insecurity is shaped by disorder as well as crime

Citizens may feel unsafe because of:

- poor lighting;
- abandoned buildings;
- aggressive public behaviour;
- public drug use;
- repeated noise;
- vandalism;
- harassment;
- dangerous traffic;
- dirty or damaged spaces.

Not every form of disorder is criminal, and aggressive policing of minor behaviour can unfairly target poverty or minority groups.

But ignoring persistent disorder also has consequences.

People withdraw from shared spaces, leaving them less socially supervised and more vulnerable to further deterioration.

Effective neighbourhood safety often depends on:

- lighting;
- cleaning;
- transport;
- youth facilities;
- mental-health services;
- housing management;
- rapid repair;
- visible foot patrols;
- active commercial streets.

Security is partly produced through urban design and functioning public services.

---

# 20. Perceived insecurity is not irrational merely because crime rates are low

A person’s perception depends on:

- personal victimization;
- stories from friends;
- visible disorder;
- media exposure;
- social-media videos;
- confidence in police;
- trust in courts;
- demographic or neighbourhood change.

A rare but brutal event can legitimately affect behaviour.

Perception becomes misleading when people infer national prevalence from:

- one repeated video;
- one neighbourhood;
- one offender;
- one nationality;
- one political media ecosystem.

Political actors have incentives to amplify either fear or reassurance.

The opposition gains from presenting loss of control.

The government gains from presenting falling national averages.

Citizens need both:

- trend data;
- localized evidence.

---

# 21. More police can help—but deployment matters more than totals alone

Police visibility can:

- deter opportunistic crime;
- reassure residents;
- improve response times;
- build local intelligence;
- identify repeat offenders.

But officers used primarily for:

- paperwork;
- prisoner transport;
- static duties;
- administrative processing;

may not create visible neighbourhood safety.

Effective deployment should combine:

- patrol data;
- victim reports;
- transport patterns;
- nightlife;
- repeat-offender locations;
- school routes;
- commercial complaints;
- community knowledge.

Predictive tools can assist resource allocation, but they risk reinforcing previous enforcement patterns. Areas with more policing generate more recorded incidents, which can then justify even more policing.

Human oversight and transparent evaluation remain essential.

---

# 22. Police legitimacy is a security asset

Citizens report crime and cooperate when they believe police will:

- treat them respectfully;
- take complaints seriously;
- avoid discrimination;
- protect confidentiality;
- respond proportionately.

Aggressive or arbitrary conduct can produce short-term control while weakening long-term information.

This is particularly important in:

- migrant communities;
- youth groups;
- areas with organized crime;
- victims of sexual violence;
- labour exploitation;
- undocumented populations.

A person afraid of immigration consequences or humiliation may not report the offender who is also harming others.

Trust is therefore not a soft alternative to enforcement.

It is part of enforcement capacity.

---

# 23. Justice speed matters as much as sentence severity

Citizens often interpret release pending trial, suspended sentences or slow proceedings as proof that crime has no consequences.

The legal system must protect:

- presumption of innocence;
- proportionality;
- defence rights;
- individualized sentencing.

But years of delay weaken deterrence and victim confidence.

A moderate consequence delivered quickly may deter more effectively than a severe sentence imposed years later.

Priority should be given to:

- violent repeat offenders;
- organized theft;
- sexual offences;
- cyberfraud networks;
- witness intimidation;
- weapon offences;
- trafficking and exploitation.

Speed should come from resources, specialization and case management—not removal of procedural rights.

---

# 24. Prison is necessary for dangerous offenders, but cannot be the only crime policy

At the end of 2024, prisons managed by the central administration held 48,947 people, excluding the separately administered prison systems in Catalonia and the Basque Country. 

Prison can:

- incapacitate dangerous offenders;
- punish serious harm;
- protect victims;
- express social condemnation.

It can also:

- disrupt family ties;
- worsen employability;
- reinforce criminal networks;
- fail to treat addiction or mental illness;
- produce reoffending after release.

The relevant question is not whether prison is good or bad.

It is which offenders require incapacitation, and what reduces their danger after release.

Serious violence requires firm consequences.

Lower-risk offenders may respond better to:

- supervised treatment;
- restitution;
- electronic monitoring;
- structured community sentences;
- intensive probation.

---

# 25. Security policy often begins after victimization rather than before it

Police and courts necessarily react to crimes already committed.

Prevention requires identifying earlier conditions:

- domestic violence escalation;
- school exclusion;
- weapons;
- addiction;
- untreated psychosis;
- trafficking recruitment;
- vulnerable elderly fraud targets;
- abandoned urban spaces;
- high-risk nightlife routes;
- repeated low-level offending.

Prevention is difficult politically because success is invisible.

No headline appears for an assault that did not occur.

Governments therefore receive more credit for dramatic arrests than for quiet systems that reduce opportunity.

---

# 26. Testing the main claims

## “Spain is becoming much more dangerous”

Conventional crime fell slightly in 2025 and remains near the lower end of the historical rate series.

**Verdict:** false as a general national claim; some violent and local categories have worsened. 

## “Crime is lower, so insecurity is invented”

Cybercrime, sexual violence and local disorder are real, while serious violence rose in several 2025 indicators.

**Verdict:** false. National stability does not invalidate victimization or local concentration. 

## “Most crime is violent crime”

Theft and other property offences dominate the volume of conventional crime.

**Verdict:** false. Violent crimes are more harmful per incident but far less numerous.

## “Cybercrime is not public insecurity”

Computer fraud represented 17.4% of all recorded criminality in 2025.

**Verdict:** outdated. Digital victimization is now central to public safety. 

## “Foreigners commit most crimes”

Most adults with final convictions had Spanish nationality.

**Verdict:** false in absolute numbers; foreign-national conviction rates were nevertheless higher. 

## “Nationality has nothing to do with crime statistics”

Official conviction rates differ by nationality.

**Verdict:** false descriptively; nationality alone does not explain the difference causally.

## “All occupied homes are family residences invaded while owners are away”

Public discussion mixes unlawful home entry, empty-property occupation and tenancy disputes.

**Verdict:** misleading legal and factual conflation.

## “Longer sentences solve insecurity”

Severity can matter, especially for dangerous offenders, but detection, speed and prevention are often more decisive.

**Verdict:** incomplete.

## “Social media proves crime is increasing”

Social media selects emotionally powerful incidents and repeats them without a denominator.

**Verdict:** it can reveal real events, but cannot measure prevalence.

# 27. What a serious public-safety strategy should prioritize

## A. Publish neighbourhood-level crime data responsibly

Data should show:

- offence category;
- rate per resident and visitor;
- time;
- location;
- trend;
- reporting limitations.

Privacy must be protected, particularly for sexual and domestic offences.

## B. Create repeat-offender case coordination

Police, prosecutors and courts should rapidly consolidate patterns involving prolific offenders while preserving independent judicial evaluation of each charge.

## C. Treat cyberfraud as infrastructure crime

Require faster cooperation among:

- banks;
- telecom operators;
- platforms;
- police;
- prosecutors;
- foreign authorities.

## D. Accelerate high-harm cases

Dedicated capacity should prioritize:

- serious violence;
- sexual offences;
- organized robbery;
- trafficking;
- cybercrime networks;
- weapons;
- witness intimidation.

## E. Improve victim support

Victims need:

- rapid information;
- translation;
- legal support;
- trauma care;
- financial-fraud assistance;
- protection from retaliation.

## F. Design safer public spaces

Use:

- lighting;
- late transport;
- active streets;
- maintenance;
- safe routes;
- transport staffing;
- rapid repair of disorder.

## G. Intervene early with violent adolescents

Combine:

- school intervention;
- family support;
- weapons enforcement;
- mentoring;
- mental healthcare;
- targeted policing;
- credible consequences.

## H. Publish adjusted crime analysis

Reports on nationality, age or sex should provide:

- population denominators;
- demographic adjustment;
- offence category;
- legal stage;
- limitations.

## I. Strengthen financial investigation

Confiscating the proceeds of organized crime often disrupts networks more effectively than repeatedly arresting low-level members.

## J. Measure outcomes, not police activity

Success is not the number of:

- stops;
- arrests;
- patrol hours;
- press releases.

It is:

- fewer victims;
- less repetition;
- faster resolution;
- greater reporting;
- higher public trust;
- safer use of public space.

---

# 28. What should not be done

## Do not use national averages to dismiss neighbourhood problems

Local concentration can be severe.

## Do not infer trends from viral incidents

Repeated visibility is not statistical frequency.

## Do not hide demographic data

Opacity strengthens conspiracy narratives.

## Do not convert demographic data into collective guilt

Group rates do not determine individual behaviour.

## Do not criminalize poverty or disorder indiscriminately

Social vulnerability and dangerous conduct require different responses.

## Do not promise total security

No democratic society can eliminate every crime without unacceptable surveillance and coercion.

---

# 29. What Spain should measure

A modern safety dashboard should include:

- conventional crime rate;
- cybercrime rate;
- homicide and attempted homicide;
- robbery and theft;
- residential burglary;
- sexual offences;
- domestic violence;
- repeat-offending rates;
- case-processing time;
- clearance rates;
- victimization surveys;
- underreporting;
- fear of crime;
- use of public space;
- online-fraud losses;
- assets recovered from organized crime;
- trust in police and courts.

Reported crime alone is insufficient.

If confidence in police improves, recorded crime may rise because more victims come forward.

That can represent institutional progress rather than social deterioration.

---

# Final verdict

Spain does not face a generalized collapse of public safety.

The strongest national evidence shows:

- conventional crime stable or slightly declining;
- historically low conventional-crime rates;
- fewer thefts and residential burglaries;
- no mass-homicide epidemic.

But reassurance becomes misleading when it ignores:

- rising cyberfraud;
- growth in some serious-violence indicators;
- persistent sexual offences;
- repeat offending;
- organized crime;
- severe local concentration;
- neighbourhood disorder;
- victims whose crimes are never reported.

Spain’s public-safety debate is trapped between two politically useful exaggerations.

One says:

> “The streets are out of control.”

The other says:

> “The statistics are good, so insecurity is manufactured.”

Both fail.

The accurate conclusion is:

> **Spain is broadly safe in conventional comparative terms, but its security institutions are adapting too slowly to digital fraud, localized violence, repeat offending and the changing ways citizens experience fear.**

A serious strategy would not try to frighten citizens or silence their concerns.

It would publish precise data, intervene where harm is concentrated, accelerate justice for repeat and dangerous offenders, protect victims and treat cybercrime as one of the principal forms of modern insecurity.
